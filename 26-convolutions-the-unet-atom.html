🔮 Predict first You switch a render from 512×512 to 1024×1024. Does the U-Net now have (a) ~4× more parameters, (b) the same parameters but ~4× more compute, or (c) both? Commit before you scroll — you have felt this one at the GPU.

The answer is (b), and it is the single most useful fact about a convolution. A conv's weights are a small stencil that does not know or care how big the image is; doubling each side leaves every parameter untouched. But it slides that stencil over 4× as many positions, so it does 4× the arithmetic. Parameters flat, compute quadratic. We will pin both numbers to the decimal below.

The stencil that slides

A convolution is one small pattern-matcher slid across the image. At every position it asks the same question — "how much does the patch under me look like my stencil?" — and writes the answer to that spot in the output. You learn the stencil; the sliding is free. That is the entire trick, and it buys one enormous thing: an edge detector learned in the top-left corner works, unchanged, in the bottom-right. The net never has to relearn the same feature at a new location, the way a fully-connected layer would (page 25: the fully-connected first layer spent 150 million weights and a conv spent 1,792 to install exactly that indifference-to-position).

The operation is technically cross-correlation — the stencil is not flipped the way a signals textbook's convolution is. It makes no difference here because the stencil is learned: the net simply learns whatever orientation it needs. Say it once for the DSP-literate and move on.

The math, with every shape

For one output channel $c_{\text{out}}$ at position $(i,j)$, with input $x$, kernel $W$, stride $s$, padding $p$:

$$y[c_{\text{out}},i,j] = b[c_{\text{out}}] + \sum_{c_{\text{in}}}\sum_{u=0}^{k_h-1}\sum_{v=0}^{k_w-1} W[c_{\text{out}},c_{\text{in}},u,v]\;x[c_{\text{in}},\,s\,i+u-p,\,s\,j+v-p]$$

Two count-formulas fall straight out and you should be able to write them without thinking:

$$P_{\text{conv}} = C_{\text{out}}\,(C_{\text{in}}\,k_h k_w + 1)\qquad\qquad \text{FLOPs} = 2\,H'W'\,C_{\text{out}}\,C_{\text{in}}\,k_h k_w$$

The parameter count has no $H$ and no $W$ in it. The FLOP count has $H'W'$ front and center. There is your PREDICT, in symbols.

🔑 The asymmetry A conv's parameters are independent of $H,W$; its compute is proportional to $H'W'$. Render at $1024^2$ instead of $512^2$ and the U-Net is not one weight bigger — but it is ~4× slower, because there are 4× as many output positions to visit. This is the "oh, that's why 1024 crawls" you have already lived through in ComfyUI, now derivable rather than felt.

A worked block from a real U-Net

✍️ Worked example A plausible SDXL-class interior conv: Conv2d(320, 320, kernel=3, padding=1) acting on a $64\times64$ feature map. (A 512-px image lands at $64\times64$ in latent space — the VAE downsamples by a factor of 8, $512/8 = 64$; you will meet that VAE on page 53. [VAE factor 8 — diffusion track fact])

Parameters: $P = 320\,(320\cdot 9 + 1) = 320 \times 2881 = \mathbf{921{,}920}$.
Output size: padding 1, stride 1, so $H' = W' = 64$ (unchanged — "same" convolution).
FLOPs: $2 \cdot 64 \cdot 64 \cdot 320 \cdot 320 \cdot 9 = \mathbf{7{,}549{,}747{,}200} \approx 7.55\ \text{GFLOP}$ — per conv, per denoising step.

A U-Net has on the order of 40 such convs, and you run ~30 steps: order $10^{13}$ FLOP before attention is even in the room. That is why sampling is slow, and every digit of it is arithmetic you can check against torch.FlopCounterMode on your box.

The receptive field: how far a neuron can see

Layer 1 sees a $3\times3$ patch. Nobody argues with that. The question that decides architecture is: after stacking many layers, how large a region of the original image can one deep neuron be influenced by? That region is the receptive field, and it grows by a recurrence:

$$r_0 = 1,\qquad r_\ell = r_{\ell-1} + (k_\ell - 1)\prod_{j<\ell} s_j$$

SymbolIsShape / unitsFrom
$r_\ell$receptive field (side length, in input pixels) after layer $\ell$scalar pxrecurrence
$r_0$a single input pixel sees itself1 pxbase case
$k_\ell$kernel size of layer $\ell$scalarthe layer
$s_j$stride of layer $j$scalarthe layer
$\prod_{j<\ell} s_j$cumulative stride (the "jump") — the input-pixel spacing one step feelsscalarproduct of earlier strides

The recurrence hides the whole story in that product term. With every layer stride 1, the jump stays 1 forever and reach grows linearly: $r_\ell = 2\ell + 1 = 3, 5, 7, 9, \dots$ To first cover a 224-px object you would need 112 layers ($2\cdot 112 + 1 = 225 \ge 224$). But the moment you put a stride-2 layer in, the jump doubles, and every later layer's contribution to reach doubles with it — the growth turns geometric. A U-Net encoder that downsamples every other layer walks this ladder:

plain 3×3, stride 1: r = 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, ... (linear, 2ℓ+1) → 112 layers to reach 224 px U-Net encoder (s=2 every other layer): r = 3, 5, 9, 13, 21, 29, 45, 61, 93, 125, 189, 253 → exceeds 224 px at layer 12

112 layers versus 12. That is the architectural reason a U-Net has a downsampling encoder — and it is not "efficiency" or "compression." It is reach. Downsampling is how the network gets to see far enough to relate the top of a face to the bottom of it. It is also exactly why SDXL bolts self-attention into its low-resolution middle blocks (attention gives global reach in one hop — page 31), and why FLUX abandoned the U-Net for a pure transformer (pages 31/35): once you can afford global mixing everywhere, you stop needing the downsampling ladder to buy reach.

Try it Build a stack and watch the receptive field grow. Flip one layer from s=1 to s=2 and the RF square jumps (13→29 in the encoder preset). Then load "Ten 1×1 convs": the RF stays pinned at 1 — a $1\times1$ conv mixes channels, never space (this is precisely what the proj_in/proj_out $1\times1$ convs around a U-Net's attention block do). The faint glow inside the square is the effective RF — real path-counting, not the theoretical box.
The Receptive-Field Grower Live
🔬 Deep dive: the effective receptive field is smaller than the theoretical one, and Gaussian

The recurrence gives the theoretical RF — the outermost input pixels that can reach a given output neuron. But not every input pixel inside that box matters equally. Count the paths from each input pixel to the output neuron: a center pixel feeds through many stacked stencils, an edge pixel through few. The path count is a repeated box-blur — a binomial — so it converges to a Gaussian falloff. The demo computes this honestly: it starts a single unit of "influence" at the traced output neuron and convolves backward through each layer (upsample by the stride, then a length-$k$ box), which is exactly the path count. The bright core is where influence concentrates; the theoretical box is mostly dim margin. [the Gaussian-falloff framing is a known empirical result — label it as such, not a theorem] The practical consequence: a net's usable reach is a fraction of its nominal one, which is a second, quieter reason downsampling matters — it grows both.

Equivariance, invariance, and why generation keeps where

Shift the input; what happens to the output? A convolution commutes with translation:

$$\text{Conv}(\text{Shift}_\delta\,x) = \text{Shift}_\delta(\text{Conv}\,x)\qquad\text{(equivariant)} \qquad\text{vs}\qquad \text{GlobalPool}(\text{Shift}_\delta\,x) = \text{GlobalPool}(x)\quad\text{(invariant)}$$

A classifier wants invariance — "cat" regardless of where the cat sits, so it ends in a global pool that deliberately forgets position. A generative U-Net wants the opposite. It outputs an image-shaped thing: shift the input noise and the predicted noise had better shift with it. Forgetting where is fatal. So diffusion U-Nets have no global pooling. They downsample with strided convs (learned, position-preserving) and upsample with transposed convs or — better — nearest-neighbour upsample followed by a conv, which avoids the checkerboard artifacts that plague transposed convolutions. Max-pooling is the old classifier way; it throws away where, and generation needs where.

One sentence of honesty: even a conv's equivariance is approximate. Zero-padding breaks it at the borders, and strided downsampling aliases. That is a real reason diffusion models sometimes misbehave at image edges — it is not a bug in your prompt.

Four traps

⚠️ "A kernel is 2-D." A single filter is 3-D — $(C_{\text{in}}, k_h, k_w)$ — because it reads every input channel at once; the full weight tensor is 4-D, $(C_{\text{out}}, C_{\text{in}}, k_h, k_w)$. This is why the worked block has 921,920 parameters and not $320\times 9 = 2{,}880$. Compute it, then match sum(p.numel()) and the illusion dies.
⚠️ "Deep nets see the whole image from layer 1." Layer 1 sees $3\times3$. Full-image reach is built, layer by layer, by the recurrence above. Run the grower with a plain stack and count how many layers it takes — it is a lot more than you would guess before you see the linear crawl.
⚠️ "Conv2d computes a convolution." It computes cross-correlation — no kernel flip. Mathematically it is a convolution with a flipped kernel, but since the kernel is learned, the flip is absorbed and never matters in practice. Harmless; worth knowing once so a signals background does not trip you.
⚠️ "Convolution is translation-invariant." It is equivariant, not invariant — shift in, shift out, not "same answer." And even that is broken at borders by padding and by aliasing in strided downsampling. The distinction is the whole reason a U-Net keeps spatial structure while a classifier discards it.
