Part IV — Adaptation

The Roofline: Your Box, Measured

A chip has two speed limits — how fast it computes, and how fast it can feed itself. One number decides which one binds. It is the last thing the trunk teaches before the LLM and diffusion tracks fork, because both tracks are the same division with opposite answers.

🔮 Predict, before you scroll Your Spark advertises ~1,000 TFLOP/s — that's FP4 with sparsity. A single-token LLM decode reads the whole model once and does about 2 FLOP per weight. Predict your decode speed for Qwen3-8B in bf16: closer to thousands of tok/s, hundreds, or tens? Commit — then measure.

Two speed limits, one division

Every accelerator can be starved two different ways. It can run out of arithmetic — the tensor cores are all busy and there is simply no more multiply-add per second to give. Or it can run out of bandwidth — the cores sit idle waiting for the next slab of data to arrive from DRAM. Which wall you hit is not a property of the chip alone; it depends entirely on how much arithmetic you do per byte you move. That ratio has a name.

(We are retiring the "Ferrari with a garden hose" line here on purpose — it is arithmetically unfair, because it divides a sparse-FP4 marketing peak by a real bandwidth. The honest version is damning enough without cheating.)

📐 Arithmetic intensity and the ridge $$I = \frac{\text{FLOPs}}{\text{bytes}}\ \ [\text{FLOP/byte}], \qquad I^\star = \frac{P_{\text{peak}}}{BW}, \qquad P(I) = \min\!\bigl(P_{\text{peak}},\ BW\cdot I\bigr).$$ The roofline is those two lines meeting at $I^\star$. Below $I^\star$ you are memory-bound (the chip starves, waiting on DRAM); above $I^\star$ you are compute-bound (the chip actually works).

The formula the whole fork hangs on

For a dense bf16 transformer, one forward pass reads every weight once ($2N$ bytes for $N$ parameters at 2 B each) and does about $2N$ FLOP per token processed. Put $S_{\text{fwd}}$ tokens through that one weight read and the arithmetic is $2N\,S_{\text{fwd}}$ while the bytes stay $2N$:

🔑 Arithmetic intensity equals tokens per forward pass $$I = \frac{2N\cdot S_{\text{fwd}}}{2N} = \boxed{\,S_{\text{fwd}}\ \ \text{FLOP/byte}\,}$$ One weight read, amortized over however many tokens you push through it. (For other weight precisions it generalizes to $I \approx S_{\text{fwd}}\times 2/b_W$.) This single frozen formula is used in both tracks — the LLM page and the diffusion page each plug in their own $S_{\text{fwd}}$ and get opposite verdicts. [THREAD:MEM] — the $2N$ bytes is the weight-read from the p.18 ledger; the $2N$ FLOP/token is from the compute page's $C\approx6ND$. [THREAD:DOT] — decode does exactly one multiply-add per weight, the dot product at hardware scale, so $I=1$.

The Spark's numbers — stated with their tags

Bandwidth is the easy one: $BW = \mathbf{273\ GB/s}$ VP (constants §6.1). The compute ceiling is where the honesty starts.

⚠️ The headline number is not a training ceiling The Spark's "1 PFLOP / 1000 TOPS" is FP4 with 2:4 structured sparsity VP — NVIDIA's own footnote says so. Your training weights are neither FP4 nor 2:4 sparse. Never size a training run off this number.
✍️ The dense-BF16 ceiling — inferred, not published NVIDIA publishes no dense-BF16 figure for GB10 VP: absence verified. So the course prints the inference chain and labels every rung INF (constants §6.3):
1000 TFLOP/s  FP4 sparse  (published, marketing)
÷2 (2:4 sparsity)  →  500  FP4 dense
÷2 (FP4 → FP8)  →  250
÷2 (FP8 → BF16)  →  125  BF16, FP16-accumulate
÷2 (FP32-accumulate penalty)  →  ~62  BF16, FP32-accum  ← what a PyTorch training matmul does
Two independent checks: a microbenchmark measured ~511 TF dense NVFP4 (102% of the implied 500) VP; and the per-SM rate closes — $48\ \text{SM}\times512\ \text{FLOP/SM/clk}\times2.6\ \text{GHz} = \mathbf{64\ TF}$ (FP32-accum), or $48\times1024\times2.6 = 128\ \text{TF}$ (FP16-accum) INF.
⚠️ The ridge is genuinely unresolved — and that is the point Because the ceiling is inferred, so is the ridge: $$I^\star = \frac{62\times10^{12}}{273\times10^{9}} = \mathbf{227}\ \text{FLOP/byte}\ \ (\text{if }\sim62\text{ TF}) \qquad\text{or}\qquad \frac{125\times10^{12}}{273\times10^{9}} = 458\ \ (\text{if }\sim125\text{ TF}).$$ We print 227 with the INF label, name 458 as the live alternative, and print neither as fact. An SDXL-BF16 benchmark implies 69.7 TF/s if CFG was on (the blog doesn't say), which would rule out 62 TF and lean toward 125; community GEMM reports lean 62 TF. The verdicts below are robust to both — only the ridge number turns on the ceiling, which is exactly why the fix is to measure it, not argue it. (Retired: the old 3,663 FLOP/byte ridge used the sparse-FP4 marketing peak.)

And here is the predict resolved. Qwen3-8B in bf16 decodes at a ceiling of 16.67 tok/s ($273\ \text{GB/s} \div 16.38\ \text{GB}$) with a realistic ~10.8 tok/s expectation once you apply the 0.65 memory-bandwidth-utilization coefficient DER (constants §6.7). Not thousands. Not hundreds. Tens. The prediction at the top of the page was answerable from one division — and quantizing to fp8 (~21.7) or NVFP4 (~43) moves it because it cuts the bytes, not because it adds compute. [THREAD:Qwen3-8B]

📐 The decode heuristic, and its one honest failure mode $$\text{decode tok/s} \approx 0.65 \times \frac{BW\ (\text{GB/s})}{\text{weight bytes read per token (GB)}} \qquad\text{[EST, dense batch-1]}$$ The 0.65 is the round, slightly-conservative choice (mean MBU 0.69, error bars ±10–15%). The punchline: if your heuristic predicts more than 100% of peak bandwidth, your model is an MoE. gpt-oss-20B decodes at 49.7 tok/s, which on its 12.8 GB checkpoint implies 637 GB/s = 234% of the 273 GB/s bus — impossible — because only ~3.6 B of its ~20.9 B params are active per token. The deeper lesson: for a quantized model, weight-bytes-per-token is the file minus the embedding table, not the file size and not params × bits.

Why you adapt instead of rebuild — priced in years

The same ceiling that makes decode slow makes pretraining a fantasy. Qwen3-8B took about $1.5\times10^{24}$ FLOP to train D is EST (constants §5; the token count $D$ is unverified). Divide by the theoretical FP16-accumulate peak and you get $1.5\times10^{24} / 1.25\times10^{14} = 381$ years. But that assumes the machine runs at its theoretical peak at 100% utilization, which it will not — at the ~62 TF FP32-accum roofline and ~40% MFU it is closer to 1,900 years.

🔑 The honest sentence "At least four centuries — and that assumes the machine runs at its theoretical peak, which it will not. Realistically, closer to two millennia." Assuming peak is generous to the machine, so 381 years is a lower bound, not a generous estimate — the conclusion is strengthened by honesty, not weakened. This is why Part IV existed: you take someone's four centuries of compute and spend a few hours redirecting it. [THREAD:MEM · final]
🔬 Deep dive: accumulate precision — the 62-vs-125 question, in detail

A GEMM multiplies bf16 inputs but has to sum the products somewhere. If it accumulates in fp16, the tensor core runs at full rate (~125 TF on GB10). If it accumulates in fp32 — which a plain PyTorch training matmul does, because fp16 accumulation loses too much precision over a long reduction — consumer-class Blackwell halves the rate, to ~62 TF. That single choice is the entire 62-vs-125 fork.

This also dissolves a community mystery. Reports of "~60 TF measured BF16" on GB10 get blamed on missing sm_121-tuned kernels. That is largely wrong: ~60 TF is ~96% of the 62.5 TF FP32-accumulate ceiling — the hardware is performing correctly. Quoting 125 TF as "the roof" for an FP32-accumulate GEMM would make a learner's honest measurement look like a 50% failure when it is at ~96% of achievable. measure_your_box.py runs a big BF16 GEMM in both accumulate modes; whichever your box lands at, understanding why teaches accumulate precision better than any paragraph — and it fixes your ridge.

Try it — the centerpiece Run ~/ComfyUI/.venv/bin/python measure_your_box.py (it installs nothing, writes nothing, read-only). First it tells you what your 128 GB box actually has. Then it measures the two numbers NVIDIA won't print — your dense bf16 TFLOP/s and your achieved bandwidth — and divides them to get your ridge. Compare it to the 227 and 458 on this page: which ceiling is your box on? Use --quick for a ~20 s run; --no-gpu for memory facts only (safe while ComfyUI is up). It contends with the GPU for a few seconds per size, so if ComfyUI is running the numbers come out pessimistic.
