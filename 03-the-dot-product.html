The Dot Product — the Atom

One operation — "how much of this is in that?" — that a neural network asks billions of times. We meet it geometrically and algebraically, watch its two forms land on the same number, and see the strange fact that in high dimensions almost everything is perpendicular to almost everything else.

Two forms, one number

The dot product of two vectors has an algebraic definition (multiply componentwise, add it up) and a geometric one (lengths times the cosine of the angle between them). They are the same number, always — and holding both in your head at once is the whole payoff of this page.

$$\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{d} a_i b_i \;=\; \lVert\mathbf a\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf b\rVert\,\cos\vartheta.$$

$\mathbf a,\ \mathbf b$the two vectors being compared$(d,1)$the inputs
$a_i,\ b_i$the $i$-th component of each (a scalar, not bold)scalarelements of $\mathbf a,\mathbf b$
$\lVert\mathbf a\rVert$length, $\sqrt{\textstyle\sum_i a_i^2}$scalarcomputed from $\mathbf a$
$\vartheta$the geometric angle between $\mathbf a$ and $\mathbf b$ — your trig, cashed inradians / degreesthe geometry
$d$the dimension (how many components)scalarthe space $\mathbb R^d$

Read it as a question. A big positive dot product means the two vectors point the same way; zero means perpendicular — unrelated; negative means opposed. Two derived quantities fall straight out:

$$\operatorname{proj}_{\mathbf b}(\mathbf a) = \frac{\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b}{\lVert\mathbf b\rVert^2}\,\mathbf b \quad(\text{the shadow } \mathbf a \text{ casts on } \mathbf b),\qquad \cos\vartheta = \frac{\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b}{\lVert\mathbf a\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf b\rVert}\in[-1,1] \quad(\text{cosine similarity}).$$

Cosine similarity strips the lengths away and keeps only the alignment; the raw dot product keeps both. Which you want depends on whether magnitude carries information — a distinction we cash in for real on the very next slide, and again when we compare attention (raw dot product) to RAG retrieval (cosine).

🔑 The one sentence A dot product asks: how much of this is in that? Every neural network is that question, asked billions of times, with the answers rearranged. If you leave this page with one thing, leave with that.

Translation (what papers call these). The angle here is $\vartheta$ (vartheta). Almost every paper writes it $\theta$ — but in this course $\theta$ means learnable parameters and nothing else, so the angle gets its own letter. When you read $\cos\theta$ in a paper's attention or RoPE section, mentally swap in $\vartheta$. Likewise the head dimension below, $d_{\text{head}}$, is written $d_k$ almost everywhere.

Why this one operation keeps coming back

The reason the dot product is the atom is that four things you will build are all the same operation wearing different clothes. Say them out loud now, so each later encounter is a reopening, not a surprise:

  • A neuron is a dot product. $z = \mathbf w\cdot\mathbf x + b$ — the weight vector $\mathbf w$ is a template, and $z$ is the match score: how much of the input looks like the template. That reframing is the whole of page 10.
  • An attention score is a dot product. $\text{score}_{ij} = \mathbf q_i\cdot\mathbf k_j / \sqrt{d_{\text{head}}}$ — a query asking each key "how much of you is in me?" This exact line returns on pages 29–30.
  • Embeddings are compared by dot product. "Is this document about my query?", "king − man + woman ≈ queen" — all dot-product geometry (page 27).
  • An output logit is a dot product. $h\cdot E_v$ — the hidden state against the vocabulary row for token $v$, which is exactly what page 9's softmax eats.

And the geometry itself returns twice more as trig — as rotations that compose (RoPE, page 33, trig payoff #2) and as the noise-schedule geometry of the diffusion forward process (page 54, trig payoff #3). You'll recognize the second one from ComfyUI.

Worked to a number

✍️ Two vectors, both ways

Take $\mathbf a = [3,\ 4]$ and $\mathbf b = [4,\ 3]$.

Algebraic$\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b = 3\cdot4 + 4\cdot3$$= \mathbf{24}$
Lengths$\lVert\mathbf a\rVert = \lVert\mathbf b\rVert = \sqrt{9+16} = \sqrt{25}$$= 5$
Cosine$\cos\vartheta = \dfrac{24}{5\cdot5} = \dfrac{24}{25}$$= \mathbf{0.96}$
Angle$\vartheta = \arccos(0.96)$$\approx \mathbf{16.26^\circ}$ — nearly aligned

Now swap $\mathbf b$ for $\mathbf c = [-4,\ 3]$:   $\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf c = 3(-4) + 4(3) = -12 + 12 = \mathbf{0} \Rightarrow \vartheta = \mathbf{90^\circ}$. The two are orthogonal — geometrically perpendicular, and, in the language a network speaks, unrelated. "Unrelated concepts are perpendicular" starts right here; it becomes the seed of how a 4096-wide model stores far more than 4096 ideas.

The $\sqrt{d_{\text{head}}}$ every transformer divides by

Here is a two-line derivation you can do yourself — and it explains the mysterious $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ in every attention diagram you've ever squinted at. It is nothing but a dot product plus one fact about variance.

Suppose the query and key entries are independent, mean-0, variance-1 numbers (roughly true after normalization). Then the score $\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf k = \sum_{i=1}^{d_{\text{head}}} q_i k_i$ is a sum of $d_{\text{head}}$ independent mean-0 terms, so:

$$\mathbb E[\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf k] = 0, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf k) = \sum_{i=1}^{d_{\text{head}}}\operatorname{Var}(q_i k_i) = d_{\text{head}}, \qquad \operatorname{std}(\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf k) = \sqrt{d_{\text{head}}}.$$

SymbolIsFrom
$\mathbf q,\ \mathbf k$a query and a key vectorprojections of a token
$q_i,\ k_i$their $i$-th components, mean-0 & variance-1assumed (normalized inputs)
$d_{\text{head}}$per-head dimensionarchitecture: 128 for Qwen3-8B
$\mathbb E,\ \operatorname{Var}$expectation, varianceprobability (page 8 formalizes)
✍️ The magic constant [THREAD:Qwen3-8B beat 2] Qwen3-8B has $d_{\text{head}} = 128$ (constants.md §1.1). So a raw attention score has a standard deviation of $\sqrt{128} = \mathbf{11.3137}$ (constants.md §5). Scores of $\pm11$ fed into a softmax come out almost one-hot — a hard argmax with almost no gradient. Dividing by $\sqrt{d_{\text{head}}}$ rescales the scores back to $O(1)$ so the softmax stays soft and gradients keep flowing. That is the whole reason the divide-by-$\sqrt{d_k}$ exists; page 30 puts it to work.

Two traps

⚠️ "A high dot product means similar magnitude." The raw dot product entangles magnitude and angle: $\lVert\mathbf a\rVert\lVert\mathbf b\rVert\cos\vartheta$. A long vector at a wide angle can out-score a short one that's perfectly aligned. If you only care about direction, use cosine similarity, which divides the lengths out. Attention deliberately keeps the raw dot product (magnitude reads as confidence); RAG retrieval usually uses cosine (it treats magnitude as noise). Same formula, opposite choice — because they want different things from the length.
⚠️ "2-D intuition survives into high dimensions." It does not. Two random unit vectors in $\mathbb R^{4096}$ have a cosine that is distributed like $\mathcal N(0,\ 1/4096)$ — standard deviation $\approx 0.0156$. They are essentially always orthogonal. Far from a bug, that is why a 4096-dimensional space can hold vastly more than 4096 near-orthogonal "concepts" — the phenomenon called superposition. 🔬 Deep dive: why the variances add, so $\operatorname{Var}(\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf k) = d_{\text{head}}$

The score is $\mathbf q\cdot\mathbf k = \sum_{i} q_i k_i$. Because the $d_{\text{head}}$ terms $q_i k_i$ are independent, the variance of their sum is the sum of their variances — that is the one property of variance that only holds under independence. Each term has $\operatorname{Var}(q_i k_i) = \mathbb E[q_i^2 k_i^2] - (\mathbb E[q_i k_i])^2 = \mathbb E[q_i^2]\,\mathbb E[k_i^2] - 0 = 1\cdot1 = 1$ (using independence again, and mean-0, variance-1 inputs). Summing $d_{\text{head}}$ ones gives $\operatorname{Var} = d_{\text{head}}$, hence $\operatorname{std} = \sqrt{d_{\text{head}}}$. The result is load-bearing and lives above the fold; this is just the machinery, which page 8 makes rigorous. The same "variances add" fact is exactly why the cosine of two random unit vectors has variance $1/d$ — try to see it.

Try it — your first measurement of superposition code/orthogonality.py — sample two random unit vectors in $\mathbb R^{4096}$ with torch, print their cosine (you'll see $\approx 0.01$–$0.02$); then loop 10,000 times and print the empirical standard deviation, which lands on $1/\sqrt{4096} = 0.0156$. Runs on CPU in under a second. You just measured why a 4096-wide model isn't limited to 4096 ideas.
