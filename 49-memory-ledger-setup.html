Five lines of a table decide whether a full fine-tune of Qwen3-8B lives on your Spark. It misses. By a hair. And you are going to watch it miss on hardware you own, rather than take my word for it.

🔮 Predict, before you compute anything Full fine-tuning Qwen3-8B needs 122.05 GiB of state — weights, gradients, an fp32 master copy, and Adam's two moment buffers. Your Spark's box says “128 GB.” Commit to an answer now: does it fit, with room to spare, or not? And — the part that decides it — how much of that 128 do you think you actually get?

The ledger recurs: 16 bytes per parameter

Trunk callback · the memory ledger

[THREAD:memory ledger — the reality check] You derived this table on p.18. Nothing here is new; we are only pointing it at Qwen3-8B. Standard bf16 mixed-precision training with AdamW keeps, for every trainable parameter, five copies:

ItemPrecisionB / paramWhy it has to exist
weightsbf162what the GPU computes the forward/backward with
gradientsbf162one per weight, filled by .backward()
fp32 master weightsfp324bf16 has 7 mantissa bits — see the box below
Adam $m$ (1st moment)fp324the running mean of the gradient
Adam $v$ (2nd moment)fp324the running mean of the gradient squared
total16memorize $16P$ — [DER, constants §2.1]
🔑 Both framings are true — state both, or you will hit a contradiction A learner who reads two blog posts finds one saying “optimizer state is $4\times$ the model” and another saying “$8\times$”. Both are right. $16$ B/param is $4\times$ the fp32 weight size (a fp32 weight is 4 B) and $8\times$ the bf16 weight size (a bf16 weight is 2 B). Same sixteen bytes, two denominators.
⚠️ “The fp32 master copy is wasteful — drop it” It is the line that makes training work. bf16 carries only 7 mantissa bits. A typical AdamW update to an $O(1)$ weight is around $2\times10^{-5}$; added to a bf16 value of order 1, it falls below the last representable bit and rounds to exactly zero. The weight never moves; training silently stalls. The fp32 master is a full-precision accumulator the tiny updates can land in. Drop it (the “8-bit Adam, no master” row below) and you court stalled updates.

The number

Qwen3-8B has exactly $P = 8{,}190{,}735{,}360$ parameters [VP, constants §1.2] — the count you reconciled to the byte back on p.36. Multiply:

$$P \times 16\ \text{B} \;=\; 8{,}190{,}735{,}360 \times 16 \;=\; 131{,}051{,}765{,}760\ \text{B}$$

Now the unit rule (constants §0) earns its keep. The same byte count reads two ways, and mixing them is the single most expensive error in this whole corpus:

PresentationValueWhen you use it
decimal (state quoted on its own)131.05 GB ($10^9$ B)matches every model card & the bytes×params arithmetic
binary (compared to hardware)122.05 GiB ($2^{30}$ B)the only form you may compare to the box's capacity

Per component, in decimal GB [DER, constants §2.2]: weights 16.38 · gradients 16.38 · master 32.76 · $m$ 32.76 · $v$ 32.76.

📐 This line is STATE ONLY 122.05 GiB is weights + grads + master + $m$ + $v$. Activations are a separate, separately-labelled line, always. At $B=1$, $S=2048$ with gradient checkpointing they are roughly 2–6 GB [EST, constants §2.2] — and the course measures them on your box, it does not assert them. Never fold activations into the 122.05; the two are different kinds of number.

Measure, do not assert

Here is where a course written for someone who owns the machine has to be honest. Three of the source drafts asserted three different usable-memory budgets — 128 GB, 119.2 GiB, “~110 GB usable.” You are about to be dared to reproduce an out-of-memory error on hardware you can inspect. So step 1 is not a claim. It is a measurement:

Python
import torch
free, total = torch.cuda.mem_get_info()          # bytes, on the unified pool
print(f"usable now: {free/2**30:.2f} GiB of {total/2**30:.2f} GiB")

state_gib = 8_190_735_360 * 16 / 2**30       # 122.05
print(f"full-FT state needs {state_gib:.2f} GiB")

On his box, this session, that first line printed $\text{total} = \mathbf{121.6875}$ GiB — not 128 [MEA-DEV, hardware-ground-truth §2]. The firmware/driver carveout eats 6.3125 GiB (4.9%) of the physical 128 GiB before you allocate a single byte. So the comparison that actually governs your run is:

$$\underbrace{122.05\ \text{GiB}}_{\text{full-FT state}} \;\;\text{vs}\;\; \underbrace{121.6875\ \text{GiB}}_{\text{measured } \texttt{MemTotal}} \;=\; \mathbf{-0.36\ \text{GiB}}$$

🔑 It does not fit — by 0.3%, before activations The predict resolves here: no, and by a razor. You do not get 128. You get 121.69, and the state overshoots it by 0.36 GiB — then activations (2–6 GB) push the real shortfall to 2.4–6.4 GiB. This is now measured fact, not a cliffhanger: full fine-tuning an 8B model does not fit on a Spark. The villain generalizes — published capacity is never usable capacity; the spec sheet is off by 4.9% before you start.

Distinguish three numbers you will otherwise conflate. The first is marketing; the second is the ceiling that actually exists; the third is what you can grab right now — and with his ComfyUI running (its normal state) the third is brutal:

The escape ladder

131 GB does not fit — so you climb down. Each rung trades a little training quality or a little precision for a lot of memory. Measured against his 121.69 GiB [MEA-DEV], recomputed live below:

Read the arc top to bottom: 131 GB doesn't fit → 8-bit Adam fits but eats most of the box on a mere 8B → LoRA drops it to 17 GB, QLoRA to 5 GB, and suddenly a 70B is on the table. You will earn LoRA on p.50, not be handed it.

⚠️ LoRA does not reduce activation memory When you switch to LoRA the bar's activation segment does not shrink — LoRA freezes the weights but you still backprop through the full network, so activations are unchanged at the same $B$ and $S$. Every LoRA-vs-Full comparison in this course is state-to-state, with activations named identically on both sides (constants §2.4). Set up the box — the setup page is not a formality

Here is the trap almost every tutorial walks you into. Your Spark has no peft, no trl, no accelerate, no bitsandbytes, and no system-wide PyTorch at all [MEA-DEV, hardware-ground-truth §3]. The only torch on the machine lives inside ComfyUI's venv (2.12.1+cu130), which is working and which you must not break. So the whole LLM stack goes into a fresh venv — mandatory, never ComfyUI's (you have 2.5 TB free):

bash
# a NEW venv — never touch ~/ComfyUI/.venv
uv venv --python 3.12 ~/llm-ft && source ~/llm-ft/bin/activate
uv pip install "transformers==5.14.1" "trl>=1.8,<2" "peft==0.19.1" \
               "bitsandbytes==0.49.2" datasets accelerate     # constants §7

export HF_HOME=/mnt/nvme/hf                # the cache hits 100s of GB — keep it on the NVMe
export PYTORCH_CUDA_ALLOC_CONF=expandable_segments:True
📐 The real aarch64 trap is narrower and sharper than folklore It is not “aarch64 is painful.” It is this: any package pinning libcudart.so.12 fails at import on the Spark's CUDA-13-only DGX OS — before kernel compatibility is even evaluated. Conversely, the good news the briefs got backwards: bitsandbytes ships a native sm_121 build for ARM64 + CUDA-13 (x86 does not get it) — so on this axis the Spark is better served than an x86 box, and your 70B QLoRA path (p.50) depends on it [VP, constants §6.9].
⚠️ The two-library dtype trap Two libraries, one script, opposite defaults. In transformers v5, from_pretrained now loads a model in its saved dtype (bf16 for Qwen3). But SFTTrainer, handed a model as a string with model_init_kwargs omitting dtype, still defaults to fp32 — silently doubling your weight memory and halving your speed. Always pass it explicitly [VP, constants §7.3]:
Python
cfg = SFTConfig(model_init_kwargs={"dtype": torch.bfloat16})  # or it's fp32
⚠️ SFTConfig's surprising defaults — know them before you run
  • gradient_checkpointing=True is already on — do not “discover” it as a memory win you added.
  • learning_rate=2e-5 is a full-FT default — for LoRA, raise it ~10× (p.50).
  • max_length=1024 silently truncates anything longer — the most common silent bug in the stack.
  • loss_type="chunked_nll", logging_steps=10, bf16=True.

Why chunked_nll exists: the logits tensor at $B=1$, $S=2048$ is $2048 \times 151936 \times 2 = \mathbf{622\ MB}$ for one tensor (constants §9.5) — fp32 cross-entropy doubles it and you need its gradient too. chunked_nll drops the -100 (masked) positions before the lm_head matmul.
