The Forward Process: Noising Is a Rotation

The whole forward chain is one rotation of a unit vector. The noise schedule only sets how fast you sweep the angle — and that single picture hands you $v$-prediction, the cosine schedule, and the terminal-SNR bug for free.

🔮 Predict first At $t = 500$ of $1000$ on the linear DDPM schedule, how much of the picture is left? Commit to a number for the signal fraction $\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{500}}$ before you scroll. If your instinct says "about half," hold that thought — then check it against the SNR table and the noise-ladder demo below.

The chain that learns nothing

Every image you have ever made in ComfyUI was trained against a process with zero learnable parameters. The forward process — the thing that turns a clean latent $\mathbf{x}_0$ into noise — is a fixed recipe. One Gaussian step:

$$q(\mathbf{x}_t \mid \mathbf{x}_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}\!\big(\mathbf{x}_t;\ \sqrt{1-\beta_t}\,\mathbf{x}_{t-1},\ \beta_t I\big)$$

⚠️ Misconception: "the forward process is part of the model, something the network learns" It is not. $q(\mathbf{x}_t\mid\mathbf{x}_{t-1})$ is a ruler, not a model — you could have written it in 1950. Its entire job is to manufacture, for free and in infinite supply, pairs of (noisy input $\mathbf{x}_t$, known answer $\boldsymbol\epsilon$). That is the strategic core of the whole field: diffusion turns unsupervised generative modelling into supervised regression. The network you do train only ever solves a least-squares problem — we build to exactly that on p.55.

Why the energy stays constant

Look at what one step does to the variance. Take the variance of both sides of $\mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{1-\beta_t}\,\mathbf{x}_{t-1} + \sqrt{\beta_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon_t$ (the sample form of that Gaussian), with $\boldsymbol\epsilon_t$ independent of $\mathbf{x}_{t-1}$:

$$\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{x}_t) = (1-\beta_t)\,\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}) + \beta_t.$$

If the input already has unit variance, $\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}) = 1$, then $\mathrm{Var}(\mathbf{x}_t) = (1-\beta_t) + \beta_t = 1$ — exactly. The total energy never moves. That is what "variance-preserving" (VP) means: signal is not turned down and noise turned up like a volume knob; signal is rotated into noise at constant total energy. It is a crossfader, not a fader. Hold that word "rotated" — it is about to become literal.

★ The rotation — the trig payoff

Applying that one step over and over collapses (next section) into a single jump whose two coefficients are $\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}$ on the signal and $\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}$ on the noise, where $\bar\alpha_t \equiv \prod_{s\le t}\alpha_s$ and $\alpha_t \equiv 1-\beta_t$. Square them and add:

$$\big(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\big)^2 + \big(\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\big)^2 = \bar\alpha_t + (1-\bar\alpha_t) = 1.$$

🔑 The forward process is a rotation The point $\big(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t},\ \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\big)$ traces the unit circle. Define the rotation angle $$\psi_t \equiv \arccos\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t},\qquad\text{so}\qquad \mathbf{x}_t = \cos\psi_t\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sin\psi_t\,\boldsymbol\epsilon.$$ At $t=0$: $\psi_0 = 0$, pure signal. At $t=T$: $\psi_T \to \tfrac{\pi}{2}$, pure noise. The whole forward process is sweeping one angle from $0$ to $90°$; the noise schedule is nothing but the speed of that sweep. Two consequences fall out for free: $v$-prediction (p.55) is just "the velocity around this circle," and the cosine schedule (below) is "sweep the angle at constant rate."

Translation. We write the rotation angle $\psi_t$ (notation §4.4, frozen). Papers write it $\phi_t$, and many skip it and speak only of $\alpha_t$ / $\bar\alpha_t$. Same angle, three names — arccos √ᾱ_t always recovers it.

Equation 3.3 — the one line the whole track leans on

The claim above was that 1000 tiny steps collapse into one jump. Here is why. Chain two steps and use the merge rule for independent Gaussians:

📐 Variances add — not standard deviations $$\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_a^2 I) + \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_b^2 I) = \mathcal{N}\!\big(0,\ (\sigma_a^2+\sigma_b^2)\,I\big).$$ When you sum two independent zero-mean Gaussians, their variances add, so the resulting standard deviation is $\sqrt{\sigma_a^2+\sigma_b^2}$ — "in quadrature," a straight Pythagoras. Numerically: $\sqrt{9}+\sqrt{16} = 3+4 = 7$, but the combined std is $\sqrt{9+16} = \sqrt{25} = \mathbf{5}$. Std does not add; variance does. This is the entire reason two noising steps merge into one.

Folding step $t$ into step $t{-}1$: the surviving signal multiplies to $\sqrt{\alpha_t}\sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}}$, and the two injected noises $\sqrt{\alpha_t}\sqrt{1-\alpha_{t-1}}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon_{t-1}$ and $\sqrt{1-\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon_t$ merge in quadrature. Induct all the way down to $\mathbf{x}_0$ and the whole telescope collapses:

🔑 Eq. 3.3 — the one-shot reparameterization $$\boxed{\ \mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon,\qquad \boldsymbol\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)\ }$$

Three things you just bought. (1) Training is $O(1)$, not $O(T)$: to get a training pair at level $t$ you do not simulate $t$ steps, you evaluate one line. (2) $t$ is a free, uniform input — sample it $t\sim\mathcal{U}\{1,\dots,T\}$ each minibatch. (3) One network covers all noise levels, because every level is reachable from $\mathbf{x}_0$ by the same formula.

So the predict answer is $\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{500}} \approx 0.280$ — barely a quarter of the signal survives to the midpoint, far below "half." Now read three facts straight off the table:

  1. The last row is not zero. $\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{1000}} = 0.0063 \ne 0$ [DER]. A trace of $\mathbf{x}_0$ leaks all the way to $t=T$, so at generation time the model is asked to start from "almost, but not quite, pure noise." The practical fallout: SD1.5 and SDXL cannot render a genuinely pure-black image — the residual signal pins the mean luminance near the training-set average. This is the "zero-terminal-SNR" bug (Lin et al., 2024); the noise_offset knob in your training configs is a hack that papers over it.
  2. The linear schedule wastes its ends. Between $t=0$ and $t\approx100$ almost nothing has happened ($\sqrt{\bar\alpha}$ still $0.95$), and past $t\approx800$ it is already all noise. Roughly a quarter of the steps do nothing interesting — which is precisely why the cosine schedule was invented.
  3. The interesting band is $t\in[300,700]$, where $\mathrm{SNR}\approx1$ and signal and noise are comparable. That is where composition is decided. A model that never trains enough on this band learns texture and palette but not structure.
⚠️ The two objects your tooling confuses: the $\beta$ schedule vs the scheduler dropdown These share a name and are completely different animals.
  • The $\beta$ schedule (linear, scaled-linear, cosine) is baked into the checkpoint at training time. It defines $\bar\alpha_t$, hence Eq. 3.3, hence what "$t$" even means for this model. Change it after the fact and the model is simply wrong — you are lying to it about how noisy its input is.
  • ComfyUI's scheduler dropdown (karras, normal, exponential, sgm_uniform, beta, simple) is an inference-time choice: it picks which timesteps, out of the $1000$, you actually stop at while sampling — the sampling grid. It never touches the trained $\bar\alpha_t$.
Baked-in physics vs. a sightseeing itinerary over that physics. You almost certainly have had these two conflated; almost everyone does.
🔬 Deep dive: the cosine schedule, and why 2048² sometimes grows a second head

The cosine schedule (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021) defines the cumulative product directly so the angle sweeps at a near-constant rate: $\bar\alpha_t = \cos^2\!\big(\tfrac{t/T + s}{1+s}\cdot\tfrac{\pi}{2}\big)\big/\cos^2\!\big(\tfrac{s}{1+s}\cdot\tfrac{\pi}{2}\big)$, with a small offset $s\approx0.008$. It spends far less budget at the dead ends and, conveniently, drives $\bar\alpha_T$ genuinely to $0$ — no terminal-SNR leak.

The resolution-dependence bug: Eq. 3.3 adds one independent noise sample per pixel. At high resolution there are vastly more pixels carrying the same low-frequency content, so per-pixel noise averages out over any region and the effective SNR at a fixed $t$ is higher than at low resolution. A schedule tuned at 512² therefore under-noises 2048² images — the model still "sees" the global composition at timesteps meant to have destroyed it, and can commit twice to the same structure (the infamous extra head / duplicated subject). The fix is to shift the schedule with resolution — exactly the shift parameter you will meet as production code in your own FLUX scheduler on p.57. Drag the resolution toggle in the noise-ladder demo to watch this happen live.
