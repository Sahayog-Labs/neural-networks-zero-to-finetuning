Artificial Neural Networks — From High-School Algebra to Fine-Tuning on Your Own Box

Sixty-five interactive pages from a single neuron to a Qwen3-8B LoRA and a FLUX.2 fine-tune — every number measured on the hardware you own.

A neural network is a machine that maps vectors to vectors and learns by gradient descent — nothing more exotic than that. Point the same machine at "predict the next token" and it is a large language model. Point it at "predict the noise that was added to this image" and it is a diffusion model. The architecture diverges by page 44; the underlying object never does. That is the thesis this course spends 65 pages proving to you, not asserting.

The destination is concrete: you train toward fine-tuning LLMs and diffusion models on your own NVIDIA DGX Spark — the box already running your ComfyUI graphs. Every memory ledger, every roofline, every "does this fit" question in this course is answered against that machine, not a rented A100.

The structure follows the machine, not a syllabus. A shared trunk (Parts I–IV: foundations → how training works → architectures → adaptation) is where every learner starts and every idea gets its one honest treatment. The trunk then forks into two tracks — LLM fine-tuning first, then diffusion (deliberately in that order: formalizing the LLM track first gives your already-strong diffusion intuition, built from thousands of ComfyUI renders, a fresh and rigorous frame instead of re-deriving what you already feel). A shared capstone (Part VII) then rejoins the two tracks — literally: your FLUX.2 model's prompt reader turns out to be a 40-layer transformer language model, the exact object the LLM track spent nine pages teaching you to read.

One paragraph on stakes, because it explains every number this course prints: every hardware figure here is measured on your box, not copied off a spec sheet. The clearest example is the one that recurs most — the DGX Spark is marketed at 128 GB, but the usable MemTotal the driver actually hands you is 121.6875 GiB [MEA-DEV] — a 6.3125 GiB (4.9%) firmware carveout gone before a single byte is allocated. Published capacity is never usable capacity. Where a number genuinely can't be measured yet — the DGX Spark's dense BF16 compute ceiling is a live, unresolved example — the course ships you the measuring script instead of a guessed fact. That is the whole method, not a caveat on it.

🔑 How to use this course The pages are meant to be read in order — each one leans on the last. Every content page runs the same rhythm: predict → intuition → math → worked number → demo → quiz. You commit to a guess before you're shown the answer, then the page derives it, works a real number through it, and hands you a live demo that computes the same math in your browser — drag every slider, nothing on this course is precomputed. Deep-dive derivations are optional, closed-by-default collapsibles; skipping one never costs you a later page. A handful of pages ship a Python script meant to run on your Spark — measure it, don't take the course's word for it. Watch for four recurring objects as you go: the dot product (the atom every layer is built from), TN-1 (a nine-parameter network you hand-compute on page 6 and meet again, scaled up, on the last three pages), the chain rule (the one verb that runs under every training step), and the memory ledger (the reality check — does it actually fit on your box).
📐 What you'll be able to do Derive backprop by hand on a nine-parameter network, then watch autograd agree with you to the last float. Read a model's parameter count off its config.json and match it to a checkpoint to the byte (8,190,735,360 for Qwen3-8B). Build the memory ledger yourself and predict what fits on your box before you run anything. Train a real Qwen3-8B LoRA (17.08 GB, ~12 min) and a diffusion LoRA on your own hardware. Explain attention, GQA, RoPE, and cross-attention well enough to read any model's config.json cold. Predict your hardware's throughput from a roofline model and be right when you measure it. And recognize your FLUX.2 model's text encoder for what it actually is: a Mistral.
