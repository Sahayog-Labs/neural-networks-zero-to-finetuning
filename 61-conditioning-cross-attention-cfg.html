Conditioning changes the loss by exactly one symbol

Everything you built on pages 54–57 — the forward rotation, the MSE objective, the samplers, flow matching — used an unconditional network $\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf x_t, t)$ that only ever saw a noisy latent and a noise level. Text-to-image adds one argument to that function and changes nothing else:

🔑 Conditioning is one extra input to the same network $$\mathcal L \;=\; \mathbb E_{\mathbf x_0,\,\boldsymbol\epsilon,\,t,\,\mathbf c} \Big[\,\big\|\,\boldsymbol\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(\mathbf x_t,\,t,\,\mathbf c)\,\big\|_2^2\,\Big].$$ Train on (image, caption) pairs instead of images alone; the forward process, the loss shape, and the samplers are untouched. That single degree of freedom is why diffusion took over: $\mathbf c$ can be a caption, a depth map, a pose, another image — you condition by concatenating, and the machinery does not care what it is.

Classifier-free guidance, derived in five lines

You have a conditional score $\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf x_t,t,\mathbf c)$ and — because you randomly dropped the caption on ~10% of training steps — an unconditional one $\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf x_t,t,\varnothing)$ from the same network. Watch what falls out of Bayes' rule.

🔑 Five lines from Bayes to the guidance formula

1. Bayes on the conditional density: $\;p(\mathbf x\mid\mathbf c)\;\propto\;p(\mathbf c\mid\mathbf x)\,p(\mathbf x).$

2. Take $\log$, then $\nabla_{\mathbf x}$. The evidence $p(\mathbf c)$ has no $\mathbf x$ in it, so it dies — the same normalizer-dodge that page 53 promised and page 56 delivered: $$\nabla_{\mathbf x}\log p(\mathbf c\mid\mathbf x) = \nabla_{\mathbf x}\log p(\mathbf x\mid\mathbf c) - \nabla_{\mathbf x}\log p(\mathbf x).$$

3. Read it aloud: (conditional score) − (unconditional score) is a classifier gradient — and we never trained a classifier. That is the classifier-free in the name.

4. To make the prompt count more, amplify that classifier term by a factor $w_g$: sample from $\tilde p_{w_g} \propto p(\mathbf x)\,p(\mathbf c\mid\mathbf x)^{w_g}$. Its score is $\;\nabla\log p(\mathbf x) + w_g\big[\nabla\log p(\mathbf x\mid\mathbf c) - \nabla\log p(\mathbf x)\big].$

5. Convert scores to $\epsilon$ with page 56's identity $\mathbf s_\theta = -\epsilon_\theta/\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}$. The minus signs and the $\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}$ are common to every term and cancel:

$$\boxed{\;\tilde\epsilon_\theta \;=\; \epsilon_\theta(\mathbf x_t,t,\varnothing) \;+\; w_g\big[\,\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf x_t,t,\mathbf c) - \epsilon_\theta(\mathbf x_t,t,\varnothing)\,\big]\;}$$

That is cfg in the KSampler node, derived from Bayes and one substitution. Page 56 was worth it.

Read it as a straight line

Rewrite the boxed formula around the conditional point: $\;\tilde\epsilon = \epsilon_\varnothing + w_g(\epsilon_c - \epsilon_\varnothing)\;$ is exactly lerp(uncond, cond, w_g) — a line you have written in shader code. The only new idea is that you are allowed to set $w_g > 1$ and walk off the end of the segment.

🔑 CFG generates images from a distribution that does not exist For $w_g>1$, $\tilde\epsilon$ points somewhere no honest denoiser would ever have pointed. $\tilde p_{w_g}\propto p(\mathbf x)\,p(\mathbf c\mid\mathbf x)^{w_g}$ is a sharpened, extrapolated distribution — like lowering a softmax temperature past 1, but on the image itself. (On the frozen canonical logits $z=[2.0,1.0,0.1]$, $\hat y=[0.659,0.242,0.099]$ DER · constants §9.2; raising those probabilities to a power $w_g$ and renormalizing pushes mass onto the top class — the same move, on classes instead of pixels.) It is one of the most embarrassing facts in generative modeling: the setting everyone uses provably leaves the data manifold, and it works. Predict-box answer #1: (c).

Why too much guidance looks "fried" — three real mechanisms

✍️ The three mechanisms, in order of blame

1. Variance / contrast blow-up (dominant, and it's arithmetic). The prediction $\epsilon_\theta$ is supposed to be unit-variance noise. But $\|\tilde\epsilon\| = \|\epsilon_\varnothing + w_g(\epsilon_c-\epsilon_\varnothing)\|$ grows roughly linearly in $w_g$ — so at $w_g=7.5$ the "noise" you subtract is several times too big. Over-subtracting inflates $\hat{\mathbf x}_0$'s contrast: blown highlights, crushed blacks, oversaturated color. The one-line fix confirms the diagnosis: the RescaleCFG node renormalizes $\operatorname{std}(\tilde\epsilon)$ back to $\operatorname{std}(\epsilon_c)$ — and the frying goes away. A std fix repairs the artifact, so the artifact was a std problem. (You may have used that node without knowing this was what it did.)

2. Off-manifold extrapolation. A straight line in $\epsilon$-space leaves the curved data manifold. Even with the std corrected, far-out $\tilde\epsilon$ lands in latent regions the VAE decodes to plausible-but-wrong texture.

3. Diversity collapse. $p(\mathbf c\mid\mathbf x)^{w_g}$ is mode-seeking: it concentrates on the single most-prompt-like sample. High $w_g$ trades variety for prompt-adherence — the "every face looks the same" complaint.

Try it Push $w_g$ high, then drag $\phi$ from 0 to 1 and watch the standard deviation of the guided prediction snap back to the conditional's. The raw curve rises without bound; the rescaled curve is flat. The frying was a scale bug; this is the one-line fix.

D61.2 · The guidance line — $\tilde\epsilon$ leaves the segment Live

Two fixed points, $\epsilon_\varnothing$ and $\epsilon_c$, and the guided point $\tilde\epsilon = \operatorname{lerp}(\epsilon_\varnothing,\epsilon_c,w_g)$. Past $w_g=1$ the dot visibly leaves the segment between them — geometry and frying, same picture.

The one that trips everyone: guidance is not cfg

⚠️ FLUX.1-dev's guidance ≠ real CFG — highest-priority confusion The FluxGuidance node's guidance (default 3.5) is not classifier-free guidance. FLUX.1-dev is guidance-distilled (page 59): the CFG behavior was baked into the weights, and guidance is just the scale-value the model was trained to emulate, fed in as a conditioning input, embedded like the timestep $t$. One forward pass. No unconditional branch. The extrapolation is already compiled in.

What this means you can act on:

  • Setting real cfg=3.5 on FLUX.1-dev doubles your time and makes it worse — you are double-guiding an already-guided model.
  • guidance is cheap: it is an embedding, not a second pass.
  • FLUX.1-schnell ignores it entirely.
  • FLUX LoRAs must be trained with guidance_scale=1.0 — the gotcha that reappears on page 62.
✍️ The cost the UI hides — predict-box answer #2 Real CFG needs both $\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf c)$ and $\epsilon_\theta(\varnothing)$, so it is 2 forward passes per sampling step. A run showing "20 steps" at cfg 7.5 actually evaluates the network $$20 \text{ steps} \times 2 \text{ passes} = \mathbf{40}\ \text{model evaluations.}$$ The steps number in the UI is half the truth. A negative prompt costs nothing extra — it just replaces the $\varnothing$ branch with an encoded $\mathbf c^-$ (the unconditional pass was already being computed). On FLUX.1-dev, which runs one pass, a negative prompt does nothing — there is no second branch to repurpose.
⚠️ Genuine, unresolved uncertainty Nobody has a satisfying theory of why $w_g\approx7$ beats $w_g=1$. Karras's "autoguidance" (guiding with a deliberately worse model works better than guiding with the unconditional one) suggests CFG is correcting model error, not sharpening the true conditional. You are turning a knob the field's best theorists do not fully understand, in a direction that provably leaves the data distribution, and it works. That is the honest state of the art — hold it lightly.
