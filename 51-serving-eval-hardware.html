Serving It, Judging It, and What the Spark Can't Do

Your fine-tune from p.50 trained in eleven minutes. Getting it in front of a user, and knowing whether it's actually better, are two separate problems — and the Spark answers the two halves of this track very differently than it answered fine-tuning.

LLM Fine-Tuning Track
🔮 Predict first Your fine-tune's held-out loss dropped beautifully. Is the model better? And which is the Spark good at — fine-tuning your 8B, or serving it to users at chat speed?

Serving: merge → convert → serve

The adapter from p.50 is 87.3 MB of $\Delta W$ sitting beside a frozen 16.38 GB base. To ship it you fold the two together with merge_and_unload() — and there is exactly one way to get this wrong that silently degrades every output.

⚠️ Merge into the bf16 base — never the NF4 QLoRA base If you trained with QLoRA, the base you loaded during training was already lossy 4-bit NF4. Calling merge_and_unload() folds a full-precision adapter update into that already-quantized representation, compounding the loss a second time [constants §9.7]. The fix is to reload the original bf16 checkpoint, attach the trained adapter to that, and merge there. code/14_merge_and_serve.py ships an assert on the base dtype so this can't happen by accident.
merge → convert → serve
# 1. merge — into the ORIGINAL bf16 base, asserted, not the NF4 one
python merge_and_unload.py --adapter out/qwen3-8b-lora --base Qwen/Qwen3-8B --dtype bf16

# 2. convert to GGUF, quantize for llama.cpp/Ollama (the default local path)
python convert-hf-to-gguf.py merged/ --outtype f16
./llama-quantize merged.gguf merged-Q4_K_M.gguf Q4_K_M
ollama create qwen3-8b-mine -f Modelfile && ollama run qwen3-8b-mine

# 3. measure — check the served tok/s against p.46's roofline, not a guess
python 02_kv_cache_and_roofline.py --model qwen3-8b-mine --measure-decode
📐 The serving recommendation (D-10) llama.cpp/Ollama is the default local path — it works today, it's the GGUF endpoint of the pipeline above, and it's the officially-documented Arm DGX Spark learning path. vLLM is the throughput option, via the vllm/vllm-openai:cu130-nightly container pinned by digest — the PyPI aarch64 wheel is built against CUDA 12 and fails to import on the Spark's CUDA-13-only stack (constants §6.9). Train in NF4/bf16; serve in GGUF/AWQ/NVFP4 — different numeric formats for different jobs, and neither one is "the" quantized format.

Evaluation: loss ≠ quality

🔑 The whole section, in one line Loss going down means the model is getting better at predicting your dataset. Your dataset is not your goal.

A held-out loss curve that falls beautifully tells you the model isn't memorizing garbage. It tells you nothing about whether the outputs are actually useful. Climb this ladder, cheapest rung first, and stop as soon as a rung actually answers your question:

  1. Held-out loss. free — answers only "am I overfitting?"
  2. mean_token_accuracy. free — marginally more legible than raw nats
  3. A programmatic task metric — a real checker: does the SQL run, does the JSON validate, does the unit test pass. if your task has one, everything below this line is optional. Push hard for one.
  4. 50 eyeballed examples. ~15 minutes — catches the regressions a metric misses
  5. LLM-as-judge. cheap at scale, biased — see below
  6. Public benchmarks. contaminated by training-set overlap; weak signal for a private fine-tune
  7. Human preference. expensive, the actual ground truth when you can afford it

The catastrophic-forgetting probe runs in parallel with every rung above and costs almost nothing: keep 10–20 general prompts unrelated to your fine-tuning task, run them before and after training, and diff the answers by eye. Fifteen minutes catches the single most common way a fine-tune quietly destroys value while the loss curve looks perfect — the model gets great at your task and noticeably worse at everything else.

LLM-as-judge: the biases, honestly

Once a task metric doesn't exist, an LLM judge is the next cheapest signal — and it comes with five well-documented failure modes. Two are cheap to fix; the rest you manage, not eliminate.

BiasWhat happensFix
Positionthe answer shown first wins more often, independent of quality fixed — average both orderings. Cheap. Do it always.
Verbositya longer answer scores higher with no new content only partially fixable — report answer lengths alongside every score
Self-preferencea judge favors text that sounds like its own model family use a judge from a different model family than the one you're evaluating
Formatmarkdown/bullets read as "more complete" regardless of substance name it; strip formatting before judging if you can
Calibration driftabsolute scores drift across prompts and sessions anchor to a small human-labelled golden set
⚠️ The closed-loop trap If you generated your synthetic training data with model X and then judge your fine-tune with model X, you are not measuring quality — you are measuring how much your model's outputs sound like X's. The score will show improvement. It will mean nothing. code/12_evaluate.py warns explicitly when the judge family matches the data-generator family.

The opposite-verdicts hand-off

Reopen the trunk's roofline (p.43) and its LLM instantiation (p.46) rather than re-deriving them: one formula, arithmetic intensity equals tokens processed per forward pass at bf16,

$$I = \frac{\text{FLOPs}}{\text{bytes}} = S_{\text{fwd}}\ \text{FLOP/byte}$$

against a ridge $I^\star$ that is 227 FLOP/byte (working value, [INF]) or 458 if the true dense-BF16 ceiling turns out to be nearer 125 TF than 62 TF — genuinely unresolved, constants §6.4, and the course prints both rather than picking one.

🔑 Same machine, same ridge, opposite verdicts LLM decode at $S_{\text{fwd}}=1$ sits hundreds-fold below the ridge; diffusion sampling at $S_{\text{fwd}}=4096$ sits comfortably above it on either candidate ridge. It isn't that the Spark is "weak" or "strong" in the abstract — it's a bandwidth-limited machine, and the two tracks sit on opposite sides of the exact same $I^\star$. The diffusion track picks this thread up on its own hardware page and lands the other half of the contrast; you already have the formula and both data points.

Fine-tuning and serving, then, are not the same verdict wearing two names. LoRA/QLoRA training is a single forward-and-backward pass per token over your whole batch — arithmetic-intensity-rich, and the Spark eats it (6,969.59 tok/s, 11.5 minutes for a real project). Chat serving is one token at a time, arithmetic-intensity $I=1$, and the Spark starves.
