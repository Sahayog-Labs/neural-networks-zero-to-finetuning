'Fine-Tuning' Names Three Different Things

Three operations wearing one word

"Fine-tuning" is three different operations wearing one word. A ComfyUI user means "LoRA or DreamBooth on a subject." An LLM engineer means "SFT plus preference alignment." A researcher means "gradient descent on any subset of the weights." All three are right, and all three are incomplete, because the real question isn't which recipe — it's a type signature. Sort every adaptation technique by that signature and your whole messy node graph becomes legible.

📐 The taxonomy — three axes
AxisAnswers
WHERE does the change go? all weights (full FT) · a low-rank correction (LoRA) · the vocabulary (textual inversion, ~3 KB) · nowhere — you change the input (ControlNet, IP-Adapter, RAG, a better prompt)
WHAT are you teaching? form (style, format, idiom, behaviour) vs. facts (→ you can't fine-tune facts in; go to RAG — the full decision lives on p.47)
WHICH recipe? SFT · DreamBooth (rare token + prior preservation) · DPO/GRPO · continued pretraining
🔑 The collapsing test

After you're done, is the base model different?

LoRA / DreamBooth / full FT / SFT / DPO → Yes. $\theta$ changed.
ControlNet / IP-Adapter / RAG / a better prompt → No. The base model is bit-identical; you bolted an extra input onto it.

One question collapses the whole confusion. Every technique on this page answers it the same way every time — that's what makes it a taxonomy and not a vibe.

✍️ The corollary that makes it stick You train a LoRA on your own images in ~20 minutes with 15–20 photos. You download a ControlNet — it took someone 100,000+ images and a datacenter, but it then works on any image, forever, because it isn't about content, it's about a kind of control. That is why there are roughly 500,000 LoRAs on Civitai and about 15 useful ControlNets [EST — community folklore, order-of-magnitude, not a verified constant]. The ratio is the distinction, made quantitative: one $\theta$-edit is cheap and narrow; one $\mathbf{c}$-edit is expensive and universal.

Resolving the PREDICT above: it's (b). A LoRA edits $\theta$; the ControlNet, the IP-Adapter, and the prompt all edit $\mathbf{c}$ (the conditioning). Nothing is being sequenced or averaged — they simply aren't the same kind of object, so there's nothing for them to collide over.

🔑 The punchline for your graph You can stack all of them at once — a LoRA edits $\theta$; the ControlNet, the IP-Adapter, and the prompt all edit $\mathbf{c}$. They don't collide because they aren't the same kind of object. Your ComfyUI graph has a clean type signature: at most one $\theta$-edit (maybe two, if you chain LoRAs), and a stack of $\mathbf{c}$-edits underneath. Once you see it, the distinction is permanent.
⚠️ Misconception: "DreamBooth vs. LoRA — which should I use?" A category error half the internet makes. DreamBooth is what you train — a recipe: a rare token plus prior-preservation loss so the model doesn't forget "person" while it learns your person. LoRA is how you store the change — a parameterization: instead of writing a new $d_{out}\times d_{in}$ weight matrix, you write a rank-$r$ correction $BA$. They answer different axes (WHICH vs. WHERE) and modern practice is both, simultaneously — look at the filename in any training repo: train_dreambooth_lora_flux.py.
⚠️ Misconception: "ControlNet / IP-Adapter is fine-tuning" No. Both change $\mathbf{c}$ — the conditioning fed to a sampling process — not $\theta$. The base model that produced the ControlNet or IP-Adapter's own weights is unrelated to and untouched by whatever checkpoint you load them onto. Run the collapsing test: after ControlNet runs, is your base model different? No — same weights, on disk, before and after.

The three LRs, one principle

These edits touch wildly different parameter counts, so their learning rates differ by orders of magnitude — generated by one rule carried from p.40: fewer trainable parameters ⇒ higher LR. A method that moves 8.19 billion numbers that already encode everything the model knows must tiptoe; a method that moves 768 numbers can stride.

MethodLRTrainable params
LLM full FT (8B)1e-5 – 2e-58.19e9

⚠️ The 100× trap A diffusion LoRA trained at $\eta = 1\text{e-}6$ (the full-fine-tune / DreamBooth rate) will barely move at all — that LR is 100× too small for a rank-16 adapter with only 3e7 trainable parameters. Diffusion LoRA wants $\eta \approx 1\text{e-}4$. Conflating the full-FT row with the LoRA row is the single most common reason a diffusion LoRA "won't learn."
🔬 Deep dive: textual inversion — teaching a word, not a weight

Textual inversion doesn't touch the network's weights at all. It adds one new embedding vector — 768 numbers, the width of the text encoder's embedding space — and optimizes that against the reconstruction loss while everything else stays frozen. $\theta$ (the network's parameters) is untouched; the vocabulary grew by one token. That's why it sits on the WHERE axis next to "the vocabulary," not next to "all weights" or "a low-rank correction" — and why it can tolerate the highest LR in the table (5e-4 – 5e-3): 768 free numbers with no risk of catastrophically disturbing a network that already works.

Read-only, installs nothing, ~2 s on your box. --workflow-dir <dir> --all reports on every saved workflow in a folder — point it at your own 12 GB of LoRA workflows (hardware-ground-truth.md §5) and see how many of your graphs have more than one $\theta$-edit stacked.
Python
# inspect_my_comfy_graph.py — the classification core.
# THETA_EDIT_TYPES / C_EDIT_TYPES / INFRA_TYPES are the type-signature table,
# as Python sets of ComfyUI node class_type strings.

def classify(nodes):
    """Tag every node theta-edit / c-edit / infra / unknown."""
    tagged = []
    for n in nodes:
        ct = n["class_type"]
        if ct in THETA_EDIT_TYPES:
            bucket = "THETA-EDIT"  # the base model is different after this node
        elif ct in C_EDIT_TYPES:
            bucket = "C-EDIT"      # the base model is untouched; the INPUT changed
        elif ct in INFRA_TYPES:
            bucket = "infra"        # load/sample/decode/save — neither
        else:
            bucket = "unknown"
        tagged.append({**n, "bucket": bucket})
    return tagged

Self-check the script asserts on its bundled example: LoraLoader → THETA-EDIT; ControlNetApply, IPAdapterApply, CLIPTextEncode (×2, positive/negative) → C-EDIT — reproducing "1 place / 4 places" exactly (spec-code.md D.2).
