Getting Text Back Out: Decoding, the KV Cache & the Roofline

Page 45 taught the model to score the next token. Now we make it actually emit one — then another, and another — and discover that generation is the single most bandwidth-starved thing the Spark ever does, and exactly why.

LLM Fine-Tuning Track

Decoding sits between "the model has logits" and "you have text." Three ideas live here: how a logit vector becomes a sampled token, why holding a conversation needs a growing cache of keys and values, and how one division predicts your box's tokens-per-second before you ever run it.

🔮 Predict first The same Qwen3-8B, on the same Spark. Prefill (reading your prompt) runs at ~8,000 tok/s. Decode (emitting one token at a time) runs at ~20 tok/s. That's a ~390× gap on identical hardware. Before you scroll: guess why — and guess how many tokens/second bf16 Qwen3-8B decode will hit on your box (10? 100? 1000?).

1 · From a logit vector to a token

At every position the model hands you a vector of $V = 151{,}936$ real numbers — the logits $\mathbf{z}$, one per vocabulary entry [VP, constants §1.1]. A decoding strategy turns that vector into exactly one chosen token ID. The base move is a temperature-scaled softmax:

$$p_v \;=\; \frac{\exp(z_v / T_{\text{samp}})}{\sum_{v'=1}^{V}\exp(z_{v'} / T_{\text{samp}})}$$

$z_v$the logit (raw score) for vocab entry $v$scalar, realthe model's final lm_head
$p_v$probability assigned to token $v$ after scaling$(0,1)$, rows sum to 1computed here
$T_{\text{samp}}$sampling temperaturescalar $>0$your decoding knob
$v, v'$vocabulary indices$1\dots V$notation §3

The limits are worth memorizing: $T_{\text{samp}}\!\to\!0^{+}$ collapses the softmax onto its largest logit — pure argmax, i.e. greedy decoding; $T_{\text{samp}}=1$ leaves the model's own calibrated distribution untouched; $T_{\text{samp}}\!\to\!\infty$ flattens everything toward a uniform draw over the whole vocabulary.

Translation note. This course writes $T_{\text{samp}}$ for sampling temperature. Papers and APIs write temperature or a bare $T$ — but $T$ in this course is the diffusion chain length (the number of noising steps), so we subscript it here to keep the two apart (notation §3.2, §4.5). Whenever you set temperature=0.7 in an API call, that is $T_{\text{samp}} = 0.7$.

On top of temperature, three truncation rules decide which tokens are even eligible before the draw. All three run on the probabilities $p_v$:

📐 The three truncations
  • Top-k: keep the $k$ largest-probability tokens, set the rest to $-\infty$, renormalize. A fixed count, regardless of how peaked the distribution is.
  • Top-p (nucleus): keep the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability first reaches $p_{\text{thresh}}$ (e.g. 0.9), renormalize. A fixed mass — the count adapts to the shape.
  • Min-p: keep every token with $p_v \ge p_{\min}\cdot\max_{v'}p_{v'}$ — a floor relative to the top token. Peaked distributions keep few; flat ones keep many.
⚠️ "Temperature adds randomness" It does not add anything. Temperature is a monotone rescaling of the logits: dividing every $z_v$ by the same positive $T_{\text{samp}}$ can stretch or squeeze the gaps between probabilities, but it can never change their order. The token with the largest logit stays the token with the largest probability at every temperature. The demo below proves it: the top-ranked token is identical at $T_{\text{samp}} = 0.1$ and $T_{\text{samp}} = 2.0$.
⚠️ "Higher temperature = more creative" Up to a point. But the probability mass you're releasing at high $T_{\text{samp}}$ lives in the tail, and the tail is mostly wrong tokens — misspellings, wrong-language fragments, non-sequiturs. Past roughly $T_{\text{samp}} \approx 1.2$ you are buying degradation, not creativity. "Creative" sampling is a narrow band, not a monotonic dial.
⚠️ "Beam search always gives the best answer" Beam search maximizes total sequence likelihood — and for open-ended generation the highest-likelihood continuation is bland, repetitive, often degenerate (the "likelihood trap": the safest next word forever is a loop). Reserve beam search for tasks with a single correct answer — translation, constrained extraction — not for chat.

Resolving the two ahas. On the confident position, top-$p$ = 0.9 keeps only a couple of tokens — the model has already made up its mind, and nucleus sampling respects that. On the uncertain position the very same top-$p$ = 0.9 keeps dozens, because the mass is spread thin. Top-$k$ = 20, by contrast, keeps exactly 20 in both cases — far too few when the model is genuinely unsure, needlessly many when it is certain. That mismatch is the entire argument for top-$p$ over top-$k$, and it is invisible until you interact with it. Entropy (shown live, in bits) is the one-number summary: low on the confident position, high on the uncertain one.

2 · The KV cache — why the second token is cheap

Decoding is autoregressive: token $i+1$ attends to every earlier position $1\dots i$. Recomputing all of their keys and values from scratch each step would be quadratic madness. So we cache them. Each layer stores one key vector and one value vector per past token; the query is the only thing computed fresh. The cache's size grows linearly with context, and its byte count is exactly:

$$\text{KV bytes} \;=\; 2 \cdot L \cdot H_{kv} \cdot d_{\text{head}} \cdot b \cdot S \cdot B$$

SymbolIsValue (Qwen3-8B)From
$2$one tensor for $K$, one for $V$2keys and values
$L$number of transformer layers36constants §1.1 [VP]
$H_{kv}$key/value heads — not query heads $H$8constants §1.1 [VP]
$d_{\text{head}}$per-head width128constants §1.1 [VP]
$b$bytes per stored element2 (bf16)KV dtype
$S$sequence length (context in tokens)up to 40,960notation §4.2
$B$batch size (concurrent sequences)1your workload
⚠️ It is $H_{kv}$, not $H$ The single most common error in this formula is writing $H = 32$ (the query heads) where it should be $H_{kv} = 8$ (the key/value heads). Qwen3-8B uses grouped-query attention: 32 query heads share just 8 K/V heads. Using $H$ overcounts the cache by exactly $32/8 = 4\times$. This is not a typo for "heads" — it is the whole reason GQA exists.
✍️ Worked — 144 KiB per token [DER, constants §4] Per token, per layer: $2 \times 8 \times 128 \times 2 = 4{,}096$ B $= 4$ KiB. Across all 36 layers: $$2 \cdot 36 \cdot 8 \cdot 128 \cdot 2 \;=\; 147{,}456\ \text{B} \;=\; \mathbf{144\ KiB/token}.$$ Now scale by context (still $B=1$, bf16): at $S = 32{,}768$ that's $\mathbf{4.50\ GiB}$; at the full $S = 40{,}960$, $\mathbf{5.625\ GiB}$; at a hypothetical $S = 131{,}072$, 18.0 GiB — which is larger than the 16.38 GB of model weights. Past a certain context the cache is the biggest thing in memory.

The GQA punchline. Had Qwen3-8B used ordinary multi-head attention with $H_{kv} = 32$, the cache would be $576$ KiB/token $= \mathbf{22.50\ GiB}$ at full context — versus $5.625$ GiB with GQA. Grouped-query attention saves exactly $4\times$ on the KV cache (constants §4), and that saving is what makes long-context decoding fit at all. And why cache $K$ and $V$ but not $Q$? Because $\mathbf{q}_i$ is used once — to attend from position $i$ — and then never again, while $K_{1:i}$ and $V_{1:i}$ are re-read by every future token. You cache what gets reused.

⚠️ "I'll just turn the KV cache off to save memory" Turning it off doesn't make decode cheaper — it makes it $O(S^2)$, recomputing every past key and value at every step, which on a long context is hundreds of times more compute (the LMSYS-style measurement is ~500× on realistic lengths). The real levers for a tight memory budget are a shorter context, a smaller batch, or an fp8 KV cache — never disabling the cache.

3 · Prefill vs decode, and the roofline that separates them

Prefill and decode run the identical network, yet they hit opposite walls. To see why, reopen the trunk's roofline.

Trunk callback — the roofline, p.43

On p.43 you derived, for a dense bf16 transformer, that arithmetic intensity equals tokens per forward pass:

$$I \;=\; \frac{\text{FLOPs}}{\text{bytes}} \;=\; \frac{2N\,S_{\text{fwd}}}{2N} \;=\; S_{\text{fwd}}\quad\text{FLOP/byte}$$

SymbolIsValueFrom
$I$arithmetic intensity — FLOPs done per byte movedFLOP/bytep.43 (trunk)
$N$non-embedding parameter count6,946,071,552constants §1.1 [VP]
$S_{\text{fwd}}$tokens processed in one forward pass1 (decode) / 2048 (prefill)the workload
$I^{\star}$the ridge — where compute- and bandwidth-bound meetsee belowp.43 / constants §6.4

The Spark's ridge is $I^{\star} = P_{\text{peak}}/\text{BW}$. Its exact value depends on the dense-BF16 compute ceiling, which NVIDIA does not publish for the GB10 — so this number is inferred, not published (constants §6.3–6.4): with the working ~62 TFLOP/s ceiling the ridge is 227 FLOP/byte; if the true usable ceiling is ~125 TFLOP/s (a live alternative that one SDXL benchmark leans toward), the ridge is 458. The course prints 227 with the inference label and names 458 as the live alternative — and the exercise below settles it by measurement. Either way the verdict for decode is the same:

  • Decode processes one token per forward, so $S_{\text{fwd}} = 1 \Rightarrow I = 1$ — that is 227× to 458× below the ridge. Decode is brutally bandwidth-bound: the GPU spends nearly all its time waiting for weights to stream in from memory, doing almost no arithmetic with each byte. This is where the Spark is weak.
  • Prefill processes the whole prompt at once, $S_{\text{fwd}} = 2048 \Rightarrow I = 2048$ — well above the ridge, firmly compute-bound. Same machine, same weights, opposite bottleneck.

That is the answer to the prediction's first half: the ~390× prefill/decode gap is not a bug or a driver problem — it is $S_{\text{fwd}} = 2048$ versus $S_{\text{fwd}} = 1$, two points on opposite sides of the roofline.

📐 The decode heuristic — [EST], ±10–15% $$\text{decode tok/s} \;\approx\; 0.65 \times \frac{\text{BW (GB/s)}}{\text{weight bytes read per token (GB)}} \qquad\text{dense models, batch 1}$$ Because decode is bandwidth-bound, throughput is set by how fast you can read the weights once per token. The 0.65 is the round, slightly-conservative memory-bandwidth-utilization (MBU) coefficient — real dense batch-1 systems land in the 60–75% band. This is an estimate; the label travels with it.

The subtle part is the numerator's denominator: which bytes get read per token. The physically correct count is decode-traffic = checkpoint size − the embedding table, because the input embedding is gathered (one row looked up per token) rather than streamed. For a quantized model this is neither the file size nor params×bits — it is the file minus that one lookup table, and getting it wrong moves a prediction by 15–25 percentage points. Under the correct convention, four independent real measurements all land in the 60–75% MBU band (constants §6.6–6.7 [VP]): Llama-3.1-8B FP8 60.3%, 70B FP8 69.8%, 8B NVFP4 70.5%, Qwen3-14B NVFP4 74.7%.

🔑 The failure mode is the lesson If your heuristic predicts more than 100% of peak bandwidth, your model is an MoE. gpt-oss-20B decodes at 49.7 tok/s, which on its 12.8 GB checkpoint implies a bandwidth of 637 GB/s — that's 234% of the Spark's 273 GB/s bus, a physical impossibility. The resolution: it's a mixture-of-experts, ~3.6B of ~20.9B params active per token, so only the active weights are read. A second trap: DeepSeek-R1-14B's 83.5 tok/s looks impossible too, until you notice it was measured at batch 8, not 1 — the weight read amortizes across 8 streams. One impossible number teaches the heuristic, its domain, and MoE's memory/compute decoupling at once.

Measured, on his box (constants §6.7 [VP]). Llama-3.1-8B on the Spark: decode $\mathbf{20.5}$ tok/s at batch 1 rising to $\mathbf{368}$ tok/s at batch 32 — an 18× gain — while prefill stays flat at $7{,}991 \to 7{,}949$ tok/s. That is the roofline, measured: decode was starving for bandwidth and batching fed it; prefill was already compute-bound and gained nothing. Memory bandwidth: $\mathbf{273\ GB/s}$ [VP].
