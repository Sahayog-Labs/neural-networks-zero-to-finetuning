Optimizers: SGD → Momentum → RMSProp → Adam → AdamW

One update rule was never enough. Each optimizer in the lineage exists to fix one specific way the previous one failed. We follow that chain of failures to the exact optimizer you will call on your LoRA in Part V — and we motivate it with nine gradients you already computed by hand.

The lineage: each optimizer fixes one failure

Gradient descent gave us one rule: step downhill, $\theta_{k+1} = \theta_k - \eta\,\mathbf{g}_k$. It has two honest failures, and the history of optimizers is the history of fixing them one at a time. The first failure is geometric — on a stretched valley the gradient points across the valley, not along it, so the iterate zigzags (page 13's ravine). The second is scale — different parameters have gradients of wildly different magnitude, and one $\eta$ either over-steps the big ones or starves the small ones. Momentum attacks the first. RMSProp attacks the second. Adam does both. AdamW fixes a bug Adam introduced. Nothing here is a speed hack bolted on for fun; each piece is a repair.

📐 Translation note — the step index is $k$, not $t$ Optimisation papers write the update with a step index $t$: $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta\nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$, and Adam's bias correction as $\beta_1^t,\beta_2^t$. This course reserves $t$ for the diffusion timestep (Part VI), so our optimizer step is $k$. When you read the Adam paper, its $t$ is our $k$. The one word that changes; the maths does not. (Bonus: $k$ makes "step vs epoch" typographically obvious — schedules are indexed by step, page 21.)

SGD — and the failure it can't fix

📐 Plain SGD $$\theta_{k+1} = \theta_k - \eta\,\mathbf{g}_k, \qquad \mathbf{g}_k = \nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}(\theta_k)$$ One learning rate, applied identically to every parameter. On the ravine of page 13 it zigzags across the valley floor and creeps along it: the very oscillation you watched blow up past $\eta_{\text{crit}} = 2/\lambda_{\max} = 0.1$.

SGD's problem is that $\eta$ is a single scalar shared by directions with very different curvature. Turn it up to make progress along the gentle direction and it diverges in the steep one; turn it down to stay stable in the steep direction and it crawls along the gentle one. There is no good single value. The next three optimizers are three ways of giving different directions different effective steps.

Momentum — a conditioning fix, not a speed hack

Keep a running average of the gradient and step with that instead of the raw gradient. Across the valley the gradient flips sign every step, so the running average cancels; along the valley it points the same way every step, so the average accumulates. The zigzag is damped and the along-valley motion is amplified — that is a change to the dynamics (the conditioning), not merely a bigger step.

📐 SGD with momentum $$\mathbf{v}_k = \mu\,\mathbf{v}_{k-1} + \mathbf{g}_k, \qquad \theta_{k+1} = \theta_k - \eta\,\mathbf{v}_k$$

✍️ Worked: the momentum amplification, $\mu = 0.9$ Point the gradient the same direction every step (motion along the valley), with $\mathbf{g}_k = \mathbf{g}$ constant. The velocity is a geometric series: $$\mathbf{v}_\infty = \mathbf{g}\,(1 + \mu + \mu^2 + \cdots) = \frac{\mathbf{g}}{1-\mu}.$$ At $\mu = 0.9$ the steady-state velocity is $\dfrac{1}{1-0.9} = \mathbf{10\times}$ the raw gradient (constants.md §9.3, [DER]). Momentum takes a persistent gentle slope and gives it a tenfold effective step — while a sign-flipping slope averages toward zero. That asymmetry is the whole point.
⚠️ "Momentum just makes it faster." It changes the dynamics. It damps oscillation across a ravine and accumulates motion along it — a conditioning fix. On a perfectly round bowl (no anisotropy) momentum buys you almost nothing; its value is precisely in the stretched directions where plain SGD zigzags.

RMSProp — give every parameter its own step

Momentum fixed the geometry but still uses one $\eta$ for every parameter. RMSProp fixes the scale: divide each parameter's step by the root-mean-square of its own recent gradients. A parameter with big gradients gets a small effective step; one with tiny gradients gets a large one. The single global $\eta$ becomes, effectively, one learning rate per parameter — which is exactly what a spread of gradient magnitudes demands.

📐 RMSProp (per-parameter scaling) $$s_k = \beta_2\,s_{k-1} + (1-\beta_2)\,g_k^2, \qquad \theta_{k+1} = \theta_k - \frac{\eta}{\sqrt{s_k}+\varepsilon}\,g_k$$
SymbolIsFrom
$s_k$running mean of the squared gradient, per parameterupdated here; $s_0 = 0$
$\beta_2$decay of that meanhyperparameter ($\sim 0.99$)
$g_k$this parameter's gradient (one component)backprop (page 14)
$\eta,\varepsilon,\theta_k$learning rate, stability floor, parameteras in the momentum rule above

$\sqrt{s_k}$ is the typical recent magnitude of that parameter's gradient, and dividing by it normalises every parameter to a comparable step. This is the piece that directly attacks the order-of-magnitude spread you are about to see.

Adam — momentum + RMSProp + one honest correction

Adam is exactly what it looks like: momentum (the first moment $m$) and RMSProp (the second moment $v$), run together, plus a bias correction that repairs a real defect at start-up.

📐 Adam $$m_k = \beta_1 m_{k-1} + (1-\beta_1)g_k, \qquad v_k = \beta_2 v_{k-1} + (1-\beta_2)g_k^2$$ $$\hat m_k = \frac{m_k}{1-\beta_1^{\,k}}, \qquad \hat v_k = \frac{v_k}{1-\beta_2^{\,k}}, \qquad \theta_{k+1} = \theta_k - \eta\,\frac{\hat m_k}{\sqrt{\hat v_k}+\varepsilon}$$
SymbolIsRange / valueFrom
$m_k$first moment — EMA of the gradient (the momentum part)$(P,)$updated here; $m_0 = 0$
$v_k$second moment — EMA of the squared gradient (the RMSProp part)$(P,)$updated here; $v_0 = 0$
$\beta_1$first-moment decay — always a numeric subscript$0.9$hyperparameter
$\beta_2$second-moment decay$0.95$ or $0.999$hyperparameter
$\hat m_k,\hat v_k$bias-corrected moments — undo the $m_0{=}v_0{=}0$ start$(P,)$the correction
$\varepsilon$stability floor (never divide by zero)$10^{-8}$hyperparameter

Because $m_0 = v_0 = 0$, both EMAs start too small — a start-up artifact, not a property of the gradient. The $1/(1-\beta^{\,k})$ factors remove exactly that artifact and fade to $1$.

✍️ Worked: why bias correction, and the $1000$-step memory At the first step, $k=1$, with $\beta_2 = 0.999$: the raw second moment is $v_1 = (1-\beta_2)\,g_1^2 = 0.001\,g_1^2$ — it is $1000\times$ too small (constants.md §9.3). The correction restores it exactly: $\hat v_1 = v_1/(1-\beta_2^{\,1}) = v_1/0.001 = g_1^2$. Without it, the update divides by $\sqrt{v_1}$, which is $\sqrt{1000}\approx 31.6\times$ too small, so the very first step would be $\approx 31.6\times$ too large. And the timescale of that memory is $$\frac{1}{1-\beta_2} = \frac{1}{1-0.999} = \mathbf{1000}\ \text{steps} \quad(\text{[DER]}).$$ $\beta_2 = 0.999$ means $v$ averages over the last $\sim 1000$ gradients. That long memory is the mechanistic reason warmup exists — the denominator is untrustworthy until it has seen enough gradients to fill, so you ease $\eta$ in. We build that on page 21. [THREAD:MEM]
⚠️ "Adam adapts the learning rate." It does not touch $\eta$. Adam adapts the per-parameter scaling of the gradient (the $\hat m/\sqrt{\hat v}$ term). The scalar $\eta$ out front is untouched, and you still need a schedule for it (page 21). Reading "adaptive" as "you don't need to tune the learning rate" is how people ship a bad run.

AdamW — decouple the weight decay

Weight decay pulls every parameter gently toward zero (regularisation, page 22). The classic way folds it into the gradient: $g_k \leftarrow g_k + \lambda\theta_k$. For plain SGD that is identical to L2 regularisation. For Adam it is not — because Adam then divides that $\lambda\theta_k$ term by $\sqrt{\hat v}$ too, so parameters with large gradients get less decay than parameters with small ones. The decay you asked for is silently rescaled per parameter. AdamW's one change: apply the decay straight to the weights, outside Adam's scaling.

📐 AdamW — the entire content of the paper $$\theta_{k+1} = \theta_k - \eta\left(\frac{\hat m_k}{\sqrt{\hat v_k}+\varepsilon} \;+\; \lambda\,\theta_k\right)$$
$\lambda$weight decay — pulls each weight toward zero at rate $\eta\lambda$hyperparameter ($0.1$)
$\hat m_k,\hat v_k,\varepsilon,\eta,\theta_k$exactly as in the Adam ledger abovereopened

The $\lambda\theta_k$ term is added after the $\sqrt{\hat v}$ division, so Adam's per-parameter scaling never touches it — every weight decays at the same rate $\eta\lambda$. In this course's engine, that is one flag: NN.Adam(params, {weightDecay: 0.1, decoupled: true}) is AdamW. $\lambda$ is weight decay and nothing else in the trunk.

⚠️ "Weight decay is the same as L2 regularisation." For SGD, yes; for Adam, no. Folding $\lambda\theta$ into the gradient lets Adam's $1/\sqrt{\hat v}$ rescale it per parameter, so the effective decay is no longer uniform. That divergence is the whole reason AdamW exists — it is not a cosmetic rename.

The spread beat — why one $\eta$ can't serve nine gradients

[THREAD:TN1 — input 2's nine gradients, reopened from page 14]. Here are the nine numbers you computed by hand, for $x=[0.60,\,-0.20]$ (constants.md §8.7):

✍️ The spread: $10.22\times$, all nine live — resolving the prediction $$\left|\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\theta}\right| \in \{0.1348,\ \mathbf{0.0449},\ 0.2214,\ 0.0738,\ 0.2246,\ 0.3690,\ 0.1975,\ 0.1504,\ \mathbf{0.4593}\}$$ The largest is $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial b_2 = 0.4593$; the smallest live one is $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial W_1[0][1] = 0.0449$. Their ratio: $$\frac{\max|g|}{\min|g|} = \frac{0.459260}{0.044917} = \mathbf{10.2246\times} \approx \mathbf{10.22\times}.$$ That is the answer to the prediction: the largest gradient is about ten times the smallest — one order of magnitude — and every one of the nine is live (no dead unit this input). A single global $\eta$ must either over-step $b_2$ or starve $W_1[0][1]$. It cannot do right by both. Per-parameter scaling is the fix, and that is precisely what RMSProp and Adam do.
🔑 The spread is structural — "just normalise your inputs" doesn't save you You might object: the spread comes from the input, so normalise the input and it goes away. It does not. Since $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial W_1[i][j] = \delta_{1,i}\,x_j$, you can inflate the spread by widening the feature ratio $|x_0/x_1|$ — an input like $[0.10,\,-2.00]$ reaches $42\times$. But the honest answer to that is "normalise your inputs," not "use Adam." The decisive check: even at a perfectly balanced $1{:}1$ feature ratio, the best achievable spread across the nine gradients is still $8.64\times$ (constants.md §8.7). The spread survives normalisation because it comes from the network's own structure — different layers, different roles — not just the data. That is what makes "therefore Adam" honest rather than a workaround for un-normalised inputs. [0.60, -0.20]'s mild $3{:}1$ ratio is where the story is transparent, not rigged.
⚠️ "Adam is strictly better than SGD." Well-tuned SGD + momentum still generalises better on vision benchmarks. Adam's win is on transformers — and, crucially, it wins untuned: the per-parameter scaling makes it forgiving of a learning rate that would break SGD. For the models this course targets, that forgiveness is why AdamW is the default.

The optimizer you will actually call

Everything above collapses to a few lines of PyTorch. This is the exact optimizer you will use on your LoRA in Part V — same call, same defaults:

Python
import torch

model = torch.nn.Sequential(
    torch.nn.Linear(2, 2), torch.nn.Tanh(), torch.nn.Linear(2, 1),
)

# AdamW = Adam with DECOUPLED weight decay. Modern LLM defaults:
opt = torch.optim.AdamW(
    model.parameters(),
    lr=2e-4, betas=(0.9, 0.95), eps=1e-8, weight_decay=0.1,
)

x = torch.randn(32, 2)
y = torch.randn(32, 1)
for k in range(1000):                       # k is the OPTIMIZER STEP, not t
    loss = torch.nn.functional.mse_loss(model(x), y)
    loss.backward()
    torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), 1.0)  # grad clip 1.0
    opt.step(); opt.zero_grad()
Try it code/17_optimizer_race.py trains the TN-1 MLP (input-2 config) to convergence under SGD, Adam, and AdamW, logs steps-to-target for each, and prints the per-parameter update magnitudes so you can watch Adam equalise the $10.22\times$ gradient spread on your own box.
🔬 Deep dive: Muon halves the optimizer state but is not your path (yet)

Optimisation is not a settled field. Muon (2024–25) keeps one momentum buffer instead of Adam's two moments — half the optimizer state — and, per its authors, reaches comparable loss at roughly 52% of AdamW's FLOPs on the matrices it handles. The catch: it applies only to 2-D parameters (weight matrices), so every real "Muon" run is an AdamW hybrid — Muon on the matrices, AdamW on the biases, norm gains, and embeddings. These figures are reported / [EST], not values this course measured, and Muon is a moving target. Ship it as evidence that "which optimizer" is still live research — not as the optimizer you should reach for on your first LoRA. That is AdamW, for the reasons above.
