Score Matching & the SDE View

You did not train an $\epsilon$-predictor and a score model. You trained one network, and three lines of calculus show it is the intractable $\nabla_{\mathbf x}\log p$ from page 53 — divided by a lookup number. Once you see that, "Karras sigmas" and the reverse-time SDE stop being folklore.

🔮 Predict first The score $\mathbf s = \nabla_{\mathbf x}\log p_t(\mathbf x)$ points from where you are toward the data. The noise $\boldsymbol\epsilon$ is what was added to the data to get you here — it points away. So before you see the three-line derivation, commit to the sign that relates them: is $\mathbf s_\theta \propto +\epsilon_\theta$ or $\mathbf s_\theta \propto -\epsilon_\theta$?

What the score is, and what it is not

Page 53 hit a wall: sampling from $p(\mathbf x)$ needs the normalizer $Z_\theta$, an integral over every possible image, and it is intractable. The escape — foreshadowed there — is to never form $p$ at all, but to work with its gradient in image space. That gradient is the score.

🔑 The score is a gradient with respect to the image, not the parameters $$\mathbf s(\mathbf x) \equiv \nabla_{\mathbf x}\log p(\mathbf x)\qquad \text{same shape as } \mathbf x.$$ Backprop taught you $\nabla_\theta\mathcal L$ — the gradient with respect to the weights, used to train. The score is the same differentiation operator pointed at a different variable: the pixels. Nothing here updates a weight. It answers "which way do I nudge this image to make it more like real data?"
[THREAD: the chain rule — beat 7] Same object as backprop (page 14) and autograd (page 15): the derivative, the course's verb. What is new: we differentiate $\log p$ with respect to $\mathbf x$ instead of $\mathcal L$ with respect to $\theta$. Reopen the beat where the chain rule first "differentiated the thing you feed in" (page 4's $\theta$-vs-$x$ flip) — the score is that flip, at scale.

The picture to hold: $\log p$ is a landscape — high on the data manifold, low in the voids between. The score is the force that landscape exerts, pointing uphill toward probability mass. Sampling is then rolling downhill-in-energy with a jitter that keeps you from collapsing to a single peak — this is Langevin dynamics:

$$\mathbf x \;\leftarrow\; \mathbf x + \tfrac{\delta}{2}\,\mathbf s_\theta(\mathbf x) + \sqrt{\delta}\,\mathbf z,\qquad \mathbf z\sim\mathcal N(0,I).$$

★ The three-line reveal

Here is the payoff. We already have a network — $\epsilon_\theta$, trained by $\mathcal L_{\text{simple}}$ on page 55. Claim: it is the score, up to a constant you can look up. Watch. The forward process handed us a Gaussian in closed form (page 54, Eq. 3.3):

$$q(\mathbf x_t\mid\mathbf x_0)=\mathcal N\!\big(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf x_0,\; (1-\bar\alpha_t)\,I\big) \;\Rightarrow\; \log q = -\frac{\lVert \mathbf x_t-\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf x_0\rVert^2} {2(1-\bar\alpha_t)} + \text{const}.$$

Differentiate the log-density with respect to $\mathbf x_t$ (a quadratic — one line), then substitute Eq. 3.3's own definition $\mathbf x_t-\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf x_0=\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon$:

$$\nabla_{\mathbf x_t}\log q = -\frac{\mathbf x_t-\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf x_0}{1-\bar\alpha_t} = -\frac{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon}{1-\bar\alpha_t} = -\frac{\boldsymbol\epsilon}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}.$$

🔑 The score is your noise-predictor, rescaled Replacing the true (unknown) $\boldsymbol\epsilon$ by the network that estimates it: $$\mathbf s_\theta(\mathbf x_t,t) \;=\; -\frac{\epsilon_\theta(\mathbf x_t,t)}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}.$$ The intractable $\nabla_{\mathbf x}\log p$ that stopped us on page 53 is literally the network we already have, divided by the schedule number $\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}$. And there is the minus sign — the predict answer: the score points opposite to $\boldsymbol\epsilon$. Of course it does — the noise points away from the data, so unwinding it (toward the data) is its negative.

Two consequences you will cash in later. First, this is what makes the CFG derivation on page 61 possible — classifier-free guidance is a statement about scores, and now you know scores are just rescaled $\epsilon$. Second, it unifies two papers that were thought to be different methods: NCSN (Song & Ermon, 2019 — "train a score model") and DDPM (Ho et al., 2020 — "train a noise predictor") are the same algorithm, discovered independently. Same lesson is coming again on page 57, where flow matching turns out to be a third face of the same object.

Why the noise was necessary all along

The score $\nabla_{\mathbf x}\log p$ only exists where $p > 0$. Real images live on a thin manifold — a crumpled sheet inside the enormous pixel (or latent) space. Off the sheet, $p = 0$, $\log p = -\infty$, and the gradient is undefined: there is no arrow telling a stray point which way to go. Adding Gaussian noise inflates the sheet into a solid — it convolves the data with $\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2 I)$, smearing probability mass into the voids so that every point gets a well-defined arrow. That is the predict payoff's deeper half: you cannot learn the raw data's score; you can only learn the noised data's score, which is exactly what $\epsilon_\theta(\cdot, t)$ gives you.

🔑 The schedule is a smoothing curriculum A single noise level gives one blurred landscape. A ladder of levels (large $\sigma$ → small $\sigma$) is a curriculum: at large $\sigma$ the landscape is one broad hill, so coarse arrows find the right neighborhood; as $\sigma$ shrinks the hills sharpen and fine arrows find the right house. Sampling is annealing down that ladder — which is precisely what running $t$ from $T$ down to $0$ does. Drag the demo below and watch the arrows go from "point at the center of mass" to "point at the nearest mode."
📐 Tweedie's formula — the posterior-mean denoiser The same score gives the best guess of the clean image in one shot: $$\hat{\mathbf x}_0 = \frac{\mathbf x_t + (1-\bar\alpha_t)\,\mathbf s_\theta(\mathbf x_t,t)}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}} = \mathbb E[\mathbf x_0\mid\mathbf x_t].$$ Substituting $\mathbf s_\theta=-\epsilon_\theta/\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}$ recovers page 55's $\hat{\mathbf x}_0=(\mathbf x_t-\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\epsilon_\theta)/\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}$ — same object. Reading it as a posterior mean explains why $\hat{\mathbf x}_0$ is blurry at high $t$: an average over everything the noise could have hidden.

The SDE view: staircase becomes ramp

DDPM's 1000 discrete steps are a staircase. Let $T\to\infty$ and the staircase becomes a smooth ramp — a stochastic differential equation. The forward noising of page 54 is the VP-SDE ("variance preserving"):

$$d\mathbf x = -\tfrac12\,\beta(t)\,\mathbf x\,dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)}\;d\mathbf w.$$

The drift $-\tfrac12\beta\mathbf x$ shrinks the signal; the $d\mathbf w$ term injects noise. Note $\mathrm{Var}[d\mathbf w]\propto dt$, so noise accumulates as $\sqrt{dt}$ — the same random-walk $\sqrt{N}$ law you already know. Set the drift to zero ($f=0$) and you get the VE-SDE ("variance exploding"), which is NCSN — and which ComfyUI exposes as the "Karras sigmas" scheduler. That dropdown is not a mystery setting; it is a choice of SDE.

🔑 The reverse-time SDE runs on the score Anderson (1982): every forward SDE has a reverse-time twin, and the score is the piece that makes it run: $$d\mathbf x = \big[\,f(\mathbf x,t) - g(t)^2\,\mathbf s_\theta(\mathbf x,t)\,\big]\,dt + g(t)\,d\bar{\mathbf w}.$$ This is the theorem the whole field stands on: learn the score, and you can integrate the noising process backwards to generate. DDPM's ancestral sampler is one discretization of it; the deterministic probability-flow ODE (page 57, page 59's DDIM) is another. Every sampler in your ComfyUI dropdown is a numerical scheme for this line.

Translation note. Papers write the score as $s_\theta$ or $\nabla_{\mathbf x}\log p_t$; the "VE / VP" names are Song et al. (2021); "Karras sigmas" is Karras et al. (2022, EDM). $\sigma$ here is a noise standard deviation (diffusion's $\sigma_t$), never a sigmoid — on this track a logistic is written torch.sigmoid / SiLU.

🔬 Deep dive: deriving the reverse-time drift from Anderson's theorem

Anderson's time-reversal takes a forward Itô SDE $d\mathbf x=f\,dt+g\,d\mathbf w$ and shows the reverse process satisfies $d\mathbf x=[f-g^2\nabla_{\mathbf x}\log p_t(\mathbf x)]\,dt+g\,d\bar{\mathbf w}$, where $\bar{\mathbf w}$ is a Wiener process running in reverse time and $p_t$ is the marginal density at time $t$. For the VP-SDE, $f=-\tfrac12\beta(t)\mathbf x$ and $g=\sqrt{\beta(t)}$, so the reverse drift is $-\tfrac12\beta\mathbf x-\beta\,\mathbf s_\theta$. Dropping the $g\,d\bar{\mathbf w}$ term and halving the score correction gives the deterministic probability-flow ODE, whose marginals match the SDE at every $t$ — that is why DDIM ($\eta{=}0$, page 59) reaches the same distribution without re-injecting noise. Nothing on later pages depends on this derivation; it is here for the reader who wants to see where the $-g^2\mathbf s$ comes from.
