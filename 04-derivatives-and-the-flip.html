A derivative is a sensitivity

Forget "slope of a tangent line" for a moment and hold onto the thing that makes derivatives matter to a network: a derivative answers "if I nudge this input a hair, how much does the output move, and in which direction?" That is the entire question training asks, a few billion times.

$$f'(x) \;=\; \frac{df}{dx} \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}.$$

Read the limit as plain notation for "make $h$ small enough that shrinking it further stops changing the answer." We will never need an epsilon-delta argument in this course. What we will need constantly is the reading: $f'(x)$ big and positive means "$f$ is very sensitive here, and moving $x$ up moves $f$ up"; $f'(x)$ near zero means "$f$ barely notices you"; $f'(x)$ negative means "up in $x$ is down in $f$."

📐 Units — the honest answer to "why is $\eta = 3\times10^{-4}$?" Derivatives carry units. If the loss $\mathcal{L}$ is in nats and a weight $w$ is dimensionless, then $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial w$ is nats per unit weight. The learning rate $\eta$ that multiplies it must carry the inverse units for $\eta\,\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial w$ to be a weight-sized change. That is the whole reason a sensible $\eta$ is $10^{-5}$ and not $0.5$: it is a units-carrying step size, not a magic dial. We make this precise on page 13; plant the intuition now.

The $\vartheta$-vs-$x$ flip, now in symbols

Page 1 said it in English: at inference the knobs are fixed and you feed data; at training the data is fixed and you turn the knobs. Here is the same sentence in one line of calculus. A network is a function with two kinds of argument,

$$f(\mathbf{x};\,\boldsymbol\theta),$$

the data $\mathbf{x}$ and the parameters $\boldsymbol\theta$. It has two derivatives, and the course cares about exactly one of them:

QuestionDerivativeWho holds stillHow often the course needs it
Inference: how does the output move if the input changes?$\partial f/\partial\mathbf{x}$knobs $\boldsymbol\theta$ fixed, data variesalmost never
Training: how does the loss move if a knob changes?$\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\boldsymbol\theta$data $\mathbf{x}$ fixed, knobs varythe course is about it
🔑 The flip We hold $\mathbf{x}$ constant and differentiate the loss with respect to $\boldsymbol\theta$. That one choice is every downstream mechanism: requires_grad, param.grad, backprop, autograd. Page 1's "training turns the knobs" was not a metaphor — the knobs are literally the differentiation variable, and the inputs usually carry no gradient at all.

The only derivatives you need

Here is a claim the rest of the course will keep proving: you need seven derivative rules. Everything else is these seven, plus the chain rule (page 5), plus shape bookkeeping (page 2).

That table is complete. The last one is worth staring at: ReLU's derivative is $1$ or $0$ — it either passes a gradient through untouched or blocks it entirely. It never shrinks one. Hold that thought until page 11.

The sigmoid slope pays for itself, so derive it once. With $\sigma(x) = (1+e^{-x})^{-1}$, the quotient/chain rule gives

$$\sigma'(x) \;=\; \frac{e^{-x}}{\bigl(1+e^{-x}\bigr)^2} \;=\; \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}\cdot\frac{e^{-x}}{1+e^{-x}} \;=\; \sigma(x)\bigl(1-\sigma(x)\bigr).$$

✍️ The number that kills deep sigmoid nets $\sigma'$ is a product of two things that sum to $1$, so it is largest when both are $\tfrac12$ — at $x=0$: $$\sigma'(0) \;=\; \sigma(0)\bigl(1-\sigma(0)\bigr) \;=\; 0.5 \times 0.5 \;=\; \mathbf{0.25}.$$ That is the maximum. A sigmoid can never pass more than a quarter of a gradient through, and usually far less. Remember $0.25$ — on page 5 you will multiply it by itself thirty-two times and watch a deep network's gradient vanish to $5.4\times10^{-20}$, and on page 11 that number is why ReLU won. ($\sigma'(0)=0.25$, constants.md §9.3 [DER].)

From one slope to the gradient — and which way is uphill

A network has millions of knobs, so we need all the partial derivatives at once. Stack them:

$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol\theta}\mathcal{L} \;=\; \left[\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\theta_1},\;\dots,\;\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\theta_P}\right]^{\!\top} \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{P}.$$

📐 The gradient has the same shape as $\boldsymbol\theta$ $\nabla_{\boldsymbol\theta}\mathcal{L}$ is a vector with one entry per parameter — exactly $\boldsymbol\theta$'s shape. This is not a coincidence you memorize; it is why param.grad always has param.shape, and it makes every optimizer's update line readable at a glance. (The rule is universal; notation.md §8.)

Now the payoff. The gradient's direction is the direction of steepest ascent — and the proof is the dot product you met on page 3, doing structural work rather than decoration. Ask: among all unit directions $\mathbf{u}$, which one increases $f$ fastest? The rate of change along $\mathbf{u}$ is the directional derivative, and by page 3's second form of the dot product,

$$\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} \;=\; \lVert\nabla f\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{u}\rVert\cos\vartheta \;=\; \lVert\nabla f\rVert\cos\vartheta \qquad(\lVert\mathbf{u}\rVert = 1).$$

SymbolIsShapeFrom
$\nabla f$the gradient — every partial slope, stacked$(P,1)$defined just above
$\mathbf{u}$a candidate step direction, unit length$(P,1)$what we vary
$\vartheta$the geometric angle between $\nabla f$ and $\mathbf{u}$scalar (radians)page 3's atom — not $\theta$
$\lVert\nabla f\rVert$the gradient's lengthscalar$\sqrt{\textstyle\sum_i (\partial f/\partial\theta_i)^2}$

$\lVert\nabla f\rVert$ is fixed by where you stand; the only free quantity is $\cos\vartheta$, which is largest — exactly $1$ — when $\vartheta = 0$, i.e. when $\mathbf{u}$ points along $\nabla f$. So the gradient is the steepest-uphill direction, and $-\nabla f$ is steepest downhill. The spine is a spine: the atom from page 3 just told us which way to walk.

🔑 One dot product, two jobs The same operation that scores "how much of $\mathbf{x}$ resembles the template $\mathbf{w}$" (page 3) also decides "which nudge to the knobs most reduces the loss." Steepest descent is not a new idea; it is the dot product, read for its angle.

Taking the step — and why $\eta$ is a step size, not a speed

A first-order Taylor expansion says that for a small move $\boldsymbol\Delta$, $f(\boldsymbol\theta + \boldsymbol\Delta) \approx f(\boldsymbol\theta) + \nabla f\cdot\boldsymbol\Delta$. Choose $\boldsymbol\Delta = -\eta\,\nabla f$ and the loss drops by about $\eta\lVert\nabla f\rVert^2$ — a guaranteed decrease, as long as the step is small enough that the linear approximation still holds. That "small enough" is the whole story, and it is where the predict lives. The update, written with the optimizer step index $k$:

$$\boldsymbol\theta_{k+1} \;=\; \boldsymbol\theta_{k} - \eta\,\nabla_{\boldsymbol\theta}\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol\theta_k).$$

SymbolIsShapeFrom
$\boldsymbol\theta_k$all parameters at optimizer step $k$$(P,1)$the knobs (page 1's "flip")
$k$optimizer step index — not $t$integercounts updates; see translation note below
$\eta$learning rate — the step size, and nothing elsescalarunits carry the inverse of the loss/param units
$\nabla_{\boldsymbol\theta}\mathcal{L}$the loss gradient — every partial slope, stacked$(P,1)$defined above; $-\nabla$ is steepest downhill
$\boldsymbol\Delta$the move we make, $=-\eta\,\nabla_{\boldsymbol\theta}\mathcal{L}$$(P,1)$the Taylor step
📐 Translation note — we write $k$, papers write $t$ This course indexes the optimizer step with $k$ ($\boldsymbol\theta_{k+1} = \boldsymbol\theta_k - \eta\mathbf{g}_k$), because $t$ is reserved for diffusion time. Optimization papers almost universally write $t$ for this same counter. A side benefit: $k$ makes "step vs epoch" typographically obvious — learning-rate schedules are indexed by step $k$, not epoch, and that is a top-five real bug. (notation.md §3.2.)

Treat $\eta$ as a step size, not a learning speed. Take a step that is too big and the linear picture is a lie — you overshoot the valley and land on the far wall, higher than you started. Do that repeatedly and the loss doesn't slow down; it explodes. Speed and stability trade off against each other. You are about to watch a loss go to NaN from a learning rate only about $3.5\times$ too large.

You lose days to a curve that looks fine. (decisions.md D-09, constants.md §9.4.)

Three things that feel true and aren't

⚠️ "The gradient points toward the minimum." No — it points locally uphill, so $-\nabla f$ points locally downhill, which in a curved valley points at the wall, not the exit. Follow it literally and you zig-zag across the valley floor instead of running down it. This is precisely why momentum and Adam exist. Words alone never fix this intuition — the zig-zag has to be seen, and you will see it on page 13.
⚠️ "A bigger gradient means you're closer to the answer." Magnitude says nothing about distance. A zero gradient could be a minimum, a maximum, a saddle, or a flat plateau — and in a space of $8\times10^{9}$ dimensions, saddles vastly outnumber true minima. The gradient tells you the local direction and steepness, never "how far to go." (The saddle count is page 13.)
⚠️ "Learning rate is how fast you learn." It is a step size. Past a threshold set by the local curvature, a larger $\eta$ makes you overshoot and diverge — you learn slower, then not at all. Speed and stability are in tension, and the whole craft of schedules and warmup (page 21) is negotiating that tension.
🔬 Deep dive: why gradient descent finds solutions that generalize is not settled in 2026

It is tempting to think there must be a clean theorem explaining why following $-\nabla f$ on a wildly non-convex loss lands on parameters that work on unseen data. There isn't one everybody agrees on. Competing accounts — implicit regularization (SGD's noise quietly prefers flat, simple minima), the lottery-ticket hypothesis (training mostly discovers a good sparse subnetwork that was there at init), and loss-landscape connectivity (the good minima form one big connected basin) — all have evidence and adherents, none is decisive. This is a real open problem, not a gap in your understanding. The engineering works anyway; the theory is genuinely incomplete, and a course that pretended otherwise would be lying.

Run this — code/descend.py The playground, in ~20 lines of Python: the same quartic, its derivative, and a descent loop with a --lr flag. Run it at --lr 0.35 and it blows up exactly like the demo. CPU, under a second. The NaN in your real training logs later is this, at scale.
Python
import argparse, math

def f(x):  # the quartic from the demo
    return x**4 - 3*x**2 + x + 2

def df(x):  # f'(x) = 4x^3 - 6x + 1
    return 4*x**3 - 6*x + 1

p = argparse.ArgumentParser()
p.add_argument("--lr", type=float, default=0.1)
p.add_argument("--x0", type=float, default=-2.0)
a = p.parse_args()

x = a.x0
for k in range(200):                 # k = optimizer step
    x = x - a.lr * df(x)      # the update, θ_{k+1} = θ_k - η f'(θ_k)
    if not math.isfinite(x):        # the loss went to Inf/NaN
        print(f"diverged at step {k}"); break
else:
    print(f"x={x:.4f}  f(x)={f(x):.4f}")
