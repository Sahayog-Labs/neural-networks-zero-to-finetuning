Setting the Gain on a 100-Stage Amplifier

Initialization, vanishing/exploding gradients, and clipping are not three topics. They are one number — the per-stage gain $g$ — told forward (how you set the weights) and backward (what the gradient does on the way home). Get $g$ near 1 and a 100-layer network trains; miss it and it is dead before you press Train.

🧵 Thread [THREAD:CHAIN — the verb, iterated $L$ times]. Page 14 mechanised the chain rule into one backprop recursion, $\boldsymbol\delta^{(\ell)} = \big(W^{(\ell+1)\top}\boldsymbol\delta^{(\ell+1)}\big)\odot \phi'(\mathbf z^{(\ell)})$. Stack that recursion $L$ deep and it becomes a product of $L$ factors — and a product of factors is an amplifier. This page is what happens when you run that amplifier a hundred stages: the same geometric product governs both how the signal flows forward and how the gradient flows back.
🔮 Predict first You stack 100 layers. Each one multiplies the signal's scale by a factor $g$ — a per-stage gain. If $g = 1.1$, what is the signal's scale by layer 100 relative to layer 0? What if $g = 0.9$? Compute $1.1^{100}$ and $0.9^{100}$ before you scroll, and write down what each does to a network.

Part A — Initialization: setting $g$ before any data arrives

🔑 The two numbers that decide everything Resolving the prediction directly: a per-stage gain compounds geometrically over depth, so by layer 100 the signal is scaled by $g^{100}$. $$1.1^{100} = \mathbf{13{,}781}, \qquad 0.9^{100} = \mathbf{2.7\times10^{-5}}.$$ A gain of $1.1$ — a mere 10% too hot — blows the signal up by four orders of magnitude and clips. A gain of $0.9$ — 10% too cold — collapses it to nothing and vanishes. You need $g \approx 1.000$, forward and backward. Two numbers do more than a page of prose: initialization is the discipline of pinning $g$ to 1 before a single example is seen. (constants.md §9.3, [DER].)
⚠️ Why all-zeros (or any constant) fails: symmetry never breaks Set every weight to zero — or to any single constant — and every neuron in a layer computes the same pre-activation, receives the same gradient, and takes the same step. They stay identical forever: a 4096-wide layer collapses to one effective neuron, and the loss drops to the prior and flatlines. The failure is not that gradients are zero — it is that they are identical. Randomness in $W$ is what breaks the tie. Note the asymmetry: biases can safely start at zero (the random $W$ already breaks symmetry), so "never initialize to zero" is an over-generalization — it is about $W$.

The three-line derivation behind every init scheme

Take one pre-activation $z_j = \sum_{i=1}^{n_{\text{in}}} w_{ji}\,x_i$. Assume the weights are zero-mean and independent of the inputs. Variance of a sum of independent terms is the sum of variances, and for a product of independent zero-mean variables the variances multiply, so:

📐 Variance propagation (training §9.2) $$\mathrm{Var}(z_j) \;=\; n_{\text{in}}\,\mathrm{Var}(w)\,\mathrm{Var}(x).$$ To hold the signal's scale constant across a layer — $\mathrm{Var}(z) = \mathrm{Var}(x)$, i.e. $g = 1$ — you need exactly $$\boxed{\;\mathrm{Var}(w) = \dfrac{1}{n_{\text{in}}}\;}$$ That single requirement is the whole of Xavier and He; everything else is a correction to it.

Two named corrections adjust the "1" for what the activation does to the variance:

  • Xavier / Glorot (for tanh / sigmoid, roughly linear near 0): $\mathrm{Var}(w) = \dfrac{2}{n_{\text{in}} + n_{\text{out}}}$ — averages fan-in and fan-out so the signal is preserved forward and backward.
  • He / Kaiming (for ReLU): $\mathrm{Var}(w) = \dfrac{2}{n_{\text{in}}}$. The "2" is not decoration — it is literally "because ReLU throws away half the signal" (it zeroes every negative input), so you double the variance to compensate.
✍️ Worked: He init at $d = 4096$ predicts GPT-2's config file [THREAD:QWEN] Qwen3-8B's residual width is $d = 4096$. A hidden linear layer has $n_{\text{in}} = 4096$, so He init sets $$\mathrm{Var}(w) = \frac{2}{4096}, \qquad \mathrm{std}(w) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{4096}} = \mathbf{0.0221}.$$ GPT-2 shipped with weights initialized at std $\approx 0.02$. The theory predicts the config file — that 0.0221 and the 0.02 you find in real model code are the same fact. (constants.md §9.3, [DER].)
🔬 Deep dive: GPT-2 scales residual projections by $1/\sqrt{2L}$ so the residual stream doesn't grow with depth

The residual stream is a running sum: each block adds its output back. Summing $L$ roughly independent contributions grows the stream's variance $\propto L$ (the same geometric-product logic, applied to an accumulating sum instead of a chain). GPT-2 counters this by scaling the weights of the layers that write into the residual stream by $1/\sqrt{2L}$, so the accumulated variance stays $O(1)$ regardless of depth. It is the same "pin the gain to 1" discipline, bookkept over a sum rather than a product.

🔬 Deep dive: μP rescales init and per-layer LR so the optimal learning rate transfers from a small proxy to a large model

[EST — medium confidence.] Maximal-update parameterization (μP) rescales both the initialization and the per-layer learning rate as a function of width, so that the learning rate that is optimal on a narrow proxy model stays optimal on a much wider one. This is why labs can tune hyperparameters on a 40M-parameter model and apply them to a 40B one, and it is the theory behind the folklore that "the learning rate should shrink as the model gets wider." Mentioned so you recognize it; not derived here.

📐 The four bad-init failure signatures (training §9.3) Each seeds a row of page 24's debugging table:
  1. All-zeros / constant → loss flatlines at the prior; symmetry never breaks.
  2. Too small (std $\sim 10^{-3}$) → signal vanishes with depth; looks exactly like a dead learning rate, and is misdiagnosed as one constantly.
  3. Too large (std $\sim 1.0$) → activations saturate or overflow to NaN.
  4. Constant nonzero → identical to all-zeros: symmetry, not scale, is the problem.
Note that (2) and (3) are opposite symptoms — stuck vs NaN — from the same cause: a mis-set gain.

Part B — Vanishing / exploding gradients: the same product, run backward

Initialization pinned the gain for the forward signal. But the gradient runs the network in reverse, and it hits the same multiplication at every layer. Iterate page 14's $\boldsymbol\delta$ recursion (BP2) all the way from the output to layer 1 and it telescopes into a product of $L$ factors:

📐 The gradient is a product of $L$ factors (training §10.2) $$\boldsymbol\delta^{(1)} \;=\; \Big(\textstyle\prod_{\ell=1}^{L-1} W^{(\ell+1)\top}\, \mathrm{diag}\,\phi'(\mathbf z^{(\ell)})\Big)\,\boldsymbol\delta^{(L)}.$$ If the typical factor's magnitude is $<1$, the product vanishes; if $>1$, it explodes. This is the identical $g^{L}$ story as Part A's $1.1^{100}$ / $0.9^{100}$ — one lesson, two directions. [THREAD:CHAIN] (each factor is exactly one step of the page-14 recursion).

✍️ Worked: why deep sigmoid stacks could not be trained The sigmoid's derivative peaks at the origin: $\sigma'(0) = \sigma(0)\,(1-\sigma(0)) = 0.5 \times 0.5 = \mathbf{0.25}$, and it is smaller everywhere else. So every layer multiplies the gradient by at most $0.25$: $$0.25^{10} = 9.54\times10^{-7}, \qquad 0.25^{32} = \mathbf{5.4\times10^{-20}}.$$ After 32 sigmoid layers the gradient reaching layer 1 is $5.4\times10^{-20}$ — still a perfectly representable float32 number (fp32 reaches down to $\sim10^{-38}$), but effectively dead: the weight will not move in any human timescale. It is tiny, not zero — the machine-vs-math theme again. This is precisely why deep sigmoid networks were untrainable, and why the field moved to ReLU/tanh, careful init, and residuals. (constants.md §9.3, [DER].)
📐 The historical fixes are one lineage solving one problem Trunk owns the WHY; Part III owns the WHERE (the block diagram). (D-14.)
  1. Better activations. ReLU has derivative exactly $1$ on its active half — no shrinkage, no saturation for $z>0$.
  2. Careful init (Part A) — pin the per-stage factor to $\approx 1$.
  3. Residual connections — the identity term. A residual block computes $y = x + F(x)$, so its Jacobian is $$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x} = I + \frac{\partial F}{\partial x}.$$ The $+I$ guarantees a gradient path of magnitude $\approx 1$ through any depth, no matter what $F$ does. Worked: a plain gain of $0.9$ over 36 layers gives $0.9^{36} = \mathbf{0.0225}$ — a $44\times$ attenuation; with the residual highway that same path stays $\approx 1$. (Foreshadows the residual stream of the transformer block, D-14.)
  4. Normalization (page 20) — keeps the gain near 1 during training, not just at init.

Part C — Gradient clipping: the operational patch

📐 Global-norm clipping at 1.0 (constants.md §9.3, [VP]) Even with good init, a rare batch can produce a gradient spike. Global-norm clipping caps it: if the whole gradient vector's norm exceeds a threshold $c$, rescale it back to length $c$ while keeping its direction — $$\text{if } \|\mathbf g\| > c: \quad \mathbf g \leftarrow c\,\frac{\mathbf g}{\|\mathbf g\|}.$$ The near-universal value is $c = \mathbf{1.0}$. Order matters absolutely: clip after backward() (the gradients must exist) and before step() (or the optimizer already moved). Clip the wrong thing, or at the wrong time, and it silently does nothing.
⚠️ Never fp16 on the Spark — bf16 has fp32's range fp16 has only 5 exponent bits: its maximum finite value is $\mathbf{65{,}504}$. A single activation past that overflows to inf, which becomes NaN the moment it meets a subtraction — a whole run lost to one large number. bf16 keeps fp32's 8 exponent bits (same range, fewer mantissa bits), so it does not overflow on the values a network actually produces. On Blackwell there is no reason to reach for fp16; the DGX Spark should never see it. (constants.md §9.3 / §9.7; ties to page 24's NaN table.)

Six traps

⚠️ "Init just needs to be random." It needs a specific variance tied to fan-in and activation. A random-but-wrong-scale init fails exactly like zeros — the signal vanishes or explodes — just less obviously, because the network still looks initialized.
⚠️ "He init is for everything." He assumes ReLU. Pair He with tanh and you over-scale by $\sqrt2$ per layer: over 50 layers that is $2^{25} \approx 3\times10^{7}$ too hot. Match the scheme to the activation.
⚠️ "All-zeros fails because the gradients are zero." They are identical, not necessarily zero — symmetry never breaks. And biases can start at zero: the random $W$ already broke the tie.
⚠️ "Vanishing gradients are a solved, historical problem." The mechanism is the same geometric product it always was. Residuals, normalization, and scaled init manage it; they do not repeal it. Very deep or recurrent stacks still hit it.
⚠️ "Clipping fixes a high learning rate." Clipping caps rare spikes. A persistently-too-high $\eta$ still diverges every step; clipping just makes the divergence tidier. Fix the learning rate, not the symptom.
⚠️ "Exploding and vanishing are opposite bugs needing opposite fixes." They are the same product with the factor on opposite sides of 1. The same three tools — scaled init, residuals, normalization — address both. There is one disease here, not two.
