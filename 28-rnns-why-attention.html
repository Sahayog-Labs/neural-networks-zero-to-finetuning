RNNs and the Sequential Bottleneck: Why Attention Had to Exist

You will never write an LSTM from scratch, and this page will not teach you to. It has two jobs: make the pain that killed recurrent networks visceral enough that attention arrives as relief, not novelty — and install three words (hidden state, step, BPTT) the diffusion sampling loop reuses without re-explaining them. Two failures, cleanly separated: one is a learning problem, one is a hardware problem, and only one of them killed RNNs.

🔮 Predict first Why did transformers beat RNNs — because attention is (a) smarter, more expressive; (b) cheaper per token; or (c) parallelizable across the sequence? Commit to one before you scroll.

Reading one token at a time, carrying a running summary

An RNN reads the way you read: one token at a time, updating a running summary as it goes. At every step it folds the new input into a fixed-size hidden state — a vector that is, by construction, the network's entire memory of everything that came before. That compression is both the elegance and, as this page derives, the death sentence: everything the network will ever know about token 1 has to survive being squeezed through the same fixed-size bottleneck at every step between then and now.

Weights are shared across every step — the same update rule applies whether you're at position 3 or position 3,000. That sharing is the RNN's installed inductive bias: time-invariance, a bet that "the rule for updating your summary shouldn't depend on how far into the sequence you are." (Recall page 25's table: every architecture is a bet like this.)

Failure 1 — vanishing and exploding gradients (a learning problem)

[THREAD:chain-rule — the verb, reopened from pages 5, 14, 15] Backprop through a recurrence is called BPTT (backpropagation through time), and it is nothing new — it is the exact same chain rule you hand- derived on TN-1, applied $S{-}1$ times in a row because the recurrence makes every hidden state depend on the one before it:

$$\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\mathbf h_1}=\frac{\partial\mathcal L}{\partial\mathbf h_S}\prod_{i=2}^{S}\frac{\partial\mathbf h_i}{\partial\mathbf h_{i-1}}, \qquad \frac{\partial\mathbf h_i}{\partial\mathbf h_{i-1}}=\operatorname{diag}\!\left(1-\mathbf h_i^2\right)W_{hh}^\top$$

✍️ Worked example — the product decays, by default

Collapse the Jacobian product to a single scalar decay rate $\gamma$ per step (the largest eigenvalue of $\operatorname{diag}(1-\mathbf h_i^2)W_{hh}^\top$, in spirit) and track $\gamma^{S-1}$, the fraction of the step-$S$ gradient that survives to reach step 1.

$\gamma=0.9$, $S=100$: $0.9^{99}=\mathbf{2.951\times10^{-5}}$ — under 0.003% of the gradient at step 100 survives to reach step 1.

$\gamma=0.9$, $S=1000$: $0.9^{999}\approx\mathbf{1.94\times10^{-46}}$ — below bf16's smallest normal number, $2^{-126}\approx1.18\times10^{-38}$ (bf16 shares fp32's 8-bit exponent field, constants §9.7). That gradient doesn't just get small. It underflows to exactly zero and stops updating anything upstream of it.

$\gamma=1.1$, $S=100$: $1.1^{99}\approx\mathbf{1.25\times10^{4}}$ — explodes instead. Exploding gradients are fixable (clip the norm); vanishing gradients are not, because clipping a number that's already zero does nothing.

And $\tanh'(z)=1-\tanh^2(z)\le1$ always — the diagonal factor in every Jacobian can only shrink, never grow, a norm-1 matrix at best. The product is structurally biased toward decay: vanishing is the default outcome, not an unlucky accident.

LSTMs and GRUs fix this with a near-additive cell-state path whose local Jacobian is close to $\operatorname{diag}(\mathbf f_i)$ with the forget gate $\mathbf f_i\to1$ — a gradient highway that bypasses the $\tanh$ squashing almost entirely. That is the same idea as a residual connection (page 34's $I$-term): an additive path the gradient can ride at norm $\approx1$ instead of a multiplicative path that decays it every step. Same problem, same fix, two different decades.

Failure 2 — the sequential dependency (this is what actually killed RNNs)

LSTMs mostly solved Failure 1. RNNs still died. Here's why: computing $\mathbf h_i$ needs $\mathbf h_{i-1}$ — not approximately, not usually, structurally. $S$ steps, each waiting on the last, none overlappable. A GPU with thousands of cores sits there running one step at a time.

✍️ Worked example — same FLOPs, radically different traffic

Take a hidden width $d_h=4096$ (Qwen3-8B's residual width, constants §4.2 — a realistic scale) and a sequence $S=2048$, weights in bf16 (2 bytes/param).

The RNN path. Each step is one matrix–vector product $W_{hh}\mathbf h_{i-1}$: $$\text{FLOPs/step}=2d_h^2=2\times4096^2=\mathbf{33.55\ MFLOP},\qquad \text{bytes read/step}=d_h^2\times2=\mathbf{33.55\ MB}$$ $$I_{\text{RNN}}=\frac{33.55\text{ MFLOP}}{33.55\text{ MB}}=\mathbf{1.0\ FLOP/byte}$$ Every step is a separate kernel launch that must wait for the previous step's output, so the full $33.55$ MB weight matrix is re-read from memory 2,048 times — once per step, because nothing can be batched across a dependency chain.

The transformer path. The same total work, done as one matmul over all $S{=}2048$ positions at once — $(2048{\times}4096)\times(4096{\times}4096)$: $$\text{FLOPs}=2\times2048\times4096^2=2{,}048\times33.55\text{ MFLOP}=\mathbf{6.87\times10^{10}\ FLOP}$$ — exactly $2{,}048\times$ the RNN's per-step FLOPs, because it's doing $2{,}048$ token-updates worth of arithmetic. But the weight matrix is read once and reused for every row: $$I_{\text{xform}}=\frac{6.87\times10^{10}\text{ FLOP}}{33.55\text{ MB}}=\mathbf{2{,}048\ FLOP/byte}$$

Identical total FLOPs. $2{,}048\times$ the arithmetic intensity — because the transformer reuses one weight-memory fetch across every position instead of paying for it $S$ times. This is exactly constants.md §6.5's formula, $I\approx S_{\text{fwd}}$ FLOP/byte at bf16, landing on its own frozen prefill row ($S{=}2048\Rightarrow I{=}2048$).

🔑 The sentence to keep RNNs didn't lose because they couldn't represent language. They lost because a matrix–vector product wastes a GPU and a matrix–matrix product doesn't. The transformer traded a sequential $O(S)$ dependency for a parallel $O(S^2)$ one — and paying $S^2$ FLOPs you can spend beats paying $S$ FLOPs you can't.

This resolves the PREDICT above: the answer is (c) parallelizable — and it's dumber (a much weaker inductive bias than the RNN's time-invariant update, page 25's table) and more expensive ($O(S^2)$ vs $O(S)$) per token. It won anyway, because $S^2$ FLOPs a GPU can actually spend beat $S$ FLOPs a GPU has to idle through. [THREAD:memory-budget — the reality check, seed] — arithmetic intensity is the tool that will price the KV cache (page 37), the parameter ledger (page 36), and the roofline (page 43); this is its first appearance.

🔬 Deep dive: recurrence came back in 2026 — because Failure 2, specifically, got fixed

Recurrent and linear-attention architectures are back, hybridized with regular attention — reported examples in the 2026 architecture-survey literature (not a constants.md figure; label it as such) include Gated-DeltaNet/gated-attention hybrids at roughly a 3:1 linear-to-full-attention ratio, and mixed Lightning-Attention/MLA designs at frontier scale. What changed isn't the math of Failure 1 — it's that the training-time sequential dependency was solved via an associative-scan / chunked-matmul reformulation of the recurrence, which parallelizes across chunks the way attention does, while keeping the $O(1)$-per-token inference memory a plain RNN always had (no growing KV cache).

The lesson generalizes past this one architecture fad: the sequential training dependency was the specific thing that killed RNNs — not vanishing gradients (LSTMs fixed that in the '90s and RNNs still died a decade later), not representational weakness. Remove the training-time dependency and recurrence is viable again. That said, plain full attention is still the default choice for most frontier models as of this writing — nobody has declared victory for hybrids either way; treat this as an honest "still moving" caveat, not a verdict.

Three ways this goes wrong in your head

⚠️ "LSTMs solved long-range dependencies." LSTMs solved the gradient problem (Failure 1). They did nothing about the fixed-size hidden-state bottleneck (everything before step $i$ is still squeezed through one vector of width $d_h$) or the sequential dependency (Failure 2). Two of the three problems remained — and Failure 2 is the one that actually ended the architecture's dominance.
⚠️ "Transformers won because attention is smarter." Attention's inductive bias is weaker than an RNN's time-invariant update (page 25), and it's more expensive per token — $O(S^2)$ against the RNN's $O(S)$. It won on parallelism, not cleverness: 1 FLOP/byte forced through a sequential bottleneck versus 2,048 FLOP/byte a GPU can actually chew through at once.
⚠️ "RNNs are just obsolete now." See the deep dive above — 2026 hybrids resurrect recurrence specifically because the training-time parallelism problem, and only that problem, got solved. The idea wasn't wrong; the hardware fit was.
