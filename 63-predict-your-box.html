The roofline, as the one formula

You built this on p.43. For a dense bf16 transformer, one forward reads every weight once ($2N$ bytes) and does $2N$ FLOP per token, so the arithmetic intensity is just the token count:

📐 Arithmetic intensity is tokens per forward — and the ridge $$I = \frac{2N\,S_{\text{fwd}}}{2N} = \boxed{\,S_{\text{fwd}}\ \text{FLOP/byte}\,}, \qquad I^\star = \frac{P_{\text{peak}}}{BW}.$$ The ridge is INF and genuinely unresolved, because the dense-BF16 ceiling is unpublished (constants §6.4): $I^\star = 62\text{e}12/273\text{e}9 = \mathbf{227}$ (working, FP32-accumulate) or $125\text{e}12/273\text{e}9 = \mathbf{458}$ (if the ceiling is ~125 TF, FP16-accumulate). The verdicts below hold under both. [THREAD:dot-product beat FINAL] — $I$ comes from $2N$ FLOP over $2N$ bytes: one multiply-add per weight, the dot product at hardware scale, the same atom from p.3.

Same machine, opposite verdicts

Plug each track's $S_{\text{fwd}}$ into $I \approx S_{\text{fwd}}$. They land on opposite sides of the ridge — which is the entire reason this one box feels weak for chat and strong for images:

◆ LLM decode, batch 1 → the Spark is weak $S_{\text{fwd}} = 1 \Rightarrow I = 1$, which is 227–458× below the ridge → bandwidth-bound. The tensor cores sit idle waiting on DRAM; you are limited by how fast the box can read the weights, not multiply them. Quantizing helps (it cuts the bytes), adding compute does not.
◇ Diffusion sampling, 4096 latent tokens → the Spark is strong $S_{\text{fwd}} = 4096 \Rightarrow I = 4096$, which is 9–18× above the ridge → compute-bound. One weight read is amortized over 4096 tokens of the latent grid; the cores actually work. Same machine, same ridge, opposite side of $I^\star$.
✍️ And it is measured, on his box SGLang, Llama-3.1-8B FP8, LMSYS VP (constants §6.7): batching decode $1 \to 32$ takes it from 20.5 → 368 tok/s (18×), while prefill stays flat: 7,991 → 7,949. Prefill was already compute-bound and gained nothing from more batch; decode was bandwidth-bound and gained ~linearly. That is $I \approx S_{\text{fwd}}$, measured, on the machine you own.

What the course still refuses to hand you

⚠️ Two numbers you are not allowed to trust from the course The ridge is a range and not a fact because two inputs are unresolved, and a course that printed them would be lying:
  • The dense BF16 ceiling — 62 TF (FP32-accumulate) vs 125 TF (FP16-accumulate). NVIDIA publishes neither. The SDXL-BF16 datapoint (69.7 TF/s if CFG was on) leans 125; community GEMM reports lean 62 INF (constants §6.3).
  • Achieved memory bandwidth — 273 GB/s is the theoretical peak VP; the fraction you actually get is MEA.
You measure both with 09_spark_capability_probe.py, and the measurement fixes the ridge. This is not a loose end to hide — it is the last lesson: measurement outranks assertion.
🔬 Deep dive: why 0.65 and not 1.0 — the decode heuristic's honest error bars

The batch-1 decode rate for a dense model has a one-line predictor (constants §6.6):

$$\text{decode tok/s} \approx 0.65 \times \frac{BW\ (\text{GB/s})}{\text{weight bytes read per token (GB)}}.$$

The 0.65 is the round, slightly-conservative memory-bandwidth-utilization coefficient (mean MBU across four dense measurements is 0.69; error bars ±10–15%). The subtle part is the denominator: weight bytes read per token is the checkpoint file minus the embedding table — embed_tokens is gathered (one row per token), not streamed. Get that wrong and the prediction moves 15–25 pp.

The punchline, verbatim: if your heuristic predicts more than 100% of peak bandwidth, your model is an MoE. gpt-oss-20B decodes at 49.7 tok/s, which on its 12.8 GB checkpoint implies 637 GB/s = 234% of the 273 GB/s bus — impossible — because only ~3.6 B of its ~20.9 B params are active per token.

For Qwen3-8B in bf16 (weights 16.38 GB), the roofline ceiling is $273/16.38 = \mathbf{16.67}$ tok/s, and the realistic expectation is $0.65 \times 16.67 \approx \mathbf{10.8}$ tok/s. Say which is which — the ceiling is what the bus allows, the ~10.8 is what you will see. (The old "~19.5 tok/s" figure is retired; it was a 7B/14 GB ceiling for a retired anchor.) The roofline verdict above does not depend on any of this, which is why it lives in a collapsible.

Measure the three numbers yourself

Try it — code/predict_your_box.py This is the console, on your box, against your silicon. It reads memory facts (no GPU), computes 122.05 GiB against your own total, then times a big bf16 GEMM against both ceilings in both accumulate modes. It installs nothing and must run in a fresh venv — never ComfyUI's (hardware-ground-truth §3).
Python · code/predict_your_box.py
import torch
GiB = 2 ** 30

# Step 1 — the wall. What does your box ACTUALLY have?
free, total = torch.cuda.mem_get_info()          # bytes
print(f"usable now: {free/GiB:.2f} GiB of {total/GiB:.2f} GiB")

# Step 2 — the ledger, against YOUR total (not 128, not the spec sheet).
P = 8_190_735_360                                # Qwen3-8B, exact (p.36)
need_gib = P * 16 / GiB                          # 122.05 GiB, full AdamW
slack = total / GiB - need_gib
print(f"full FT needs {need_gib:.2f} GiB -> slack {slack:+.2f} GiB "
      f"({'FITS' if slack > 0 else 'OOM, before activations'})")

# Step 3 — the ceiling + the ridge. A big bf16 GEMM, both accumulate modes.
n = 8192
a = torch.randn(n, n, device="cuda", dtype=torch.bfloat16)
b = torch.randn(n, n, device="cuda", dtype=torch.bfloat16)
for allow_fp16_acc in (False, True):
    torch.backends.cuda.matmul.allow_bf16_reduced_precision_reduction = allow_fp16_acc
    torch.cuda.synchronize(); t0 = torch.cuda.Event(enable_timing=True)
    t1 = torch.cuda.Event(enable_timing=True); t0.record()
    for _ in range(50): c = a @ b
    t1.record(); torch.cuda.synchronize()
    secs = t0.elapsed_time(t1) / 1e3 / 50
    tf = 2 * n ** 3 / secs / 1e12            # YOUR ceiling: 62-ish, or 125-ish?
    ridge = tf * 1e12 / (273e9)                # YOUR ridge: 227, or 458?
    print(f"{'fp16' if allow_fp16_acc else 'fp32'}-acc: "
          f"{tf:6.1f} TF/s  ->  ridge {ridge:5.0f} FLOP/byte")

You just measured the wall, the ceiling, and the ridge — the three numbers the course refused to hand you (constants §10, MEA list). Whichever ceiling your box lands on, understanding why teaches accumulate precision better than any paragraph.
