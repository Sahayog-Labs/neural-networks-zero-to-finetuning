The Denoiser's Body I: the U-Net, in Depth

For five pages the network was a black box called $\epsilon_\theta$ — we knew its job (predict the noise) but never opened it. Here we open it. Its whole silhouette is forced by one shape constraint, its famous skips carry information rather than gradients, and its cross-attention matrix is — literally — your prompt, laid over the picture.

🔮 Predict first Take a working U-Net and cut its skip connections — the wires that jump straight from the down-path to the matching level of the up-path. Does the crippled network (a) train more slowly but eventually reach the same quality, or (b) produce permanently blurry output no matter how long you train? Commit to one before the ablation demo below.

The job is a shape constraint

You already know what this network is for: pages 54–57 handed it a noisy latent and a timestep and asked it to name the noise (or the velocity — same triangle, page 55). What we never wrote down is the network's type signature, and the type signature is what forces the architecture:

$$\epsilon_\theta:\ \underbrace{\mathbb{R}^{C\times H_{\text{lat}}\times W_{\text{lat}}}}_{\text{noisy latent}} \times \underbrace{\mathbb{R}}_{\text{noise level }t} \times \underbrace{\mathbb{R}^{S_{\text{txt}}\times d_c}}_{\text{text}} \ \longrightarrow\ \underbrace{\mathbb{R}^{C\times H_{\text{lat}}\times W_{\text{lat}}}}_{\text{predicted noise}}$$

The U-Net silhouette

A dense predictor has a problem: to decide what a pixel should be, you need global context (is this region sky or water?) and local detail (where exactly is this edge?). Convolutions see locally and cheaply; to see globally you must shrink the map so a fixed kernel covers more ground. So the U-Net does both in sequence — squeeze down to a tiny, wide bottleneck to reason about what, then expand back up to full resolution to place where. The classic SD1.5 body:

✍️ SD1.5 U-Net levels (512² image → 64² latent) [EST] — read your own unet/config.json to confirm the multipliers
pathspatialchannelsblock
in64²320ResBlock ×2 + Transformer2D

down 216²1280ResBlock + Transformer2D

up 216²1280 → (concat)ResBlock + Transformer2D
up 132²640 → (concat)ResBlock + Transformer2D
out64²320 → (concat)ResBlock ×3, then conv → 4 ch
Spatial goes 64² → 8² → 64², channels 320 → 1280 → 320 — the "U". The up-path convolutions take double the input channels you might expect, because each level concatenates the matching down-path activation before convolving. That concat is the skip, and it is the whole story.

★ The skips carry information, not gradients

Here is the explanation almost every tutorial gets wrong. At the $8\times8$ bottleneck, each of the 64 activations summarizes an $8\times8$ patch of latent — a $64\times64$-pixel patch of image at the VAE's $f=8$. That activation genuinely knows "there is a face in the upper-left" — that is the what. But it cannot possibly know where each eyelash goes: that information was averaged away three downsamples ago and is physically not in the tensor anymore. No amount of clever up-path convolution can invent it back from an $8\times8$ summary.

🔑 The skip is a wire carrying information that would otherwise not exist Down-path decides what. Up-path decides where. The skip hands the up-path the original high-resolution activation from before the bottleneck destroyed it — routing the fine detail around the squeeze instead of through it. It is not an optimization aid that makes training converge faster. It is the only path by which the where survives the trip. Sever it and the detail is gone for good — not slow to learn, impossible to produce.
⚠️ Misconception: "U-Net skips help gradients, like ResNet" Different mechanism, and even a different operation. A ResNet residual is $\mathbf{h}_{\text{out}} = \mathbf{h}_{\text{in}} + F(\mathbf{h}_{\text{in}})$ — it adds, same shape in and out, and its job is to give gradients a short-circuit so deep stacks train (page 34). A U-Net skip concatenates a high-res down-path tensor onto a low-res up-path tensor at the matching level — that is why the up-path convs take double channels. One is a highway for gradients through depth; the other is a highway for spatial information around a bottleneck. Conflating them is the single most common U-Net error. So the predict answer is (b): a skip-free U-Net is permanently blurry — the ablation demo below severs skips one band at a time and you can watch the edges leave.

Where the timestep enters — as gain, not signal

The latent flows through the convolutions as the signal. The timestep $t$ does not join that stream as an extra channel; it enters as modulation (FiLM), rescaling and shifting each normalized feature map:

$$\mathbf{h}\ \leftarrow\ \mathrm{GroupNorm}(\mathbf{h})\odot\big(1+\gamma_{\text{film}}(t)\big)\ +\ \boldsymbol\beta_{\text{film}}(t)$$

$\mathbf{h}$a feature map inside a ResBlock$(C_\ell, H_\ell, W_\ell)$the conv stream
$\gamma_{\text{film}}(t)$per-channel scale (gain)$(C_\ell,)$a small MLP on the $t$-embedding
$\boldsymbol\beta_{\text{film}}(t)$per-channel shift$(C_\ell,)$the same MLP
$\odot$elementwise (broadcast over $H_\ell,W_\ell$)——

Translation. FiLM's scale/shift are conventionally named $\gamma,\beta$; the shift here is $\boldsymbol\beta_{\text{film}}(t)$, not the noise-schedule $\beta_t$ of page 54 — different object, same Greek letter, so we subscript it.

[THREAD: Qwen3-8B · beat] Same object, new use. Before the MLP, $t$ is turned into a vector by a sinusoidal embedding — the exact same function as the LLM track's positional encoding (p.33), applied to a scalar noise level instead of a token position. Reopen p.33: sines and cosines at geometric frequencies. We do not re-derive it; we reuse it.

Why gain and not an extra input channel? Because $t$ is a global mode switch — it tells every block which job it is doing right now: at high $t$ (near pure noise) the network's job is coarse composition; at low $t$ it is fine texture. A mode switch that reconfigures how a whole layer behaves belongs in the layer's gain, applied identically across every spatial position — not smuggled in as one more pixel-like signal the convolution has to disentangle.

Self- and cross-attention, told apart by their shapes

Each Transformer2D block does two attentions, and you already own attention mask-agnostically from the trunk (p.29–31). The only new thing here is what plays $Q$, $K$, $V$ — and that is entirely a statement about shapes. Flatten the feature map $[B,C,H,W]\to[B,HW,C]$ so each spatial location is one "image token":

h (B, C=640, 32, 32) # SD1.5 down-1 feature map flatten (B, 1024, 640) # HW = 32×32 = 1024 image tokens ── self-attention: "let distant pixels talk" ── Q, K, V all from h (B, 1024, 640) A = softmax(QKᵀ / √d_head) (B, 1024, 1024) # image ↔ image, cost O((HW)²) ── cross-attention: "inject the prompt" ── Q from h (B, 1024, 640) K, V from c (text) (B, 77, 640) # S_txt = 77 CLIP tokens A = softmax(QKᵀ / √d_head) (B, 1024, 77) # image ↔ prompt, cost O(HW·S_txt)
🔑 The $[1024\times77]$ matrix is your prompt, laid over the picture Read that cross-attention matrix literally. It has 1024 rows, one per image patch, and 77 columns, one per CLIP prompt token. Entry $(i,j)$ answers: how much does patch $i$ look at prompt token $j$? The softmax is over the last axis (the 77 tokens), so each row sums to 1; columns do not (page 30's rule — learners read heatmaps backwards constantly). The 77 columns are your prompt. Take column $j$ for the word "dog," reshape its 1024 entries back to $32\times32$, and you have a heatmap of where the model is drawing the dog. This one matrix is the entire mechanism of text-to-image; everything else in the block is plumbing — and it is exactly what the attention-map nodes in your ComfyUI graph visualize.

[THREAD: The dot product · beat] Every entry of that matrix is a dot product — $\langle\mathbf{q}_i,\mathbf{k}_j\rangle/\sqrt{d_{\text{head}}}$, patch-query against token-key — the same atom from p.3, scaled the same way as p.30. Cross-attention is just: let the keys come from somewhere else. Reopen p.31 (self vs cross): the only difference between the two attentions above is where $K,V$ are read from.
