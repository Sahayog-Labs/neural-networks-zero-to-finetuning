Flow Matching & Rectified Flow: Your FLUX Scheduler, Decoded

DDPM was the arc; rectified flow is the chord. One route change makes the sampler a single subtraction, kills the terminal-SNR bug structurally, and explains why plain euler is all your FLUX needs. Then you open your own scheduler_config.json and every field has a name.

🔮 Predict first You know this from your own bench runs: SD1.5 needs dpmpp_2m (or worse) to look clean at 20 steps, but FLUX looks fine on plain euler at the same step count. Same sampler budget, different sampler requirement. Before the page tells you — guess why. Is FLUX (a) just a bigger model that forgives sloppy integration, (b) using a fundamentally different loss, or (c) trained on a path that a first-order solver can integrate almost exactly?

The construction: a straight line from data to noise

Every route we have drawn so far — DDPM, cosine, the score SDE — lived on the circle $a_t^2+b_t^2=1$. Flow matching asks a blunt question: why an arc at all? Connect $\mathbf{x}_0$ (data) to $\boldsymbol\epsilon$ (noise) with the shortest path there is — a straight line — and travel it at constant speed:

🔑 The rectified-flow path and its velocity With the SD3/FLUX time convention $t=0$ is data, $t=1$ is noise: $$\mathbf{x}_t = (1-t)\,\mathbf{x}_0 + t\,\boldsymbol\epsilon,\qquad \mathbf{u}_t = \frac{d\mathbf{x}_t}{dt} = \boldsymbol\epsilon - \mathbf{x}_0.$$ The velocity is constant along the whole path — it does not depend on $t$. Train a network to regress it: $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{FM}} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}_0,\,\boldsymbol\epsilon,\,t} \big\|\,v_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t) - (\boldsymbol\epsilon - \mathbf{x}_0)\,\big\|_2^2.$$

Now the payoff that the whole reverse-process machinery of p.55 was fighting for. Sampling is Euler integration of the ODE $d\mathbf{x}/dt = v_\theta$, run backward from noise ($t=1$) to data ($t=0$):

📐 The flow-matching sampler — one line $$\mathbf{x}_{t-\Delta} = \mathbf{x}_t - \Delta\,v_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t).$$ No $\bar\alpha_t$. No posterior mean $\tilde\mu_t$. No re-injected $\sigma_t\mathbf{z}$. Compare it to the ancestral sampler on p.55 — half a screen of variances and a random kick per step — and then to this: subtract a step of the predicted velocity. That structural simplicity is most of why the 2026 frontier switched.
⚠️ Translation: "$t=0$ data, $t=1$ noise" is not "opposite to DDPM" A common claim says SD3/FLUX reversed DDPM's time. It did not — DDPM also puts data at $t=0$ and noise at the large-$t$ end ($t=T=1000$). Nothing is flipped. Two things are genuinely confusing and worth naming so they never trip you: (a) Lipman's original flow-matching paper puts noise at $t=0$ — the opposite of the diffusers convention this course uses; and (b) the diffusers sigmas array runs $1\!\to\!0$ during sampling, so the number you watch tick down is $\sigma\equiv t$, not a DDPM timestep. This course holds one convention — $t=0$ data, $t=1$ noise, sampling runs $t$ down — and translates everything else (notation §6).

[THREAD: The chain rule · beat] Same verb, newest object. The target $\mathbf{u}_t=\boldsymbol\epsilon-\mathbf{x}_0$ is literally $d\mathbf{x}_t/dt$ — a derivative along the path, the twin of p.55's $v=d\mathbf{x}_t/d\psi_t$ (a derivative along the arc). Velocity is a chain-rule object either way; flow matching just chooses the straight parameterization.

Why a straight path still is not one step

Here is the trap that catches almost everyone who hears "rectified flow is straight."

⚠️ Misconception: "the paths are straight lines, so one Euler step must reach the data" The training paths are straight — each $(\mathbf{x}_0,\boldsymbol\epsilon)$ pair rides a line. But $v_\theta$ never sees the pair; it sees only $\mathbf{x}_t$. Many different $(\mathbf{x}_0,\boldsymbol\epsilon)$ pairs pass through the same $\mathbf{x}_t$, so the least-squares solution is the conditional average $$v_\theta^\star(\mathbf{x}_t,t) = \mathbb{E}\big[\boldsymbol\epsilon - \mathbf{x}_0 \,\big|\, \mathbf{x}_t\big].$$ And averaging a bundle of crossing straight lines gives a curved field. So "rectified flow is straight" is half true: the marginal field the network actually learns is curved, which is exactly why base FM still needs several Euler steps — see the two-panel demo, and drag $N$ down to watch the curvature bite.
🔬 Deep dive: Reflow — how the field gets straightened for real (schnell / turbo / klein)

If crossing lines are the problem, Reflow is the fix: sample many $\mathbf{x}_1\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$, integrate the trained model down to $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0$, then re-couple each noise draw to the data point it actually produced and retrain on those straight, non-crossing pairs. After one or two rounds the marginal field is nearly straight, and now one or a few Euler steps really do land — which is the ancestry of every few-step model on your disk (z_image_turbo, FLUX schnell/klein, the "turbo" LoRAs). Distillation (progressive, consistency, LADD) is the other route to the same end; both are covered as production choices on p.59. The base FLUX.2-dev you fine-tune, however, is not reflowed — its field is curved, so it wants ~20 steps.

★ The reconciliation: there is no third theory

Every path in this track — the DDPM arc of p.54, the cosine schedule, the VE/Karras sigmas of p.56, and now rectified flow — is a single object with two knobs. Write the general path and everything becomes a table, not a set of rival theories:

$$\mathbf{x}_t = a_t\,\mathbf{x}_0 + b_t\,\boldsymbol\epsilon.$$

DDPM is the arc; rectified flow is the chord. Of course the chord integrates in one Euler step better than an arc — a straight route has no curvature to miss — and of course it hits $a_1=0$ exactly: no residual signal leaks to the endpoint, so the terminal-SNR bug of p.54 is gone structurally, not patched with noise_offset. ($a_T\approx0.0063$ is the same frozen leak [DER, constants.md §9.6] you met on p.54.)
🔑 $v$-prediction is flow matching's velocity — on the circle instead of the chord Put the general velocity on the circular path $a_t=\cos\psi_t,\ b_t=\sin\psi_t$ and differentiate with respect to the angle: you recover $v = \cos\psi_t\,\boldsymbol\epsilon - \sin\psi_t\,\mathbf{x}_0$ — exactly p.55's $v$-prediction target. So $v$-pred was never a DDPM-only trick; it is flow matching's velocity evaluated on the arc. Rectified flow makes the same move on the straight chord, where the conversions have no square roots and no small division: $$\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0 = \mathbf{x}_t - t\,\hat v,\qquad \hat{\boldsymbol\epsilon} = \mathbf{x}_t + (1-t)\,\hat v.$$ Substitute $\mathbf{x}_t=(1-t)\mathbf{x}_0+t\boldsymbol\epsilon$ and $\hat v=\boldsymbol\epsilon-\mathbf{x}_0$ to check both collapse to identities — no $1/\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}$ pole anywhere.
📐 The summary you keep DDPM and flow matching differ in exactly three places, and nowhere else: (1) the route $(a_t,b_t)$ — arc vs chord; (2) which coordinate of the rigid triangle the network reports — $\epsilon$, $\mathbf{x}_0$, or $v$; (3) the loss weighting across $t$. Same endpoints, same rigid triangle, same MSE. There is no third theory.

What your FLUX actually does — open the config

Two design choices turn the clean chord above into the production scheduler in your ~/…/FLUX.2-dev/scheduler/scheduler_config.json. Both are answers to "which timesteps deserve the training budget?"

1. Logit-normal timestep sampling. Uniform $t$ wastes samples at the easy ends. FLUX draws $z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and passes it through the logistic squash $t=\text{logistic}(z)=1/(1+e^{-z})$, which piles the mass near $t=0.5$ — the $\mathrm{SNR}\approx1$ band where composition is decided (p.54, observation 3).

✍️ Worked number: two-thirds of every batch lands in the hard band The logistic map sends the interval $z\in(-1,1)$ to $t\in(\text{logistic}(-1),\text{logistic}(1))=(0.269,0.731)$. A standard normal puts $$P(-1 < z < 1) = \Phi(1)-\Phi(-1) = 0.8413 - 0.1587 = \mathbf{0.6827} \approx 68\%$$ of its mass in $(-1,1)$ [DER]. So ≈68% of every minibatch has $t\in(0.27,0.73)$ — two-thirds of the compute spent squarely on the composition band. (Uniform sampling would put that same 68% over the much wider, mostly-easy $[0.16,0.84]$.) The histogram demo below lands on this number live.

2. The shift warp. Higher resolutions under-noise at a fixed $t$ (p.54's resolution bug: more pixels average the per-pixel noise away). The fix is one scalar that pushes the schedule toward the noisy end:

$$t' = \frac{\text{shift}\cdot t}{1 + (\text{shift}-1)\,t}.$$

⚠️ num_train_timesteps=1000 is a fossil, not a chain length Flow matching is continuous in $t\in[0,1]$ — there is no 1000-step Markov chain here. The 1000 is indexing bookkeeping inherited from DDPM tooling: it only quantizes $t$ into 1000 buckets for code that expects integer timesteps. Reading it as "FLUX denoises in 1000 steps" is the mistake; you sample FLUX in ~20. [VP: it is a real default in FlowMatchEulerDiscreteScheduler, notation §6.]

So the predict answer is (c). FLUX is not forgiving because it is big, and it is not using a different loss — it is trained on a straighter path, and a first-order solver like euler is nearly exact on straight lines. SD1.5's DDPM arc has curvature that a plain Euler step overshoots, so it needs a higher-order solver (dpmpp_2m) to fake the missing curvature. Same step budget; the route, not the solver, was the difference.

⚠️ Genuinely unsettled: is flow matching better, or better-packaged? Honest answer — contested, and you should hold both camps. The "same thing" camp: FM is a reparameterization of the same Gaussian-path family (the table above), so any win is cleaner defaults and simpler code, not new capability. The "genuinely helps" camp: the chord gives zero terminal SNR for free, scales across resolution with one shift scalar, is first-order-solvable, and distills readily — and the entire 2026 frontier chose it. What is not in dispute: FM's code is dramatically simpler and its results are at least as good. The field largely stopped arguing because the practical answer is settled even while the theoretical one is not.
