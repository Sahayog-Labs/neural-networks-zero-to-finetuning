Latent Diffusion & the Roofline: Why It Runs on Your Desk

The VAE of page 53 was not a convenience — it moved diffusion off a cliff. We compute the exact size of that cliff (65,536×), then place diffusion on the one formula that decides whether your Spark is strong or weak. Same machine, same ridge, opposite verdicts from the LLM track — and one number you will only ever get by measuring.

🔮 Predict first Pixel-space self-attention at $1024^2$, one head, one layer, fp16: how many bytes is the attention matrix on its own? Order of magnitude only — and remember you have a 128 GB box. Commit to a power of ten before you scroll.

Do the pixel-space arithmetic first

Attention builds an $S\times S$ score matrix, where $S$ is the number of tokens it attends over. That $S^2$ is the whole story: it is the dot product atom of Part III, one entry per query–key pair, and it is quadratic in the token count. So the only question that matters for cost is: how many tokens?

✍️ Pixel vs latent, at $1024^2$ — [DER, constants.md §9.6] Pixel space ($f=1$, no patchify): $S_{px} = 1024^2 = 1{,}048{,}576$ tokens. $$S_{px}^2 = (1024^2)^2 = 1.10\times10^{12}\ \text{entries}\ \times\ 2\,\text{B} = \mathbf{2.2\ TB}$$ Per head. Per layer. Per step. Before you multiply by 24 heads, 57 blocks, and 28 steps. That is the answer to the predict: $\sim10^{12}$ bytes — 2.2 TB — which overflows your 128 GB box by 17× on a single head.

Latent space (FLUX, $f=8$, then $2\times2$ patchify): $\tfrac{1024}{8} = 128$, then $\tfrac{128}{2} = 64$ per side, so $S_{lat} = 64^2 = 4096$ tokens. $$S_{lat}^2 = 4096^2 = 1.68\times10^7\ \times\ 2\,\text{B} = \mathbf{33.5\ MB}$$ Fits in cache. The ratio is the whole reason the field exists: $$\frac{S_{px}^2}{S_{lat}^2} = \frac{1.10\times10^{12}}{1.68\times10^7} = \boxed{\ \mathbf{65{,}536\times} = 16^4\ }$$ 65,536× reduction in the expensive operation, bought with a 12× lossy compression (p.53). That trade is why Stable Diffusion exists, and why your Spark runs FLUX at all.

The $16^4$ is not a coincidence: $f=8$ downsamples each side by 8, and $2\times2$ patchify by a further 2, so the side shrinks by 16, area by $16^2$, and the attention matrix — quadratic in area — by $16^4$. Historically, Imagen and DALL·E 2 diffused directly in pixel space and had to stack three-model cascades (64² → 256² → 1024²) to dodge exactly this quadratic wall. The cascade was the workaround for not having a VAE.

[THREAD: The dot product · beat] Same atom, new bill. Attention's $S^2$ is $S^2$ dot products; latent diffusion is the trick of making $S$ small before you pay for them. The atom never changed — only how many of them you can afford.

The model is the mass

Latent diffusion fixed the compute wall. It did almost nothing for the memory wall, because in diffusion the memory is the weights, not the state you are generating. Read the inference footprints of the models on your disk:

The thing you are generating is half a megabyte; on FLUX.2 the weights are ~114 GB — roughly 200,000× larger. Diffusion inference is streaming ~114 GB of weights past a half-megabyte of state, once per step, 28 times. The state is free; the weights are the machine.

Four mitigations you should know cold, because they are the difference between FLUX.2 fitting and not:

  • Precompute text embeddings — run the Mistral-3 24B encoder once, cache its output, then never load it during the diffusion loop. −48 GB on FLUX.2, the single biggest lever.
  • enable_model_cpu_offload() — stream weights from CPU RAM. On a normal GPU this pays a PCIe copy tax; on the Spark the memory is unified and coherent, so the "copy" is near-free. This is a genuine Spark advantage, not a consolation.
  • FP8 — roughly halves the weight bytes (−50%).
  • NVFP4 — native 4-bit tensor cores; this is what the marketing "1000 TOPS" number is measured in (p.41).

★ The roofline — one formula, opposite verdicts

You now have the two facts the roofline needs: diffusion does a lot of arithmetic (compute) on a small state that streams a lot of weights (bandwidth). Which one is the bottleneck on your box? The roofline model answers it with a single ratio, arithmetic intensity — FLOPs done per byte moved. The trunk derived it (p.43); we only plug in. For a dense transformer at bf16, one forward pass moves its $2N$ weight-bytes and does $2N$ FLOPs per token, so over $S_{\text{fwd}}$ tokens:

🔑 Arithmetic intensity = tokens per forward $$I \;=\; \frac{\text{FLOPs}}{\text{bytes}} \;=\; \frac{2N\cdot S_{\text{fwd}}}{2N} \;=\; \boxed{\,S_{\text{fwd}}\ \text{FLOP/byte}\,}$$ The bytes are the weights (read once); the FLOPs scale with how many tokens you push through that one read. So intensity is just tokens per forward pass. A workload is compute-bound when $I$ is above the machine's ridge $I^\star = P_{\text{peak}}/BW$, bandwidth-bound when below.

The ridge needs the Spark's peak BF16 compute — and here honesty costs us a clean number. NVIDIA publishes no dense BF16 figure for GB10. The value is inferred, not published [INF, constants.md §6.3], and it is genuinely unresolved between two candidates:

⚠️ The ridge is [INF] and the ceiling is unsettled — 62 vs 125 TF Inferring down from the "1000 TOPS" FP4-sparse marketing peak by halving for sparsity, then FP4→FP8→BF16, lands at ~125 TF (FP16-accumulate) or, with the consumer-Blackwell FP32-accumulate penalty, ~62 TF — which is what a plain PyTorch training matmul actually uses. Community GEMM reports (~60 TF) lean 62; an SDXL BF16 benchmark (≈70 TF/s sustained if CFG was on) leans 125. So the ridge is: $$I^\star = \frac{62\times10^{12}}{273\times10^{9}} = \mathbf{227}\quad\text{(working)}\qquad\text{or}\qquad \frac{125\times10^{12}}{273\times10^{9}} = \mathbf{458}\quad\text{(if the ceiling is }{\sim}125\text{ TF).}$$ We print 227 as the working value with its inference label, name 458 as the live alternative, and print neither as fact. Your bench_spark.py GEMM (below) settles it by measurement — that is exactly why the course measures the ridge rather than asserting it.

Here is the payoff, and it is the deliberate contrast this whole course was built toward. The verdict is robust to which ceiling is right, because the two tracks sit on opposite sides of the ridge under both:

Same machine, same ridge, opposite verdicts — because the two tracks sit on opposite sides of $I^\star$. Diffusion generates 4096 tokens per forward and is deep in compute-bound territory under both ceilings; LLM decode generates one token per forward and is hundreds-fold bandwidth-starved. The Spark — memory-rich, bandwidth-poor — is exactly the machine that is strong for diffusion and weak for single-stream LLM decode. That is not two stories; it is one formula read at two values of $S_{\text{fwd}}$.

⚠️ Correcting a common claim: bf16 FLUX is compute-bound, not bandwidth-bound A tempting-but-wrong reading says diffusion is limited by an 88 ms/step bandwidth floor. Check it. FLUX.1-dev is $N\approx12\text{e}9$; one step over 4096 tokens needs $$2\times12\text{e}9\times4096 \;=\; 9.83\text{e}13\ \text{FLOP}\ \div\ 62\text{e}12\ \text{FLOP/s} \;=\; \mathbf{1585\ ms/step}\ \text{(compute)}$$ — an order of magnitude above the ~88 ms it would take to merely stream the weights. Compute is the wall nobody is near on the bandwidth side. The step time is set by arithmetic, which is why the Spark's weak bandwidth does not hurt here.

The honest FLOP count — and a deleted showpiece

There is a famous number: "FLUX at FP4 does a 1K image in 2.6 seconds." An earlier draft of this course built a showpiece around it — "we predicted a published benchmark from first principles." That showpiece is deleted, because the number it used was mislabeled.

✍️ The 2.6 s number is Schnell/4, not dev/28 — [VP + DER, constants.md §9.6] The correct label: "FLUX.1-Schnell, 4 steps, $1024^2$, batch 1, FP4 = 23 img/min = 2.6 s/img" [VP]. Read it as FLUX.1-dev at 28 steps and the arithmetic becomes impossible: $$28\times2\times12\text{e}9\times4096 \;=\; 2.75\text{e}15\ \text{FLOP}\ \div\ 2.6\ \text{s} \;=\; \mathbf{1.06\ PFLOP/s}$$ That is 106% of the sparse-FP4 marketing peak, on a workload that uses neither FP4 sparsity nor the full model — physically impossible. The numerator (dev/28) and the measurement (Schnell/4) are unrelated quantities whose "agreement" was coincidence.

The honest Schnell/4 count, per step at $1024^2$ (4096 image + 512 text = 4608 tokens): linear/matmul $2NS = 1.097\text{e}14$; attention $4S^2 d\cdot57 = 1.487\text{e}13$ (11.9%); total $1.245\text{e}14$/step. Over 4 steps: $$4.98\times10^{14}\ \text{FLOP}\ \div\ 2.609\ \text{s} \;=\; \mathbf{191\ TF/s}$$ Against the inferred ~500 TF dense-FP4 ceiling that is 30–38% MFU — physically plausible. The lesson survives; the false precision does not. You cannot honestly predict a 2.6 s wall-clock when the FP4 dense ceiling is itself an unpublished ±2× inference. So you will measure your own.

⚠️ The budget is [MEA] — you measure it, the course never asserts it The comparison above uses your box's measured MemTotal = 121.6875 GiB [MEA-DEV] — not the 128 on the spec sheet. A firmware carveout eats 6.3125 GiB (4.9%) before you allocate a byte, and with ComfyUI already resident, torch.cuda.mem_get_info() reported only 19.41 GiB free at measurement time. Published capacity is never usable capacity. Do not trust this paragraph — run the one-liner in bench_spark.py and read your number.
🔑 Capacity, not speed The Spark's 273 GB/s is about 1/7th a 5090's ~1.8 TB/s, but it has ~4× the memory, unified. So for a job that fits in 32 GB — an SDXL or FLUX.1 LoRA — the 5090 is simply faster. The Spark wins only on the jobs the 5090 cannot start: the ones that need more than 32 GB to hold weights + state. You own the box; it is a capacity machine, not a speed machine. Buy it for what it does.
