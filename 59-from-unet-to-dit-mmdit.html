Part VI · Diffusion Track ◇ · The Denoiser, Part II

The Denoiser's Body II: DiT, MMDiT & the Samplers

The U-Net's hierarchy was a constraint that scale outgrew. Swap it for a transformer over patch tokens and two things change: quality tracks GFLOPs, and text can finally talk back to the image. Then the sampler dropdown you use daily turns out to be a menu of textbook ODE solvers — one of which is a genuine free lunch.

🔮 Predict first Two questions you can answer from experience, then check. (1) You run euler_a at 20 steps, then again at 100 steps, same seed, same prompt. Do you get the same image, only sharper, or a different image? (2) dpmpp_2m is a second-order method — it gets a more accurate step than plain Euler. How many model calls per step does it spend to buy that second order: 1 or 2? Commit to both before scrolling.

Two bodies for one job

Page 58 pinned the denoiser's job as a shape constraint, not an architecture: take a noised latent, a noise level, and the text, and return a tensor of the same shape as the latent. Dense prediction, not classification — the output is the thing you are generating, at full latent resolution.

eps_theta : (C, H_lat, W_lat) x R x (S_txt, d_c) -> (C, H_lat, W_lat) # noised latent noise level the prompt same shape back

Two network families satisfy that constraint, and you own checkpoints of both. The U-Net (SD1.5, SDXL) meets it with a spatial hierarchy and skip wires — page 58. The DiT (SD3.5, FLUX.1/2, Z-Image, Qwen-Image) throws the hierarchy out and meets it with a plain transformer over patch tokens. This page is the second body, and the sampler that walks it.

The DiT: patchify, transform, unpatchify

A transformer eats a sequence of tokens. A latent is a grid. The DiT bridges them with the exact move you already met on the VAE page — patchify — then runs $N$ ordinary transformer blocks, then puts the grid back:

x (latent) (B=1, C=16, H_lat=128, W_lat=128) # a FLUX.1 latent, from p.53 patchify 2x2 (B=1, 4096, 64) # (128/2)^2 = 4096 tokens; width 16*2*2 = 64 linear proj (B=1, 4096, 3072) # each token widened to d = 3072 + N x block (1, 4096, 3072) # attention + MLP, shape preserved every block unpatchify (1, 16, 128, 128) # tokens folded back to the grid -> VAE decode
🔑 The 4096 tokens are the same 4096 That token count is not new. It is the 4096 from page 53 — $64\times64$ after the VAE's $f=8$ downsample and the $2\times2$ patchify. On the U-Net those patches were pixels in a feature map; on the DiT they are tokens in a sequence, and every one attends to every other. Peebles & Xie (2023) measured the payoff: DiT quality tracks the block's GFLOPs monotonically — the same clean scaling behaviour you saw in the LLM trunk. The U-Net's convolutional inductive bias, so useful at small scale, becomes a constraint once you have the compute to not need it.

The DiT block is a plain transformer block; it has nowhere obvious to inject "denoise this much, toward this caption." Three designs solved it, and the field converged on the third.

Translation: the DiT paper calls $\boldsymbol\gamma_2$ the residual gate $\alpha$; we subscript it to keep a bare $\alpha$ meaning LoRA scaling (notation §5.4).

★ Why MMDiT won: the prompt gets to look at the canvas

Cross-attention — the U-Net's mechanism — is one-directional. The image queries the text; the text is shouted once, encoded, frozen, and never gets to see what is being drawn. The prompt's representation of "a red cube on a blue sphere" is fixed before a single pixel exists.

MMDiT concatenates the text and image tokens into one sequence and self-attends over the union — so text tokens attend to image tokens too. Each modality keeps its own $W_Q, W_K, W_V$ (text and image are different animals), but they share one softmax over the joined sequence.

img tokens (B, 4096, d) # the patchified noised latent txt tokens (B, S_txt, d) # the prompt, from the frozen encoder concat (B, 4096 + S_txt, d) # ONE sequence Q,K,V separate W per modality, then concatenated # different weights, shared attention scores = Q Kt / sqrt(d_head) (B, H=48, 4096+S_txt, 4096+S_txt) # image<->text, both directions
🔑 A capability change, not a refactor Because the text can now adapt to the half-drawn canvas, "a red cube on a blue sphere" can resolve which noun owns which colour by looking at where the shapes landed. This is why SD3/FLUX render legible text and bind attributes, and why SD1.5 — with its one-way cross-attention — constantly swapped them (red sphere, blue cube). You have lived this exact jump moving your own work from SD1.5 to FLUX. It was not a bigger model alone; it was the attention becoming bidirectional. [THREAD: the dot product · the atom] Joint attention is still just $\operatorname{softmax}(QK^\top/\sqrt{d_\text{head}})$ — the same dot-product similarity from the trunk. What is new is only which tokens are in the sequence: text and image together. Reopen p.29's soft-lookup picture; the mechanism is unchanged.

🔬 Deep dive: the SD3.5-Large joint sequence, walked

SD3.5-Large concatenates its $4096$ image tokens [DER, §9.6] with $\approx154$ text tokens (its dual CLIP encoders contribute $77+77$, T5 pooled separately), giving a joined length of $\approx\mathbf{4250}$ [EST — the 154 text count is illustrative, not frozen]. Every joint-attention block then forms a $[B, H, 4250, 4250]$ score matrix — image-to-image, text-to-text, and both cross directions in one softmax. That $4250^2 \approx 1.8\times10^{7}$ entries per head per block is the price of letting the prompt watch the canvas; page 60 does the memory arithmetic in full.

The sampler dropdown is a menu of ODE solvers

Sampling is integrating an ordinary differential equation downward in noise level $\sigma$. Writing the network's posterior-mean prediction as the denoiser $D(\mathbf{x},\sigma)$, the probability-flow ODE is a single line:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{d\sigma} = \frac{\mathbf{x} - D(\mathbf{x},\sigma)}{\sigma} \;=\; -\,\sigma\,\mathbf{s}_\theta(\mathbf{x},\sigma).$$

Every entry in your sampler dropdown is a different numerical scheme for walking that curve from $\sigma_{\max}$ down to $0$. The differences are pure numerical analysis — order, and how many times you evaluate the (expensive) network per step.

Three takeaways no tutorial spells out:
  1. dpmpp_2m is the free lunch. It reaches second-order accuracy by reusing the previous step's model output (an Adams–Bashforth multistep) — so it spends 1 model call per step, exactly like Euler, while Heun pays 2 calls for the same order. That answers the second predict: 1 call, not 2. Same accuracy as Heun at half the network evaluations.
  2. Ancestral samplers ($\_a$) do not converge. They inject fresh noise every step, so euler_a at 100 steps is not euler_a at 20 steps improved — it is a different image (the first predict). Deterministic samplers (euler, ddim with eta=0, dpmpp_2m) walk one fixed trajectory and sharpen toward a single answer as you add steps. This resolves the DDIM-$\eta{=}0$ puzzle from page 55: $\eta{=}0$ removes the re-injected noise, turning the stochastic sampler into a deterministic ODE that does converge.
  3. Step count is step size. Error falls as $O(h^p)$ for a $p$-th-order method with step $h$. FLUX needs fewer Euler steps than SD1.5 not because it is "better tuned" but because flow matching straightened the path (page 57) — Euler is exact on a straight line.
🔑 denoise decoded — where on the ladder you jump in Setting denoise=0.6 in a KSampler is not "remove 60% of the noise." It means: skip the first 40% of the schedule, noise the input latent to $t = 0.6\,T$, and run only the last 12 of a 20-step grid. It is a starting point on the noise ladder, which is exactly why low denoise preserves the input image (you start close to it) and denoise=1.0 starts from pure noise (img2img → txt2img). Not a mystery dial — a choice of $\sigma_{\text{start}}$.

Distillation: buying steps back with weights

If step count is the cost, the frontier's move is to bake the trajectory into the weights so you need almost no steps. The family tree — progressive distillation, Reflow (page 57's re-coupling), consistency / LCM, ADD, DMD, and guidance distillation — all trade training compute for inference steps. Guidance distillation is the one on your disk: it bakes CFG into the weights (FLUX.1-dev/schnell), which is why FLUX's guidance is not the same knob as real cfg — page 61 derives that in full.

⚠️ The step count is not free — you paid in controllability A distilled model is less steerable, and the symptoms are ones you have hit. The klein example's guidance_scale=1.0 is a constraint, not a default — real CFG largely does not work on a guidance-distilled model (page 61). Distilled checkpoints also show narrower diversity and are harder to LoRA-train (page 62). "4-step FLUX" and "one-step turbo" are not free quality; they spent your controllability to buy the steps.

Four trends, and one deliberate contradiction. (1) Everything is a transformer. (2) Everything is flow matching. (3) The text encoders became LLMs — CLIP's 123M → T5's 4.7B → a 24B Mistral-3, twice the size of the entire FLUX.1 model, just to read the prompt (page 61 cashes this out). (4) Small + distilled beats big: a 6B Z-Image-Turbo tops a 32B FLUX.2-dev on the open-weights arena. That last one is the opposite of the LLM track's still-scaling story — the LLM side keeps winning by getting bigger; the diffusion side is winning by getting smaller and distilled. The tracks genuinely diverge here (flagged deliberately). "Teach on SD1.5, work on FLUX.1, flex on FLUX.2."

⚠️ "Transformers won" has a scale asterisk At small scale — under ~1B params, 256² and below — a U-Net still wins. Its convolutional hierarchy is a genuine prior that helps when you cannot afford the compute for a transformer to learn spatial structure from scratch. That is exactly why page 58's from-scratch MNIST artifact (code/ddpm_mnist.py) is a U-Net, not a DiT. The honest rule is not "transformers won" but "the right architecture depends on scale" — which is the same lesson the whole course keeps handing you.
