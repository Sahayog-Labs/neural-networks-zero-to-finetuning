Fine-Tuning Diffusion: Putting Your Thing in the Model

Everything on your disk — the LoRAs in models/loras/, the ControlNets, the IP-Adapters — is one of a small number of ways to change what a diffusion model does without retraining it from scratch. This page gives you the taxonomy, the arithmetic, and a protocol for knowing whether it worked.

🔮 Predict first You train a LoRA on 20 photos in 20 minutes. You download a ControlNet someone trained on 100k+ images. One of those changed the base model; one didn't. Which is which — and why are there roughly 500,000 LoRAs on Civitai but only about 15 useful ControlNets? Commit to an answer before you scroll.

Orthogonal to WHERE is WHAT (form vs. facts — facts you can't teach this way; that's what RAG is for) and WHICH recipe (SFT / DreamBooth / DPO). The test that actually separates them:

After you're done, is the base model different?

LoRA, DreamBooth, full FT → yes, the weights moved. ControlNet, IP-Adapter → no — bit-identical base model, you bolted something onto the input pipeline. "DreamBooth vs. LoRA" is a category error you'll see argued online: DreamBooth is what you train (a recipe — rare-token + prior-preservation loss), LoRA is how you store it (a parameterization). Modern practice is both at once — the actual training script in the wild is named train_dreambooth_lora_flux.py.

The Civitai ratio, resolved. A LoRA changes the model — but by a tiny, cheap, low-rank nudge, trainable on 20 photos in minutes. A ControlNet changes nothing about the base model; it is a whole new encoder (~360M params [EST] — not in constants.md, carried from the brief, flagged) trained on the full distribution the base model was trained on (100k+ pose/depth/edge pairs), because it has to work for every subject, not one. That is why 500,000 people can each ship a LoRA of their own face by lunchtime, and why the field has produced perhaps 15 ControlNets total that anyone actually uses — training one is a full research project, not a Tuesday afternoon.

Why not just fully fine-tune?

The optimizer line is the headline: 96 GB → 0.24 GB, a factor of ≈400 [DER] — because AdamW state scales with trainable params (12B vs. 30M), and that ratio is even sharper than the LLM track's 187× because the diffusion attn-only target is a smaller slice of the model.
⚠️ The win is not the weights All 24 GB of frozen base weights are still resident in VRAM whether you train a LoRA or not — LoRA freezes them, it does not delete them. It also does not shorten the computation graph: activations are ~unchanged between full FT and LoRA at the same batch/resolution (constants.md §2.4). If you measure your LoRA run and find it sitting above 29–31 GB by roughly the activation term, that is expected, not a leak.
🔑 network_alpha and strength — your daily knobs The effective scale multiplying $BA$ is $\alpha/r$, not $\alpha$ alone. Trainers commonly default network_alpha = r/2 — which halves your adapter's effect before you've turned a single knob in ComfyUI. This is the single most common "why isn't my LoRA learning?" cause [EST — training-tool convention, not a constants.md figure]: the fix is either raise LR ~2× or set network_alpha = r. ComfyUI's strength_model slider is a further multiplier on top of that: $$W = W_0 + \text{strength}\cdot\frac{\alpha}{r}\,BA.$$ strength > 1.0 is extrapolation past what you trained — the exact same move as pushing $w_g > 1$ on p.61, the exact same failure mode (over-saturated, "fried" output). [THREAD: The dot product · beat] $BA$ is a sum of $r$ rank-one outer products — the atom, reopened as "the directions a LoRA is allowed to push in."
🔬 Deep dive: where to attach a LoRA, and the variant zoo

to_q, to_k, to_v, to_out — attention-only — is the diffusion default (D-06/D-11): this is a claim about where cross-modal binding lives, not a parameter-budget trick. Cross-attention-only targets teach "a new word" (a subject); adding the convolutions (LoCon) teaches "a new look" (a style). QLoRA on a 4-bit base is the practical path for FLUX.2-dev: NF4 with double-quant is 0.516 B/param = 4.127 bits/param [VP, constants.md §3] — but only if you explicitly set bnb_4bit_use_double_quant=True; leave it at its library default (False) and you silently get 0.5625 B/param [VP] instead, 9% more base memory than the number every guide quotes.

★ Rank, LR, example-count — one principle, three symptoms

🔑 How much are you asking the model to learn? Rank follows the information content of the dataset, not the model size. A diffusion concept LoRA — your face, your dog, a specific object — is one point on a manifold the model already knows how to draw; 15–20 images [EST] of it need only $r{=}8\text{–}32$. Push $r$ much higher on 20 images and the adapter memorizes the training photos instead of learning the concept — it has capacity to spare and spends it on noise. (LLM SFT sits at the opposite end: a dataset of instructions is information-dense, so $r{=}64\text{–}256$ is nearly free — reconciling with the LLM track's own rank choice, D-09/D-11.)

LR follows trainable-param count (the same principle p.40 states for the LLM track): fewer trainable params tolerate a higher LR because the loss landscape they move in is smaller.

⚠️ The 100× trap Diffusion LoRA's LR is 1e-4, not the full-FT figure of 1e-6 — a hundred-fold gap (D-09). Copy the full-FT learning rate into a LoRA config and the adapter trains at 1/100th the speed it should; the loss barely moves and it looks like the LoRA "isn't learning" when it is simply crawling. This is the single most common diffusion fine-tuning bug reported online.

DreamBooth and textual inversion

✍️ DreamBooth: a rare token plus a mirror Bind the concept to a rare token (e.g. sks) and add a prior-preservation term so the model doesn't forget what "a photo of a man" means in general: $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{DB}} = \mathbb{E}\big\|\boldsymbol\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(\cdot,\mathbf{c}_{\text{sks}})\big\|^2 + \lambda_{pp}\,\mathbb{E}\big\|\boldsymbol\epsilon' - \epsilon_\theta(\cdot,\mathbf{c}_{\text{class}})\big\|^2, \qquad \lambda_{pp} = 1.0.$$

30-second drift diagnostic: after training, generate "a photo of a man" with no trigger token. If it's your specific person — the concept leaked into the general class — that's drift; add prior-preservation images (or more of them). If it stays generic, skip it. (Live disagreement in the community over whether prior-preservation is worth the extra training cost for small personal LoRAs — this is the actual diagnostic that settles it for your case, not folklore.)
🔑 Textual inversion — the intellectual climax of this page Freeze everything — the whole denoiser, untouched — and optimize one new embedding vector $v^\star \in \mathbb{R}^{d_c}$. For SD1.5's CLIP encoder, $d_c = 768$: $$768 \text{ floats} \times 4\text{ B} = \mathbf{3\text{ KB}}.$$ The model already knew how to draw your thing — it has to, it was trained on billions of images that certainly include your general category. TI doesn't teach it anything; it finds an address in a space the model already has and labels it. The 3 KB is a pointer, not a payload. Rule to hold: TI retrieves; LoRA teaches.

ControlNet and IP-Adapter — not fine-tuning

🔑 Two more places to attach, neither one $\theta$ ControlNet: clone the encoder half of the denoiser, feed the clone a hint (an edge map, a depth map, a pose skeleton), and wire its output back into the frozen original via zero-initialized convolutions — the same "start the new thing at exactly zero so it's a no-op on step 0" move you've now met three times: LoRA's $B{=}0$, adaLN-Zero's $\alpha{=}0$ (p.59), and this. ~360M params [EST — brief-sourced, not in constants.md] ≈ 1.4 GB [EST] — not a LoRA-scale object; the reason so few exist.

IP-Adapter: decoupled cross-attention — a second cross-attention branch reads a CLIP image encoding instead of text, and its output is added to the text branch: $$Z = \mathrm{Attn}(Q, K_{\text{text}}, V_{\text{text}}) + \lambda\,\mathrm{Attn}(Q, K_{\text{img}}, V_{\text{img}}).$$
SymbolIsShapeFrom

The text branch is untouched; only $W_K',W_V'$ for the image branch are new — "a mixing desk, not a surgery."

The punchline: you can stack a LoRA ($\theta$ changes) + a ControlNet ($\mathbf{c}$ gains an input) + an IP-Adapter ($\mathbf{c}$ gains another input) + a text prompt ($\mathbf{c}$ again) all at once, and they don't collide — because they aren't the same kind of object. 🔬 Deep dive: dataset craft, derived from the loss (not folklore)

The invariance principle: the model learns whatever is constant across your dataset. So make the concept constant and vary everything else — angle, lighting, background, crop. 15–20 images [EST] is the usual sweet spot; "more is often worse," because 100 photos taken in one sitting bundle "this face" with "this lighting, this outfit, this room" and the model can't tell which part you meant.

🔑 Caption what you want to VARY, omit what you BAKE IN This is derived from the loss, not a style guide: any feature that appears in the caption $\mathbf{c}$ gets attributed to those words, and the model learns to draw it only when asked. Any feature that's absent from every caption has nowhere to go but the trigger token / the weights themselves. So: training a subject — describe the context, omit the subject. Training a style — describe the content, omit the style (this is exactly how "triggerless" style LoRAs work: the style word never appears in a single caption, so it has no home except the adapter weights).

Bucketing is a batching constraint, not a quality knob: group images by aspect ratio so every batch has a constant pixel count → constant latent token count → constant step time. Dimensions divisible by 64 come from the pipeline's own compounded downsampling: $f{=}8$ (VAE) $\times\ 2$ (patchify) $\times\ 4 = 64$. "Ten minutes reading your own captions beats a week of hyperparameter search."

Evaluating a LoRA — a protocol, not a vibe

⚠️ The loss curve is nearly useless Diffusion training loss is dominated by the random $t$ draw (p.55) — a lucky batch of high-$t$ steps looks like a plateau, a batch near $t\approx500$ looks like a spike, and the curve stays flat-with-huge-variance whether the model is learning cleanly or quietly memorizing your 20 photos. Watching it converge tells you almost nothing.
🔑 The protocol
  • Fixed prompt grid, fixed seeds, every 250 steps → a contact sheet you can flip through like a flipbook.
  • The Mars probe: "sks man as an astronaut on Mars, full body" — an out-of-distribution prompt that tests whether the concept generalizes or only reproduces training poses.
  • The drift control: "a photo of a man", no trigger — did the concept leak into the general class (the DreamBooth diagnostic above, reused here as a protocol step).
  • A strength sweep: a healthy LoRA should be usable around strength_model = 1.0 without artifacts; if it only looks right at 0.6, something's off.
  • ArcFace IdentitySim for faces specifically: >0.6 good, >0.4 recognizable [EST].
  • Your eyes are the actual metric here — the protocol's job is to turn "the vibe" into a repeatable procedure, not to replace judgment with a number.
🔬 Deep dive: six overfitting signs, and why FID/CLIP-score don't save you
  • Background bleed (the training backdrop shows up uninvited) → caption the background so it isn't "constant" anymore.
  • Pose lock (every output copies a training pose) → add pose variety to the dataset.
  • Adherence collapse (the prompt stops mattering) → over-trained; back off steps or LR.
  • Contrast burn (blown highlights/shadows) → classic over-training artifact, same family as CFG-frying.
  • Memorization (outputs are near-duplicates of specific training photos) → rank too high for the dataset size, or too many steps.

FID and CLIP-score are worth naming and distrusting: FID assumes a Gaussian feature distribution that real image sets don't have, it's badly biased at small $N$ (exactly your 20-image regime), and both scores are gameable by simply cranking CFG higher — "the judge and the defendant share a brain" (both are built from the same class of vision-language embeddings the generator was steered toward). Use them as a coarse sanity check, never as the deciding number.
