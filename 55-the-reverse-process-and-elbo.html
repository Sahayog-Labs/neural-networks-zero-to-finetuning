The Reverse Process & the ELBO Collapse

The reverse of one big noising step is as tangled as all of image space; the reverse of a tiny step is a Gaussian blob. That is the whole trade. And a thousand-term variational monster over a million dimensions collapses, by one lemma you already own, into the single line every image you have ever generated was trained on: MSELoss.

🔮 Predict first The training objective behind every image you have ever made in ComfyUI — the loss the denoiser was optimized against — is it (a) an adversarial game between two networks, (b) reinforcement learning against a reward, or (c) plain MSELoss on a regression problem? Commit to one letter before you read on.

Why you cannot just run the ruler backwards

Page 54 handed you a forward process with zero parameters: a ruler that rotates a clean latent $\mathbf{x}_0$ into noise. The obvious idea is to invert it — walk the chain back from noise to a picture. The obstacle is that the single honest reverse step

$$q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t) = \frac{q(\mathbf{x}_t\mid\mathbf{x}_{t-1})\,q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1})}{q(\mathbf{x}_t)}$$

needs $q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1})$ — the marginal distribution of all slightly-cleaner images. That is the data distribution you are trying to learn in the first place. You cannot write it down. The reverse of a big jump is a dead end because undoing a lot of noise means guessing which of the astronomically many possible pictures the noise came from — a wildly multimodal answer.

🔑 Why 1000 steps: small steps are Gaussian, big steps are not Feller (1949): when $\beta_t$ is small, the true reverse step $q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t)$ is itself approximately Gaussian. So you never model the whole intractable reversal — you model one small step at a time and parameterize it as $$p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t) = \mathcal{N}\!\big(\mathbf{x}_{t-1};\ \boldsymbol\mu_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t,t),\ \Sigma_\theta\big),$$ learning only the mean. The reverse of a big step is as multimodal as $p_{\text{data}}$ itself; the reverse of a tiny step is a small Gaussian bump around where you already are. Diffusion trades one impossible problem for a thousand easy ones — and the 1000 is a training-time statement about tractability, not a claim that you must sample in 1000 steps (page 57's four-step FLUX is the counterpoint).
🔬 Deep dive: the tractable cousin $q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{x}_0)$ and where $\boldsymbol\mu_\theta$ comes from

The reverse step is intractable, but the reverse step conditioned on the clean image is not. Bayes with the two forward Gaussians (both known) gives a Gaussian whose mean and variance are closed-form: $$\tilde{\boldsymbol\mu}_t(\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{x}_0) = \frac{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\,\beta_t}{1-\bar\alpha_t}\mathbf{x}_0 + \frac{\sqrt{\alpha_t}\,(1-\bar\alpha_{t-1})}{1-\bar\alpha_t}\mathbf{x}_t,\qquad \tilde\beta_t = \frac{1-\bar\alpha_{t-1}}{1-\bar\alpha_t}\beta_t.$$ The network never sees $\mathbf{x}_0$ at sampling time, so it is trained to predict it (or, equivalently, the noise). Setting $\Sigma_\theta = \tilde\beta_t I$ — a fixed, unlearned covariance — is what makes the next section's KL tractable, and it is the choice DDPM ships. (The rigid-triangle demo below verifies the $\boldsymbol\epsilon/\mathbf{x}_0/v$ conversions this covariance choice rides on — the round-trip is exact to machine precision; $\tilde{\boldsymbol\mu}_t$ itself is what the next section's KL collapse consumes.)

★ One triangle, three names: $\boldsymbol\epsilon$ vs $\mathbf{x}_0$ vs $v$

Before we can talk about the loss we have to settle what the network should report. This is the source of endless confusion in the field — $\epsilon$-pred, $\mathbf{x}_0$-pred, $v$-pred — and it dissolves the instant you notice it is one equation, not three models. Eq. 3.3 from page 54, $\mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon$, is a single line with two unknowns and one known: give it any one of $\{\mathbf{x}_0, \boldsymbol\epsilon, v\}$ and simple algebra returns the other two.

Reopen page 54's circle. With $\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t} = \cos\psi_t$ and $\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t} = \sin\psi_t$, the whole zoo lives on the unit circle spanned by the two orthonormal axes $\mathbf{x}_0$ and $\boldsymbol\epsilon$:

🔑 The rigid triangle on the circle $$\mathbf{x}_t = \cos\psi_t\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sin\psi_t\,\boldsymbol\epsilon, \qquad v \equiv \frac{d\mathbf{x}_t}{d\psi_t} = \cos\psi_t\,\boldsymbol\epsilon - \sin\psi_t\,\mathbf{x}_0.$$

Why the choice matters at all, if the information is the same: the three targets put the squared error in different places, and the schedule reweights them by the signal-to-noise ratio. Converting an $\epsilon$-error into an $\mathbf{x}_0$-error costs a factor of $\mathrm{SNR}_t$:

$$\|\boldsymbol\epsilon - \hat{\boldsymbol\epsilon}\|^2 = \mathrm{SNR}_t\,\|\mathbf{x}_0 - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_0\|^2, \qquad \mathrm{SNR}_t = \frac{\bar\alpha_t}{1-\bar\alpha_t}.$$

Over the linear DDPM schedule $\mathrm{SNR}_t$ runs from about $\mathbf{10^{4}}$ at low $t$ down to $\mathbf{4\times10^{-5}}$ at $t=T$ [DER, constants §9.6] — nine orders of magnitude. Whichever coordinate you regress, you are implicitly choosing how loss budget is spread across noise levels. That is the whole game, and it is why the field argues about targets at all.

Page 56 differentiates once more, w.r.t. $\mathbf{x}_t$ this time, and finds the score; page 57 shows $v$ is flow matching's velocity on the circular path. Keep the word "velocity"; both pages reopen it.

⚠️ The $\epsilon$-pred pole — why $v$-pred was invented Recover the clean image from a noise prediction and you divide by the signal coefficient: $$\hat{\mathbf{x}}_0 = \frac{\mathbf{x}_t - \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\hat{\boldsymbol\epsilon}}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}}.$$ At the last steps the denominator is tiny: $\sqrt{\bar\alpha_T} = 0.0063$ [DER, constants §9.6], so dividing by it multiplies any error in $\hat{\boldsymbol\epsilon}$ by $1/0.0063 = \mathbf{159\times}$. A denoiser that is slightly wrong about the noise at $t\approx T$ is catastrophically wrong about the image. The $v$-pred conversions above have coefficients $\cos\psi_t,\sin\psi_t \le 1$ — no division, no pole, stable at both ends of the circle. This is not a footnote: $v$-prediction is the ancestor of flow matching's velocity (p.57). Do not skip it — it prepays that page.

★ The ELBO collapse — where the monster dies

Now the loss. The principled objective is the variational bound on the negative log-likelihood (the same ELBO the VAE of page 53 used, now with a thousand latent layers instead of one). Written out, it is genuinely frightening:

$$-\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0) \le \mathbb{E}_q\Big[\underbrace{L_T}_{\text{prior}} + \sum_{t=2}^{T} \underbrace{L_{t-1}}_{\text{denoising KLs}} + \underbrace{L_0}_{\text{decoder}}\Big], \qquad L_{t-1} = D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\big(q(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{x}_0)\,\|\,p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t)\big).$$

A thousand KL divergences, each between two distributions over $\mathbb{R}^{3{,}145{,}728}$ (a $1024^2$ RGB image; even the FLUX latent of page 53 is $\mathbb{R}^{524{,}288}$). No one optimizes that by staring at it. You take it apart — click through it below, one term at a time.

Three terms to click. Start with $L_T$.

Here is what each click actually does. $L_T = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q(\mathbf{x}_T\mid\mathbf{x}_0)\,\|\,\mathcal{N}(0,I))$ contains no $\theta$ — the forward process is a fixed ruler, so this term cannot be optimized and drops. (Note it is not zero: that residual is the same nonzero-terminal-SNR fact from page 54.) $L_0$ is a single reconstruction term. The 1000 middle terms are the ones that matter, and they fall to a lemma you already own.

📐 The equal-covariance KL lemma (trunk-owned — reopened, not re-derived) For two Gaussians with the same fixed covariance, the KL is just a scaled squared distance between their means: $$D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\big(\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol\mu_1,\sigma^2 I)\,\|\,\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol\mu_2,\sigma^2 I)\big) = \frac{\|\boldsymbol\mu_1 - \boldsymbol\mu_2\|^2}{2\sigma^2}.$$ The trunk derived this from the Gaussian log-density. A KL over $\mathbb{R}^{3M}$ becomes a number divided into a squared distance. This one lemma is where the monster dies.

Both distributions in $L_{t-1}$ are Gaussians with the same covariance $\tilde\beta_t I$ (the deep dive above), so the lemma applies term by term. Substitute the $\epsilon$-parameterization of both means, and the $\mathbf{x}_t$ terms — present identically in $\tilde{\boldsymbol\mu}_t$ and $\boldsymbol\mu_\theta$ — cancel exactly, leaving a plain noise-prediction error:

$$L_{t-1} = w_t\,\big\|\boldsymbol\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)\big\|^2, \qquad w_t = \frac{\beta_t^2}{2\tilde\beta_t\,\alpha_t\,(1-\bar\alpha_t)}.$$

The principled weight $w_t$ is exactly the $\mathrm{SNR}_t$ reweighting from the triangle section. Then comes the move that made diffusion work:

🔑 $\mathcal{L}_{\text{simple}}$ — Ho et al.'s honest hack Delete the weight. Set $w_t := 1$. It makes the variational bound technically wrong, and it makes the images dramatically better: $$\boxed{\ \mathcal{L}_{\text{simple}} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}_0,\ \boldsymbol\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I),\ t\sim\mathcal{U}\{1,T\}}\Big[\big\|\boldsymbol\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon,\ t)\big\|_2^2\Big]\ }$$

This is plain MSELoss on a regression problem. It is the same "MSE falls out of a Gaussian likelihood" the trunk proved — a diffusion corollary of it — and it is simpler than the LLM track's cross-entropy: predict a vector, subtract, square.
⚠️ Genuine, live uncertainty: dropping $w_t$ is not theory-justified Do not file $w_t := 1$ under "settled." It is not derived; it is a choice that happens to reweight training toward the mid-noise band ($t\in[300,700]$, where composition is decided — page 54's SNR$\approx 1$ region) and away from the near-clean and near-noise ends the bound would have you obsess over. What weight belongs there is an open 2026 research question, not a solved one: min-SNR-$\gamma$, logit-normal timestep sampling (p.57), EDM's weighting, P2 — these are all live rival answers to "what should $w_t$ be." When you set timestep_sampling or a loss weighting in a FLUX training config, this is the knob you are turning.

[THREAD: The memory budget · beat] The reality check returns. Training this MSE still backprops through the whole denoiser at every step, so the activations cached on the forward pass (page 54's shape-ribbon tensors, $\phi'$-style intermediates) are what eat VRAM — not the loss, which is one subtraction. The loss being simple does not make the memory small. We settle exactly how much on page 60's latent-diffusion roofline and page 62's LoRA budget.

The whole training loop is six lines

✍️ DDPM training — and there is no loop over $t$ Every term above collapsed into one sampled expectation, so the algorithm is a draw-and-regress:
Python
for x0 in data:                              # 1. a clean latent (VAE-encoded, p.53)
    t   = torch.randint(1, T + 1, (x0.shape[0],))  # 2. one uniform timestep per example
    eps = torch.randn_like(x0)                   # 3. the label
    xt  = abar[t].sqrt() * x0 + (1 - abar[t]).sqrt() * eps   # 4. Eq. 3.3, ONE line
    loss = F.mse_loss(eps_theta(xt, t), eps)   # 5. L_simple
    loss.backward(); opt.step(); opt.zero_grad()  # 6. one SGD step
Line 4 is Eq. 3.3 — you jump straight to level $t$, you never simulate a chain. That is what "training is $O(1)$, not $O(T)$" (page 54) buys you in code.
🔑 A diffusion model is a VAE with 1000 latent layers and a frozen encoder Page 53 cashes out here. The forward ruler is the encoder — fixed, unlearned. The 1000 noise levels are the latent layers. The denoiser is the decoder, trained by the ELBO you just watched collapse. The only reason it beats a one-layer VAE is that breaking the encode into a thousand tiny Gaussian steps is what made each reverse step tractable in the first place.

One loose end for sampling, since you turn these knobs in ComfyUI. Each reverse step draws $\mathbf{x}_{t-1} = \boldsymbol\mu_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t,t) + \sigma_t\,\mathbf{z}$ with a fresh $\mathbf{z}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ — you re-inject noise. Why, when the network already predicted the mean? Because $p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1}\mid\mathbf{x}_t)$ is a distribution, not a point. Take only the mean at every step and you march toward the single highest-probability blob — mode-seeking mush, a brown-grey average of everything. The re-injected $\sigma_t\mathbf{z}$ is what keeps you sampling a picture instead of collapsing to the average picture. (Page 59's DDIM sets eta$=0$ and drops that noise entirely, yet still produces sharp images — a genuine puzzle we bank for there.)
