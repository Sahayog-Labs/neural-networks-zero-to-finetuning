What "rank" means, before any machinery

A matrix is a machine that moves every point at once; its rank is how many independent directions it can actually move things in — everything else it does is a combination of those. A 4096×4096 weight matrix could in principle stir space in 4096 independent directions. The entire bet of this part of the course is that the matrices we care about — specifically the updates a fine-tune makes — stir it in far fewer, and the rest is noise you can drop.

From the dot product to rank

[THREAD:dot-product] Reopen the atom from page 3. Take two column vectors $\mathbf u\in\mathbb R^{d_{out}}$ and $\mathbf v\in\mathbb R^{d_{in}}$ and multiply them the "wrong" way round — column times row — to get a matrix, the outer product:

$$\mathbf u\,\mathbf v^\top \;=\; \begin{bmatrix} u_1 v_1 & u_1 v_2 & \cdots \\ u_2 v_1 & u_2 v_2 & \cdots \\ \vdots & & \ddots \end{bmatrix} \qquad (d_{out}, d_{in}).$$

Every row of $\mathbf u\mathbf v^\top$ is the same row $\mathbf v^\top$, just scaled by a different $u_i$; every column is $\mathbf u$ scaled by a different $v_j$. One direction in ($\mathbf v$), one direction out ($\mathbf u$). That is a rank-1 matrix — the smallest non-trivial machine there is. The rank of any matrix is the fewest rank-1 pieces you must add together to rebuild it exactly. Outer products are the atoms; a matrix is a sum of them. The only question worth asking is: how few do you actually need?

🗺️ Notation note — three symbols to keep straight This is a trunk page, so $\sigma$ has no sigmoid duties here: $\sigma_i$ (subscripted, no argument) always means the $i$-th singular value — the stretch factor of the $i$-th direction, ordered $\sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\ge\cdots\ge 0$. $r$ is the rank, a count — the same $r$ that becomes LoRA's rank one page from now. And the invariant holds everywhere: $W$ is $(d_{out},d_{in})$. Papers write the factorization $A\Sigma B^\top$, $U S V'$, or "the SVD" — same object; we use $U\Sigma V^\top$.

The SVD: every matrix is a weighted sum of outer products

The remarkable fact — true of every real matrix, no exceptions, no conditions — is that you can always choose the rank-1 pieces to be orthonormal and order them by importance. That choice is the singular value decomposition:

📐 The singular value decomposition $$W \;=\; U\,\Sigma\,V^\top \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{\min(d_{out},\,d_{in})} \sigma_i\,\mathbf u_i \mathbf v_i^\top, \qquad \sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\ge\cdots\ge 0.$$ A sum of rank-1 outer products $\mathbf u_i\mathbf v_i^\top$, each weighted by how much the matrix stretches that direction. The $\mathbf u_i$ are orthonormal, the $\mathbf v_i$ are orthonormal, and the $\sigma_i$ are sorted largest-first — so the front of the sum carries the most of the matrix.

That every real matrix has such a decomposition is a theorem we will use, not prove here (the proof, and the link to eigenvalues for readers who remember them, is in the deep dive below — nothing later depends on it).

Keep the first $r$ terms — the rank-$r$ approximation

Because the sum is ordered, truncating it after $r$ terms keeps the most important part of the matrix and throws the tail away:

$$W_r \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{r}\sigma_i\,\mathbf u_i\mathbf v_i^\top, \qquad \text{stored numbers} \;=\; r\,(d_{out}+d_{in}+1)\;\;\text{vs.}\;\; d_{out}\,d_{in}.$$

The $+1$ is the one singular value $\sigma_i$ you keep per term; the $d_{out}$ and $d_{in}$ are the columns of $U$ and rows of $V^\top$ you keep. Whether that is a bargain depends entirely on how fast the $\sigma_i$ fall.

✍️ Worked — the storage count on a 256×256 photo A 256×256 grayscale image is $256\times 256 = \mathbf{65{,}536}$ numbers. Keep the top directions:
  • $r=8:\;\; 8\,(256+256+1) = 8\times 513 = \mathbf{4{,}104}$ numbers $= \mathbf{6.3\%}$ of 65,536 — and, as the demo is about to show you, recognizable.
  • $r=16:\;\; 16\times 513 = \mathbf{8{,}208}$ numbers $= \mathbf{12.5\%}$.
  • $r=32:\;\; 32\times 513 = \mathbf{16{,}416}$ numbers $= \mathbf{25.0\%}$, with a relative error around $3\%$ — hard to tell from the original.
And $W_r$ is not just a good rank-$r$ approximation — by the Eckart–Young theorem it is the best one in Frobenius norm. There is no cleverer way to spend $r$ directions. (Proof in the deep dive.)

The scree plot, and the punchline

Plot the singular values $\sigma_1,\sigma_2,\dots$ in order, log scale, and you get a scree plot — the matrix's "how concentrated am I?" fingerprint. If it falls off a cliff, a handful of directions carry nearly all the matrix and a low-rank approximation is almost free. If it decays slowly, the information is spread everywhere and truncating destroys it.

🔑 Key idea Real matrices are often approximately low-rank: their information is not spread evenly across all 65,536 numbers — it concentrates in a few dozen directions. That concentration is the entire LoRA hypothesis, and you are about to verify it on a photograph. But — and this is the whole game — the claim that matters is about $\Delta W$, the change a fine-tune makes, not about $W_0$, the pretrained weight itself. Those two matrices have opposite scree plots.

⚠️ The misconception: "the pretrained model is low-rank" False — and getting this backwards is the single most common LoRA misunderstanding. A real slice of a pretrained weight (the demo's $W_0$, matching what you measure on a Qwen3 q_proj) is nearly full rank: its scree plot decays slowly, and replacing it with a rank-16 approximation destroys the model (that is quiz Q5). What is low-rank is $\Delta W$, the update a fine-tune adds on top: its singular values fall off a cliff. LoRA never approximates $W_0$. It only ever stores $\Delta W$ cheaply. You just watched both scree plots — this is not an assertion, it is something you can see.
🔬 Deep dive: why every real matrix has an SVD, and why $W_r$ is optimal

Existence. $W^\top W$ is symmetric and positive-semidefinite, so it has an orthonormal eigenbasis $\mathbf v_1,\dots,\mathbf v_{d_{in}}$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_i\ge 0$. Define $\sigma_i=\sqrt{\lambda_i}$ and, for $\sigma_i>0$, $\mathbf u_i = W\mathbf v_i/\sigma_i$. A short check shows the $\mathbf u_i$ are orthonormal and $W = \sum_i \sigma_i\mathbf u_i\mathbf v_i^\top$. So the singular values squared are the eigenvalues of $W^\top W$ — the bridge to eigen-anything, offered as a translation, not required (nothing on this page or later needs you to think in eigenvalues).

Optimality (Eckart–Young). Among all matrices of rank $\le r$, the truncated SVD $W_r$ minimizes $\lVert W - B\rVert_F$, and the minimum equals $\bigl(\sum_{i>r}\sigma_i^2\bigr)^{1/2}$. Intuition: the squared Frobenius norm splits cleanly across the orthonormal directions as $\lVert W\rVert_F^2=\sum_i\sigma_i^2$, so the cheapest way to lose the least "energy" is to drop the smallest $\sigma_i$ — which is exactly what truncation does. The live demo's relative error and its energy-tail formula agree to the digit for this reason.

Try it · code/svd_rank_probe.py Confirm the whole page on the full 4096×4096 matrices, not a 64×64 toy. Load a real Qwen3 q_proj and a saved $\Delta W$ from an actual fine-tune, run torch.linalg.svd on both, plot the two scree curves, and print the rank each needs to reach 90% of its energy. You will watch $W_0$ need hundreds of directions and $\Delta W$ need a couple dozen — the LoRA hypothesis, measured on your box.
code/svd_rank_probe.py
import torch
from safetensors.torch import load_file

def energy_rank(W, frac=0.90):
    # rank needed to retain `frac` of the squared-Frobenius energy
    s = torch.linalg.svdvals(W.float())      # singular values, descending
    e = torch.cumsum(s**2, 0) / torch.sum(s**2)
    return int((e < frac).sum()) + 1, s

# a frozen pretrained weight, and an update from a real fine-tune
W0 = load_file("Qwen3-8B/model-00001-of-00005.safetensors")["model.layers.0.self_attn.q_proj.weight"]
dW = torch.load("delta_q_proj.pt")         # merged_adapter_weight - W0, same shape

assert W0.shape == (4096, 4096), W0.shape   # the real thing, not a toy
r0, s0 = energy_rank(W0)
rd, sd = energy_rank(dW)

print(f"W0  90%-energy rank: {r0:4d} / 4096   (nearly full rank)")
print(f"dW  90%-energy rank: {rd:4d} / 4096   (falls off a cliff)")
assert rd < r0 // 4, "the update should be far lower-rank than the weight"

So: an SVD is a matrix written as a sum of rank-1 outer products, ordered by importance; a low-rank approximation keeps the front of that sum; a scree plot tells you how few terms you need; and — the fact the whole course's destination rests on — the update $\Delta W$ is low-rank even though the weight $W_0$ is not.
