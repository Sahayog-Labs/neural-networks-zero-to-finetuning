The formula, before the arithmetic

A transformer's parameters live in exactly three places per block, plus the vocabulary at the two ends. Every one is a matrix whose size you already know from page 35. Per block:

$$P_{\text{attn}} = 2\,d\,d_{\text{head}}(H + H_{kv}), \qquad P_{\text{ffn}} = 3\,d\,d_{\text{ff}}, \qquad P_{\text{norm}} = 2d + 2\,d_{\text{head}}$$

and the whole model stacks $L$ of them between an input embedding and an output head:

$$P_{\text{total}} \;=\; \underbrace{Vd}_{\text{embed}} \;+\; L\cdot P_{\text{block}} \;+\; \underbrace{d}_{\text{final norm}} \;+\; \underbrace{Vd\cdot\mathbb{1}[\neg\text{tied}]}_{\text{lm\_head}}$$

📐 Two things that are not in the formula — and why

No positional-embedding table. RoPE (page 33) rotates $Q$ and $K$ by pure geometry — zero parameters. There is no learned position table to count.

No biases. attention_bias: false, and the SwiGLU FFN has none either — with RMSNorm carrying a learned gain, biases are redundant (page 35). Every matrix above is weight-only.

🗺️ Translation note — our symbols vs the config keys Papers and configs spell these a dozen ways. Course-wide we write $d$ (papers: $d_{\text{model}}$, hidden_size), $L$ (num_hidden_layers, papers' $N$), $H$ (num_attention_heads), $H_{kv}$ (num_key_value_heads, $n_{kv}$), $d_{\text{head}}$ ($d_k$, head_dim), $V$ (vocab_size, sometimes $K$). Open the config alongside this page and the mapping is one-to-one.

And that is before the 4× KV-cache saving you'll count on page 37: two wins from one config field.

🔑 The FFN is 78% of the block $150{,}994{,}944 / 192{,}946{,}432 = \mathbf{78.26\%}$. The page-35 claim, verified. Attention gets all the attention; the feed-forward network holds most of the weights — which is exactly why MoE (page 38) sparsifies the FFN and not attention.
Careful with denominators: 78.26% is the FFN's share of one block. Of the whole model it's 66.4% (different denominator — both are true). Never the retired “~70%,” which was a Llama-3.1 figure with $d_{\text{ff}}=3.5d$.

✅ The verification — closed twice

A formula that lands on a suspiciously round “8.2B” could be coincidence. It isn't — and here is how you know, two independent ways that never touch each other.

Route 1 — the model card Qwen's card publishes 8.2B total and 6.95B non-embedding. Our non-embedding $6{,}946{,}071{,}552 = 6.95\text{B}$ exact; our total $8{,}190{,}735{,}360 = 8.19\text{B} \to$ “8.2B” exact. Every published figure matches.
Route 2 — the checkpoint's own bytes (the independent route) The checkpoint ships a model.safetensors.index.json with a field total_size = 16,381,470,720 bytes. All 399 tensors are bf16 — 2 bytes each. So the parameter count is just $$16{,}381{,}470{,}720 \,/\, 2 \;=\; \mathbf{8{,}190{,}735{,}360}.$$ The exact same integer — from the checkpoint itself, not from any formula. Two roads, one number.
🔑 Nothing about this model is hidden We derived, from four numbers in a config and nothing else, a count that matches the official card to every significant figure they publish — and the checkpoint's own byte total, halved, lands on the same integer. This is the most empowering fact in the unit: open any HuggingFace config and you can predict the parameter count to a rounding error. Prediction resolved: exactly, to the byte — not to a rough 5%.

A trap you'll hit in Part IV — the LoRA-target subtotal

When you configure LoRA (page 40) to target “all linear layers,” the tooling reports a base count of 6,945,767,424 — 7 linear matrices per layer, times 36. That is not the model's parameter count, and a learner who confuses the two spends an afternoon confused. It's smaller because it omits the norms (norms aren't LoRA targets — correctly) and both embedding matrices. It reconciles to the full count exactly when you add them back:

✍️ It closes to the byte $$6{,}945{,}767{,}424 \;+\; \underbrace{36 \times 8{,}448}_{304{,}128\ \text{block norms}} \;+\; \underbrace{4{,}096}_{\text{final norm}} \;+\; \underbrace{2 \times 622{,}329{,}856}_{\text{embed + lm\_head}} \;=\; \mathbf{8{,}190{,}735{,}360}.$$ Exact. Not “closes to three significant figures” — it closes to the byte, and saying so is the point.

The three numbers you can now produce for any model

Once you have $P$, three memory numbers fall out by multiplication. These are the numbers that decide what you can run.

(1) Weights in memory $= P \times b_W$. At bf16 (2 bytes): $8{,}190{,}735{,}360 \times 2 = 16{,}381{,}470{,}720\ \text{B} = \mathbf{16.38\ GB}$. At fp8, 8.19 GB; at int4, ~4.1 GB (plus scales); at fp32, 32.8 GB (nobody does this for inference).

(2) KV cache (sized fully on page 37): 144 KiB/token, and 5.625 GiB at the full 40,960-token context. That is $2 \cdot L \cdot H_{kv} \cdot d_{\text{head}} \cdot 2\text{ B} = 147{,}456$ B per token — note $H_{kv}=8$, not $H=32$; the same GQA field, paying a second time.

(3) Full fine-tune memory — the memory-budget thread's climax. Standard bf16 mixed precision with AdamW stores 16 bytes per parameter (the ledger you built on page 18 — reopen it for the why of each row):

$8{,}190{,}735{,}360 \times 16\text{ B} = 131{,}051{,}765{,}760\text{ B} = \mathbf{131.05\ GB}$ (decimal) $= \mathbf{122.05\ GiB}$ (binary). Memorize 16P.

⚠️ This is STATE ONLY — activations are a separate line The 122.05 GiB above is weights + gradients + optimizer state. Activations are counted separately, always. With gradient checkpointing at $B=1, S=2048$ they add an estimated 2–6 GB [EST] on top; without checkpointing, far more. Never fold activations silently into the state number — it's the mistake that makes two blog posts look like they contradict each other.

Does it fit on your box? Measure it.

This is the question the whole course points at, and the honest version does not assert a budget — it hands you a script. Step one of it is one line:

Python
import torch
free, total = torch.cuda.mem_get_info()   # bytes, on your GB10
print(f"usable now: {free/2**30:.2f} GiB of {total/2**30:.2f} GiB")
state = 8_190_735_360 * 16            # full-FT state, bytes
print(f"full-FT state: {state/2**30:.2f} GiB")  # 122.05
⚠️ Published capacity is never usable capacity Measured on the learner's own DGX Spark (2026-07-16) [MEA-DEV]: usable MemTotal = 121.6875 GiB, not the 128 on the box. A firmware carveout eats 6.3125 GiB (4.9%) of the physical 128 GiB before a single byte is allocated. So the comparison the script prints for you is $$122.05\ \text{GiB (state)} \;-\; 121.6875\ \text{GiB (usable)} \;=\; \mathbf{-0.36\ GiB}.$$ A full fine-tune of an 8B model does not fit — by 0.3%, before activations. Not by a comfortable margin, not with room to spare: by a hair. You find this out by measuring, not by trusting the spec sheet — which is off by 4.9% before you start.
🔑 The hand-off to Part IV Full fine-tuning your 8B: 122 GiB of state, misses your box by a hair, before activations. LoRA: ~16 GB state, fits many times over. State-to-state that's 131.05 GB → 17.08 GB = 7.67×; counting only the trainable state it's 114.67 GB → 0.61 GB = 187× — and 187 is exactly the parameter ratio $8{,}190{,}735{,}360 / 43{,}646{,}976 = 187.7$. That gap is the entire reason this course's destination is reachable on hardware you own. The next Part is how.

A bonus number: your decode speed ceiling

At decode you read every weight for every token. The Spark's memory bandwidth is 273 GB/s [VP], so the time to stream 16.38 GB of bf16 weights once is the floor on one token:

✍️ 16.67 tok/s, and why quantizing quadruples it $16.38\ \text{GB} / 273\ \text{GB/s} = 60\ \text{ms/token} \Rightarrow \mathbf{16.67\ tok/s}$ — the ceiling. Realistic expectation is about 10.8 tok/s ($\times0.65$; the ceiling is 16.67, the expectation is 10.8 — say which is which). This is page 28's irony made numeric: at decode a transformer has RNN economics, bandwidth-bound. It's also why int4 (~4.10 GB → ~66.6 tok/s ceiling) roughly quadruples decode speed even though it changes nothing about the FLOPs. You've watched your tok/s jump when you quantize in ComfyUI — now you can predict it with one division. (Measured for real on page 43.)

Three ways the count gets misread

⚠️ “An 8B model needs 8 GB” That's roughly int8 weights only. bf16 inference is 16.4 GB + KV + activations; a full fine-tune is 131.05 GB. The multiplier from “the name” to “the memory” runs from ~0.5× (int4 weights) to 16× (full-FT state). The name tells you almost nothing until you multiply.
⚠️ “Parameter count tells you compute cost” For a dense model, roughly. For an MoE, catastrophically false — Kimi K2 is 1T parameters but activates ~32B per token. Compute tracks active parameters; memory tracks total. That's why the “397B-A17B” notation exists, and why you can't read FLOPs off a headline count (page 38).
⚠️ “Embedding params matter as much as the rest” The embeddings are 15.20% of Qwen3-8B, but they're a pure lookup table — $O(1)$ compute per token, not a matmul over the sequence. They cost memory, not FLOPs. That's exactly why $C \approx 6ND$ uses non-embedding $N$, and why cards report “non-embedding params” as a separate line (page 38).

The config-file parameter counter Live
Try it · code/count_params.py Run the whole beat on your box: load Qwen3-8B's config.json, compute 8,190,735,360 from the formula, read model.safetensors.index.json's total_size and divide by 2 to confirm the same integer, then print all three downstream numbers and run mem_get_info() so the 122.05-GiB-vs-your-box comparison is your measurement.
code/count_params.py
import json, torch

cfg = json.load(open("Qwen3-8B/config.json"))
d, L = cfg["hidden_size"], cfg["num_hidden_layers"]
H, Hkv = cfg["num_attention_heads"], cfg["num_key_value_heads"]
dh, dff, V = cfg["head_dim"], cfg["intermediate_size"], cfg["vocab_size"]
tied = cfg["tie_word_embeddings"]

p_attn = 2 * d * dh * (H + Hkv)          # 41,943,040
p_ffn  = 3 * d * dff                     # 150,994,944
p_norm = 2 * d + 2 * dh                 # 8,448
p_block = p_attn + p_ffn + p_norm         # 192,946,432
P = V * d + L * p_block + d + (0 if tied else V * d)
assert P == 8_190_735_360, P          # route 1: the formula

idx = json.load(open("Qwen3-8B/model.safetensors.index.json"))
total_bytes = idx["metadata"]["total_size"]   # 16,381,470,720
assert total_bytes // 2 == P              # route 2: the checkpoint itself

print(f"params        {P:,}")
print(f"bf16 weights  {P*2/1e9:.2f} GB")                 # 16.38
print(f"full-FT 16P   {P*16/1e9:.2f} GB = {P*16/2**30:.2f} GiB")  # 131.05 / 122.05
free, total = torch.cuda.mem_get_info()
print(f"your box      {total/2**30:.4f} GiB usable")   # measure it
