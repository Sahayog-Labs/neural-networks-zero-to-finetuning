Scaling laws: the most reliable fact in ML, and nobody knows why

Plot pretraining loss against training compute on log-log axes, across ten orders of magnitude of compute, and the points fall on a nearly straight line. Bigger models trained on more data reliably get lower loss, smoothly, with no plateau anyone has found yet. Hoffmann et al. (2022, the "Chinchilla" paper) fit that line with a three-term form:

$$L(N,D) = E + \frac{A}{N^{\alpha}} + \frac{B}{D^{\beta}}$$

The panel-1 demo below uses Hoffmann et al.'s own reported fit — $E\approx1.69$, $A\approx406.4$, $\alpha\approx0.34$, $B\approx410.7$, $\beta\approx0.28$ — to draw the curve live. Treat those five numbers honestly: they are one paper's fit to one training run's data, not constants of nature. Different data mixes, tokenizers, and architectures shift them. What survives across every scaling-law paper since is the functional form — a power law in each of $N$ and $D$ plus an irreducible floor — and that is the durable, reusable idea.

📐 Deriving the 6 in $C\approx6ND$ Training compute is usually quoted as $C\approx6ND$ FLOP. Don't take the 6 on faith — it falls out of counting multiply-adds:
  • Forward pass: each parameter does about one multiply and one add per token it touches $\Rightarrow \approx 2N$ FLOP/token.
  • Backward pass: autograd needs the gradient with respect to the input (to keep propagating backward) and the gradient with respect to the weight (to actually update it) — two more passes of forward-sized work $\Rightarrow \approx 2\times(2N) = 4N$ FLOP/token.
$2N + 4N = 6N$ FLOP/token, and over $D$ tokens, $C \approx 6ND$. The constant isn't magic — it's "one forward, one backward-through-inputs, one backward-through-weights," each costing $2N$.

Minimize $L(N,D)$ subject to a fixed compute budget $C=6ND$ and Hoffmann et al.'s numbers give the textbook answer: $D\approx20N$ — for every parameter, about 20 training tokens is compute-optimal. That's "Chinchilla-optimal," and it's the number every 2022-era retrospective quotes.

Then what actually happened: nobody trains Chinchilla-optimal. $D=20N$ minimizes training compute only. A model you'll serve billions of times pays inference cost forever, so real labs minimize training plus inference — which pushes toward smaller models trained much longer than 20 tokens/param. Meta's Llama-3-8B trained on roughly 15T tokens, about 1,900 tokens/param, ≈95× past Chinchilla-optimal — and it is a stronger 8B than a Chinchilla-optimal 8B would have been, because the extra tokens keep buying loss even after the "optimal-for-training-FLOPs" point. Chinchilla is the right answer to a question ("how do I minimize training compute for a fixed loss?") that almost nobody is actually asking anymore.

⚠️ "Chinchilla tells you how to train a model" It tells you how to minimize training FLOPs for a target loss — nothing about inference cost, which is what labs actually optimize for once a model will be served at scale. Every serious 2026 model is trained far past its Chinchilla-optimal token count, on purpose. Quoting "$D=20N$" as a recipe is the misconception; quoting it as "the FLOP-minimal point, which almost nobody targets" is the fact.
⚠️ "Scaling laws guarantee more compute makes a better model" They predict pretraining loss (cross-entropy, in nats) on the training distribution — that's it. The map from "lower loss" to "more useful capability" is empirical, messy, and not part of the law. A model can have lower loss and still be worse at the one task you care about.

★ The number that opens Part IV

Now point $C\approx6ND$ at the model this whole course is anchored to. Qwen3-8B: $N=6{,}946{,}071{,}552$ non-embedding parameters [DER, constants §1.2], trained on an estimated $D\approx3.6\times10^{13}$ tokens — [EST — ~36T tokens is unpublished; constants §5 labels it explicitly].

✍️ Worked example — how long would that take on your Spark? $$C \approx 6ND = 6\times6.95\times10^{9}\times3.6\times10^{13} = \mathbf{1.5\times10^{24}}\ \text{FLOP}$$ [EST — inherits the [EST] on $D$, constants §5] Divide by a compute rate to get time. Two honest ways to do that division:
  • Generous to the machine — assume the Spark's theoretical FP16-accumulate peak, 125 TFLOP/s [INF, constants §6.3], sustained at 100% utilization forever (it never is): $$\frac{1.5\times10^{24}}{1.25\times10^{14}} = 1.2\times10^{10}\ \text{s} = \mathbf{381\ years}$$ [DER, on an EST+INF input]
  • Realistic — the Spark's actual dense-bf16 training roofline is ~62 TFLOP/s [INF, constants §6.3], and real training runs hit maybe 40% of that, not 100%: $$\frac{1.5\times10^{24}}{0.4\times6.2\times10^{13}} \approx \mathbf{1{,}900\ years}$$ [INF, constants §5]
Notice which number is the lower bound: 381 years assumes the machine performs better than it ever will. That makes "at least four centuries" a floor, not an exaggeration — the honesty strengthens the conclusion rather than softening it.

Mixture of Experts: the FFN, chopped into specialists

You already counted it: the FFN is 78.26% of every transformer block and 66.4% of the whole model [THREAD:Qwen3-8B beat: FFN share, p.35/36 callback] — and it does something different for a Python token than for a Portuguese one. MoE's bet: instead of one FFN doing everything, build many smaller FFNs ("experts") and let a tiny learned router send each token to only a few of them.

$$\mathbf{y}=\sum_{e\,\in\,\text{TopK}(g(\mathbf{x}))}g_e(\mathbf{x})\,\text{FFN}_e(\mathbf{x}), \qquad g(\mathbf{x})=\text{softmax}(W_r\mathbf{x})$$

The routing is per-token, per-layer — not per-sequence, and not per-topic. Say it explicitly, because "expert" wrongly suggests "the French expert" or "the math expert." In practice a single sentence routes its tokens through a shifting, mostly-uninterpretable mix of experts, re-decided at every layer.

Most 2026 frontier MoEs also keep one shared expert that runs on every token in addition to the routed ones. The reasoning: some processing — basic grammar, syntax, "this is English" — is needed by everything, and making $K_E$ routed experts each independently rediscover it wastes capacity that a single always-on expert can hold instead.

⚠️ The honest MoE costs [THREAD:memory-ledger beat: MoE total-vs-active] Memory is total, not active. Kimi K2 is 1T parameters — roughly 1 TB even at fp8. You are not running it on a 128 GB box, full stop. MoE trades your memory for someone else's compute savings — a data-center optimization that is, for local inference on a fixed-memory box, actively hostile: at the same parameter footprint, a dense model usually beats an MoE, because none of your memory sits idle waiting to be routed to. Two more honest costs: load balancing is real and only partly solved (routers collapse onto a handful of favorite experts without an auxiliary loss discouraging it), and fine-tuning an MoE is harder than fine-tuning a dense model of the same active size — routing itself shifts under gradient updates, and individual experts can starve. Forward pointer to p.40: Hugging Face implements MoE experts as raw nn.Parameter tensors, not nn.Linear layers, so target_modules= "all-linear" (p.40's LoRA default) silently skips every expert. PEFT's target_parameters=[…] is what actually reaches them.
⚠️ "MoE models are just more efficient" More compute-efficient per active parameter, yes. Less memory-efficient per unit of capability — the total parameter count you must keep resident is the real cost, and it's the number MoE architectures inflate on purpose. It's the right trade for a data center serving millions of requests in parallel across many GPUs; it is close to the wrong trade for one box with a fixed, unified memory pool.

"Emergence": be honest about which half is real

The popular claim: some capabilities appear abruptly at a scale threshold — useless, then suddenly competent, with no warning in between. Schaeffer et al. (NeurIPS 2023) showed that much of that sharpness is a metric artifact, not a property of the model. Discontinuous metrics — exact-match, multiple-choice accuracy — manufacture cliffs: a model that gets 4 of 5 digits of an answer right still scores zero, identical to a model that gets 0 of 5. Swap in a continuous metric (edit distance, per-token log-likelihood) on the same models, and the cliff becomes a smooth, predictable ramp. This is the correction to the popular story, and it's the largely-accepted result — lead with it.

Panel 3 below reproduces the actual mechanism, not just the claim: an exact-match score on a task with $n$ independent steps (say, $n$ digits that must all be right) is the per-step accuracy raised to the $n$-th power, $p(x)^n$. Raising a smoothly-rising curve to a large power is exactly what manufactures a cliff out of a slope — no mystery, just an AND over many steps.

🔬 Deep dive: what the metric-artifact correction does not resolve

Two things survive the continuous-metric fix, and the honest page says so [VP]:

  1. Predictability in advance. Even granting that curves are smooth in hindsight, nobody reliably predicts which capability will appear at what scale before training the model. Smoothness after the fact is not the same claim as predictability beforehand.
  2. Bimodal outcomes persist. Recent work finds that across random seeds, some training runs land near full competence on a capability and others land near chance — a genuinely bimodal split — even measured with the same continuous metric that dissolves the sharp-cliff illusion. "You picked a bad metric" does not explain a split that survives switching metrics. Panel 3's second toggle reproduces this: identical smooth logistic curves, jittered per seed, with a fraction of seeds capped at a low asymptote no matter which metric you view them through.
🔑 The honest formulation to ship "Emergence" bundles two separate claims. "Curves have sharp discontinuities" is largely a metric artifact — measure continuously and the cliff becomes a slope. "We cannot predict which capabilities a bigger model will have" is not an artifact and is still true in 2026. Be skeptical of "emergent" when it means magic; take it seriously when it means we genuinely couldn't have called this in advance. And the field hasn't fully converged — bimodal outcomes survive the continuous-metric fix, and nobody has fully explained them.
⚠️ "Emergent abilities prove something deep about scale" Most of the sharpness is your ruler, not the model — an artifact of scoring an AND over many correct steps with a pass/fail metric. The unpredictability is real and currently unexplained. Keep the two claims apart; conflating them is how "emergence" became a word that means both "obviously a measurement bug" and "possibly the most interesting open question in the field," depending who's talking.
