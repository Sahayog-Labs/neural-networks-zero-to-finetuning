The Memory Ledger: 16 Bytes per Parameter, and Does It Fit on YOUR Box?

Backprop (page 14) makes one gradient per weight; page 15 showed why you also keep an fp32 master copy; page 17 named Adam's two moment buffers. This page adds them up into one number — the memory a full fine-tune actually costs — and then dares you to measure whether your own machine can hold it.

🧵 Thread [THREAD:MEM — the reality check] · [THREAD:QWEN]. This is the reality-check thread's home page. Every earlier beat pointed here: page 14's cached activations (BP3), page 15's fp32 master weights, page 17's Adam $m,v$. We turn them into a ledger, price it on our anchor model Qwen3-8B, and hold the result against hardware you own. The rule the whole page enforces: you do not get to assume how much memory you have — you measure it.
🔮 Predict first You are about to compute how much memory a full AdamW fine-tune of Qwen3-8B needs. Your box was sold as 128 GB. Before you run anything, commit to an answer: does it fit? Write the numbers in GiB, not GB — the two differ by 7%, and that gap is where this whole page lives. Then, further down, run the script and read your machine's actual usable memory off your own screen. Hold your guess until then.

Why one parameter costs sixteen bytes

A parameter is one number. Naively you would think training it costs the size of that number — 2 bytes in bf16. It costs eight times that, and every row of the reason is something you have already met.

[THREAD:CHAIN] callback: the gradient and fp32-master rows are not arbitrary. Backprop made the gradients (one per weight, page 14), and the float-that-isn't-zero (page 15) is why the master copy has to be fp32 — a bf16 master would silently swallow every small update. The ledger is the memory shadow of the last four pages. Confidence: [DER], constants.md §2.1.

⚠️ "16 B/param? I've read it's 4× the model, and also 8×. Which?" Both, and they are the same 16 bytes — a reader who skims two blog posts hits this apparent contradiction. 16 B/param is 4× the fp32 weight size (4 B/param) and 8× the bf16 weight size (2 B/param). Different denominators, one footprint. State which baseline you mean and the paradox evaporates.

The number: pricing Qwen3-8B

Qwen3-8B has exactly 8,190,735,360 parameters (page 36 counts every one; the checkpoint's own safetensors index confirms it to the byte). Multiply by 16:

✍️ Full-FT state of Qwen3-8B

$$8{,}190{,}735{,}360 \times 16\ \text{B} = 131{,}051{,}765{,}760\ \text{B} = \underbrace{131.05\ \text{GB}}_{\text{decimal},\ /10^9} = \underbrace{122.05\ \text{GiB}}_{\text{binary},\ /2^{30}}$$

Look at what dominates. Adam's two moment buffers alone — $m + v$, the 8 B/param of optimizer moments — come to 65.53 GB, exactly half the entire footprint (8 of the 16 bytes). Each single buffer (32.76 GB) is twice the size of the model's weights (16.38 GB). The thing you are training is a minority of what you are storing. [DER], constants.md §2.2.

⚠️ This is STATE only — activations are a separate line, always The 131.05 GB above is weights + gradients + optimizer state. It does not include activations — the forward values BP3 caches to compute weight gradients (page 14's memory villain). Those are a separate, separately-labelled line: [EST] roughly 2–6 GB at batch 1, sequence 2048 with gradient checkpointing, and far more without it. It is an estimate — this course has you measure it, never quotes it as a fact. Never fold activations silently into the state number. [THREAD:MEM]
📐 Units discipline — do not mix GB and GiB in one comparison Anything you compare against hardware capacity is GiB ($2^{30}$ B); a weight or optimizer figure quoted on its own is GB ($10^9$ B). $1\,\text{GiB} = 1.0737\,\text{GB}$. So "131.05 GB of state" is correct, and to ask whether it fits your box you first convert to 122.05 GiB and compare that to the machine's GiB. The single most expensive arithmetic error in this whole subject is a GB/GiB mismatch. constants.md §0.

Now stop computing and measure

You have the need: 122.05 GiB of state. The obvious next step is to compare it to your box's 128 GB — and that is the trap. "128 GB" is the number on the box, not the number the allocator hands you. Firmware and driver reserve a slice before you touch it, and that slice is invisible and unpublished. So we refuse to assert your budget. You measure it.

Whatever it said, notice the shape of the answer. The state you computed by hand is 122.05 GiB. A machine advertised at 128 GiB of usable memory would clear it with 5.95 GiB to spare — but published capacity is never usable capacity. The spec sheet is off by a firmware carveout of a few percent before you allocate a single byte, and 122.05 is close enough to the ceiling that a few percent is the whole story. That is why this page has you read your own MemTotal rather than trusting a marketing figure — and why the answer to the predict is, honestly, "measure it, then decide": the demo below lands the verdict on your number, not ours.

⚠️ "128 GB is what I have." Measure it. Usable MemTotal is materially less than the advertised figure, and the difference — a firmware/driver carveout — is invisible until you look. A course that told a man with the hardware in front of him what his hardware has is a course that gets caught. The number is yours to read.

The escape ladder

If full state does not fit — and on an 8B model against a real budget, it is a near-miss — you climb down a ladder, trading optimizer fidelity for bytes. Each rung is a real technique with its own later page.

Full → LoRA is 131.05 GB → 17.08 GB = 7.67× on total footprint. But the sharper sentence is about the trainable state alone (gradients + master + $m$ + $v$, the 14 B/param that scales with what you train): 114.67 GB → 0.61 GB = 187× — and 187 is exactly the parameter ratio $8{,}190{,}735{,}360 / 43{,}646{,}976 = 187.7$, because optimizer state is linear in trainable parameters.

🔑 LoRA compresses optimizer state, not the model LoRA is not a model compression technique. LoRA is an optimizer-state compression technique. The base model didn't shrink — all 16.38 GB of it is still resident. The trainable state shrank by 187×, which is exactly $8{,}190{,}735{,}360 \div 43{,}646{,}976$, because optimizer state is linear in trainable parameters. That's the whole trick. (The full LoRA math is a later trunk page; this is the foreshadow — hold the 187×.)
⚠️ "LoRA shrinks the model / saves activation memory." Neither. LoRA freezes the weights but still backprops through the entire network — the graph is the same length, so the activation cache (page 14's BP3) is unchanged between full FT and LoRA at the same batch and sequence length. That is why, in the demo, switching to LoRA leaves the activation band exactly where it was. What LoRA collapses is optimizer state, by 187×. A learner who measures LoRA's real footprint finds it sitting above 17.08 GB by precisely the activation term — and now you knew first.
⚠️ "Adam's state is a minor overhead." It is 50% of the full-FT footprint: $m + v$ = 65.53 GB of the 131.05 GB total. Each moment buffer alone is 4× the bf16 model. Optimizer state is not a rounding error on the weights — on a full fine-tune it is the memory.
📐 A principle both tracks reuse: fewer trainable params ⇒ higher learning rate The ledger's denominator — how many parameters you actually train — sets more than memory; it sets the learning rate. Full FT moves all 8.19 billion parameters, which already encode everything the model knows, so you tiptoe: $\eta \approx 1$–$2\times10^{-5}$. The fewer you train, the harder you can push each one: textual inversion trains ~768 numbers at $\eta \approx 5\times10^{-4}$–$5\times10^{-3}$; an LLM LoRA runs $10^{-4}$–$3\times10^{-4}$; a diffusion LoRA runs $\eta \approx 10^{-4}$ — not the $10^{-6}$ full-FT figure (that 100× confusion is a classic trap). One ledger, two consequences: bytes and step size.
🔬 Deep dive: why gradients accumulate by default, and how that becomes a memory trick

Page 15's zero_grad() lesson has a memory sequel. PyTorch accumulates gradients into .grad by default rather than overwriting — that is why you must zero them each step. But the same behaviour is a deliberate tool: run several small micro-batches, call .backward() on each without zeroing, and their gradients sum into one large effective batch — then step once. This is gradient accumulation, and it lets you train at a large effective batch size while only ever holding one micro-batch of activations in memory. The activation cache is the term that scales with batch size, so trading batch-as-one-shot for batch-as-accumulation is a pure memory win. The optimizer-state rows of the ledger are unchanged; only the activation line shrinks. It is the same accumulation semantics from page 15, read as a budget lever. [THREAD:MEM]

And the compute half of measure_your_box.py — the one we did not run here — comes back on page 43 to find your box's roofline: the ridge where a workload flips from memory-bound to compute-bound. We only foreshadow it; like your memory budget, the roofline is something you will measure, not assume.
