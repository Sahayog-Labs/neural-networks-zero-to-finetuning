Before you look at anything: what kind of network do you think does that reading — a bag-of-words embedder, a CNN, an RNN, or a full transformer language model? And roughly how big is it relative to the 32 B image model itself? Commit to both answers, then open the config on your disk.

You have spent one track learning to read an LLM's config — tokenization, GQA, RoPE, the transformer block, a KV cache — and another learning to read a diffusion model's — the VAE, flow matching, the DiT, cross-attention conditioning. This page is the moment the two readings collide. Not by analogy. On the bytes in ~/.cache/huggingface/hub/.

Your image model's prompt reader is a Mistral — a 24 B multimodal VLM, a full 40-layer transformer language model. Not a lookup table, not a CNN, not an RNN. That answers your prediction's first half. The second half: 24 B is about 0.75× the size of the 32 B image transformer it feeds — and roughly twice the size of the entire old FLUX.1 model (12 B) [VP, constants §9.6]. A whole language model, larger than yesterday's whole image model, sits in front just to read the prompt.

"The tracks grew back together" stops being a metaphor: the LLM track you just finished is the manual for the front half of the diffusion model you just fine-tuned.

THREAD · dot-product · beat FINAL The atom you met on p.3 — $\langle \mathbf q, \mathbf k\rangle$, one dot product per attention row — is running inside every one of this encoder's 40 layers. Reopen p.3 (the neuron's weighted sum) and p.29 (the first attention score): same atom, now doing the reading that binds text to your image. The dot product's thread ends here, inside your FLUX.2.

🎁 The GQA gift Qwen3-8B is 32 query heads / 8 KV heads. Mistral-3 is also 32 / 8. The exact GQA ratio you computed on p.30 — four query heads sharing each key/value head, a 4× cut on the KV cache — recurs, unchanged, inside your image model [VP, constants §1.1 vs §4.1]. You do not have to re-derive it. You already own it.

Translation note (notation §3.2): the config writes num_key_value_heads; papers write $n_{kv}$ or $H_{kv}$. The course writes $H_{kv}$ — and the KV formula uses $H_{kv}$, never $H$. Using $H=32$ here is the field's single most common cache-sizing error.

Worked: the encoder's own KV cache

Same formula as the LLM track, same warning — count $H_{kv}$, not $H$. First the shape ribbon, so the bytes are inescapable:

shape ribbon · Mistral-3 encoder, one layer
x            (B, S, d=5120)
W_k          (H_kv·d_head = 8·160 = 1280,  5120)   # (out, in) — ALWAYS
k = x @ W_k.T                        (B, S, 1280)
reshape      (B, S, H_kv=8, d_head=160)
cache K,V per token per layer = 2 · H_kv · d_head · b
                              = 2 · 8 · 160 · 2 B = 5120 B = 5 KiB
✍️ KV per token, all 40 layers [DER] $$\text{KV bytes} = 2\,L\,H_{kv}\,d_{head}\,b\,S\,B$$ Per token, per layer: $2\times 8\times 160\times 2 = \mathbf{5120\ B = 5\ KiB}$.
All 40 layers ($S=1$, $B=1$): $5120\times 40 = 204{,}800\ \text{B} = \mathbf{200\ KiB/token}$.

Your Qwen3-8B anchor was $2\times 8\times 128\times 2\times 36 = 147{,}456\ \text{B} = \mathbf{144\ KiB/token}$ [DER, constants §4]. So your image model's text encoder carries a heavier per-token KV than the LLM you fine-tuned: 200 vs 144 KiB/token — not because it has more heads (both are $H_{kv}=8$), but because it is deeper (40 vs 36 layers) and wider per head (160 vs 128).

How the text talks to the image

✍️ The conditioning width [VP, on‑disk] The FLUX.2 transformer's joint_attention_dim is 15360, and $$\frac{15360}{5120} = \mathbf{3}.$$ The DiT reads a conditioning tensor exactly 3× the Mistral hidden size wide. And its in_channels is 128 — verify it live: $32 \times 2^2 = 32\times 4 = \mathbf{128}$ (32 latent channels from the VAE, × 2×2 patch packing) [DER, constants §9.6]. The cross-attention that binds text to image — the one the trunk taught you was mask-agnostic and first-class (p.31, D-14) — takes its $K$ and $V$ from this Mistral encoder. A different modality on the key/value side, which is exactly the case cross-attention exists to handle. You did not learn a special "conditioning" mechanism for diffusion; you learned cross-attention on the trunk, and it already covered this.

THREAD · memory-ledger The ledger you started on p.18 (16 B/param) and carried through the KV cache on p.37 reaches all the way into your image model: this encoder has its own 200 KiB/token KV cache. The reality-check thread does not stop at the LLM.

🔑 Your scheduler, on your disk From scheduler/scheduler_config.json: your FLUX.2 sampler is a FlowMatchEulerDiscreteScheduler [VP, on‑disk] — the flow-match Euler step the diffusion track derived (p.57), sitting on your machine. Its num_train_timesteps=1000 is vestigial: an indexing habit inherited from DDPM tooling. Flow matching is continuous in $t\in[0,1]$; there is no 1000-step chain to walk.

Translation note (notation §6): a num_train_timesteps of 1000 in a flow-matching config does not mean a 1000-step DDPM Markov chain. Data is at $t=0$, noise at $t=1$, and you choose how many Euler steps to take (your FLUX.2-dev-Turbo takes very few). The 1000 is a leftover integer, not a step count.

⚠️ The belief this page kills "The text encoder is just a CLIP-style embedding lookup, or a bag of words." That was true for CLIP, and half-true for T5. It is false for FLUX.2: the prompt reader is a full 40-layer autoregressive-class transformer — multimodal, with attention, RoPE, GQA, and a KV cache. This matters because a learner who thinks $Q,K,V$ are "three memories" or that conditioning is a bespoke diffusion trick cannot understand cross-attention, and therefore cannot understand conditioning at all (D-14, the trunk's highest-stakes ask). This is where that belief dies — not on a slide, but on the config in your own cache directory.
🔬 Deep dive: FLUX.1 vs FLUX.2 — why the text encoder ballooned from 4.7 B to 24 B

FLUX.1-dev: a T5-XXL (4.7 B) plus a CLIP-L, encoder width $d=3072$, 12 B MMDiT, guidance-distilled. FLUX.2-dev: a 32 B MMDiT plus a Mistral-3 24 B VLM encoder, a 32-channel VAE at spatial factor $f=8$ (6.0× compression), 4096 latent tokens after 2×2 patchify at width 128 — all [VP], constants §9.6.

The trend is unmistakable and it is the whole reason this page exists: image models are putting a complete language model in the front. Reading the prompt well turned out to be worth more parameters than the entire previous image model. The reveal above stands without this history, but the history is the "why."

Verify it yourself, on your disk

Try it Two shell one-liners prove the reveal in ten seconds; code/verify_flux2_encoder.py then computes the 200 KiB/token KV and prints it beside Qwen3-8B's 144 KiB/token. Nothing is downloaded — it reads the FLUX.2-dev config already in your HF cache.
bash
D=~/.cache/huggingface/hub/models--black-forest-labs--FLUX.2-dev/snapshots
cat $D/*/model_index.json | python3 -c "import json,sys; print(json.load(sys.stdin)['text_encoder'])"
# -> ['transformers', 'Mistral3ForConditionalGeneration']
cat $D/*/text_encoder/config.json | python3 -c "import json,sys; c=json.load(sys.stdin); t=c.get('text_config',c); print(t['hidden_size'], t['num_hidden_layers'], t['num_attention_heads'], t['num_key_value_heads'])"
# -> 5120 40 32 8
code/verify_flux2_encoder.py
import json, glob, os

root = os.path.expanduser(
    "~/.cache/huggingface/hub/models--black-forest-labs--FLUX.2-dev/snapshots")
cfg_path = glob.glob(os.path.join(root, "*", "text_encoder", "config.json"))[0]
with open(cfg_path) as f:
    c = json.load(f)

# Mistral3ForConditionalGeneration nests the LM fields under text_config,
# alongside vision_config. Fall back to the top level for text-only layouts.
t = c.get("text_config", c)
for key in ("hidden_size", "num_hidden_layers", "num_attention_heads",
            "num_key_value_heads", "rope_theta"):
    print(f"{key:24s} {t.get(key)}")

def kv_kib_per_token(L, H_kv, d_head, b=2):
    return 2 * L * H_kv * d_head * b / 1024

d_head = t["hidden_size"] // t["num_attention_heads"]   # 5120 // 32 = 160
mistral = kv_kib_per_token(t["num_hidden_layers"], t["num_key_value_heads"], d_head)
qwen    = kv_kib_per_token(L=36, H_kv=8, d_head=128)
print(f"Mistral-3 encoder KV: {mistral:.0f} KiB/token")   # 200
print(f"Qwen3-8B        KV: {qwen:.0f} KiB/token")         # 144

If a key is ever missing, print the whole config first rather than hard-failing — some transformers versions flatten the layout. The c.get("text_config", c) fallback handles both.
