- **Scaling laws (brief §11).** Loss falls as a straight line on a log-log plot across ten orders of compute — the most reliable quantitative fact in ML, and nobody knows why. Chinchilla form: $L(N,D)=E+A/N^{\alpha}+B/D^{\beta}$ (Symbol Ledger; $N$ = non-embedding params, $D$ = training tokens, $E$ = irreducible loss ≈ text entropy). **Present the fitted constants as "one paper's fit, dataset-specific," not constants of nature** [M — Hoffmann et al. 2022: $E\approx1.69$, $A\approx406.4,\alpha\approx0.34$, $B\approx410.7,\beta\approx0.28$; the *functional form* is the durable part]. # 39 — 39-rank-and-svd.html  (D-21: 39)

**Title:** "Rank, SVD & Low-Rank Approximation" · **Part IV — Adaptation** · build **O** · no track class (trunk; D-12 ruling — this is the page foundations cut, rebuilt).

**Owns the gap foundations cut (D-12).** Build **from the dot product**, not from eigenvalues. Nothing before this needs eigen-anything; do not import a spectral-theorem chapter. The one load-bearing claim under the whole course's destination — "the *update* is low-rank" — gets a full page with the demo, immediately before LoRA.

**Learning objectives**
1. Define the rank of a matrix as the number of independent directions it acts in — via dot products / outer products, no eigenvalues.
2. Read an SVD $W=U\Sigma V^\top$ as a sum of rank-1 outer products weighted by singular values $\sigma_i$.
3. Quantify a rank-$r$ approximation's storage ($r(d_{out}+d_{in})$ vs $d_{out}d_{in}$) and error, and read a scree plot.
4. Distinguish "the *matrix* is low-rank" (usually **false** for $W_0$) from "the *update* is low-rank" (LoRA's actual, testable claim about $\Delta W$).

**PREDICT (hook: he has a geometric picture from the attention page).**
> "A real 256×256 grayscale photo has 65,536 numbers. If you keep only the 8 most important 'directions' of that matrix and throw the rest away, do you get (a) unrecognizable noise, (b) a blurry-but-clearly-the-photo, (c) a perfect copy? Commit." (Answer: (b) — resolved by the demo.)

**Section outline**
- **Intuition (inline, one sentence).** "A matrix is a machine that moves every point at once; its *rank* is how many independent directions it can actually move things in — everything else it does is a combination of those." (Ties to notation §4.1's "W's rows are where the basis vectors land.")
- **From dot product to rank (inline, load-bearing).** An outer product $\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^\top$ is a rank-1 matrix: every row is $\mathbf v$ scaled, every column is $\mathbf u$ scaled — one direction in, one direction out. Rank = the fewest rank-1 pieces you must add to rebuild the matrix. `[THREAD:dot-product]` — "the same *how much of this is in that?* from p.13, now assembling matrices."
- **The SVD (`.box rule`, with Symbol Ledger — >3 symbols).** $$W = U\Sigma V^\top = \sum_{i=1}^{\min(d_{out},d_{in})} \sigma_i\, \mathbf u_i \mathbf v_i^\top,\qquad \sigma_1\ge\sigma_2\ge\dots\ge 0.$$ Ledger: $W$ $(d_{out},d_{in})$ any real matrix; $U$ $(d_{out},d_{out})$ orthonormal output directions (from the factorization); $\Sigma$ diagonal singular values $\ge0$ descending; $V^\top$ $(d_{in},d_{in})$ orthonormal input directions; $\sigma_i$ how much the $i$-th direction is stretched. **Shape check** in the ribbon: $(d_{out},d_{out})(d_{out},d_{in})(d_{in},d_{in})=(d_{out},d_{in})$ ✓. Keep the *existence* of the SVD as a stated fact (every real matrix has one); push the proof to a deepdive.
- **The rank-$r$ approximation & the storage count (`.box worked`).** $$W_r = \sum_{i=1}^{r}\sigma_i \mathbf u_i \mathbf v_i^\top,\qquad \text{stored numbers } = r(d_{out}+d_{in}+1)\ \text{vs}\ d_{out}\,d_{in}.$$ Worked on the demo's 256×256 image: $r=8 \Rightarrow 8(256+256+1)=4104$ numbers $=\mathbf{6.3\%}$ of 65,536, **recognizable**; $r=32 \Rightarrow 25\%$, relative error $\approx3\%$, **hard to distinguish** (D-12 numbers — quote them). Eckart–Young ("$W_r$ is the *best* rank-$r$ approximation in Frobenius norm") stated as a fact, proof in deepdive.
- **The scree plot & the punchline (`.box key`).** "Real matrices are approximately low-rank: the information isn't spread evenly across 65,536 numbers — it's concentrated in a few dozen directions. That is the entire LoRA hypothesis, and you just verified it on a photograph." But then the honest correction, inline: **it is a claim about $\Delta W$, not $W_0$.**
- **Misconception (`.box warn`): "the pretrained model is low-rank."** FALSE. A real slice of $W_0$ (Qwen3 `q_proj`) is **nearly full rank** — its scree plot decays slowly, and a rank-16 approximation of it *destroys the model*. What is low-rank is $\Delta W$, the *change* a fine-tune makes: its singular values fall off a cliff. This distinction is the whole idea and the thing learners get wrong (llm-finetuning §9). Corrected by direct observation in the demo, not by assertion.
- **Deepdive (collapsible): "Why every real matrix has an SVD, and why $W_r$ is optimal."** Eckart–Young sketch; the connection $\sigma_i^2 = $ eigenvalues of $W^\top W$ (for the reader who *does* remember eigenvalues — offered as a translation, not required). Strictly optional (anti-rule: nothing later depends on it).

**Section outline**
- **Intuition (inline).** "A pretrained weight matrix is 16 million numbers clustered near zero in a bell curve. You don't need 16 bits to say 'a bit left of centre' — you need about 4, *if* you place the 16 levels smartly and re-anchor the scale every 16–64 weights so one outlier can't ruin the neighbourhood." The load-bearing half is the re-anchoring; blockwise scaling *is* modern quantization, the bit-width is almost a detail.
- **Floats: range vs precision (`.box rule`, table).** sign / exponent (range) / mantissa (precision). fp32 (8 exp, 23 mant, ~1e-7 rel); **bf16 (8 exp — fp32's range — 7 mant, ~1e-2)**; fp16 (5 exp, **max 65,504**, 10 mant); fp8 E4M3 (4 exp, max ~448). **The one thing to remember:** bf16 has fp32's *range* and fewer mantissa bits; fp16 has smaller *range* and needs a GradScaler (gradients underflow below ~6e-8 to zero). **Use bf16 on the Spark, always** — mention GradScaler once, in the past tense (constants §9.7; tooling §7.4). fp16 max **65,504** and fp32 epsilon **1.19e-7** cited [VP] (constants §9.3).
- **The general form (`.box rule`).** $$w \approx s\cdot q,\quad q\in\{16\text{ levels}\},\ s=\text{scale (per block)};\qquad \text{eff. bits/param} = \text{bits}(q) + \frac{\text{bits}(s)}{\text{block size}}.$$
- **★ QLoRA's NF4 budget — the exact derivation (`.box worked`; constants §3, cite [VP]; the 0.53 is REFUTED — there is NO range).** NF4 weight = 4 bits. First-level fp8 absmax scale per 64-block (with DQ): $8/64 = 0.125$ bits. - **Why NF4 and not INT4 (inline).** Pretrained weights are ~$\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2)$, so NF4 places its 16 levels at the **quantiles of a standard normal** — each code equally likely, dense near zero where the mass is. - **The 2026 format table (`.box rule`; constants §9.7, cite tags).** bf16 [VP] · fp16 (never on Blackwell) [VP] · FP8 E4M3 (0.5–2% degradation [EST]) · **MXFP4** (4-bit E2M1, block 32, **E8M0 power-of-two scale**) [VP] · **NVFP4** (4-bit E2M1, block **16**, FP8 micro-scale + fp32 global; **88% lower quantization error than MXFP4** [EST — vendor, label it]) · NF4 (~4.13–4.25 bits, block 64, a *training* format not a serving format) [VP]. **`.box warn`:** the "AWQ 95% / GGUF 92% / GPTQ 90%" retention figures are SEO-grade, mutually inconsistent — **do NOT print them as facts**; the *ordering* is probably right; measure them yourself (exercise).
