Downhill: Gradient Descent and the Loss Landscape

Page 12 built the loss — a single number that is small when the model is right. This page turns that number into a landscape over parameter space, and gives you the one rule the whole course runs on: step downhill, a little, over and over. Then we find the exact learning rate at which "a little" becomes "too much," and watch a network fly apart.

The loss is a landscape

Fix a dataset. Every setting of the parameters $\theta$ produces one loss value $\mathcal L(\theta)$. Sweep $\theta$ over all its possibilities and you trace out a surface: a height $\mathcal L$ above every point in parameter space. Training is a marble on that surface, and the only move it knows is roll downhill. For a real network the space has millions of axes; we will draw it with two, because the geometry that matters — which way is down, how far to step — is already visible in two.

"Downhill" has a precise meaning. At a point $\theta$, the gradient $\nabla_\theta\mathcal L$ is the vector of partial slopes; it points in the direction of steepest increase. So the steepest decrease is the exact opposite, $-\nabla_\theta\mathcal L$. That single fact is the entire algorithm.

The update rule

📐 Gradient descent $$\theta_{k+1} \;=\; \theta_k \;-\; \eta\,\mathbf g_k, \qquad \mathbf g_k = \nabla_\theta\mathcal L(\theta_k)$$

$\theta_k$all parameters at optimizer step $k$$(P,)$current state
$k$the optimizer step — a counter, $0,1,2,\dots$scalarindex, not time
$\mathbf g_k$the gradient of the loss at $\theta_k$$(P,)$ — same shape as $\theta$backprop (page 14)

Read it left to right: take the gradient, flip its sign, scale it by $\eta$, add it to where you are. Repeat. Every optimizer in Part II — momentum, Adam, AdamW — is a refinement of this one line, and none of them changes its shape.

🔤 Translation note (notation §3.2) Optimisation papers write this step index as $t$: $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta\nabla\mathcal L(\theta_t)$. This course reserves $t$ for the diffusion timestep (you will meet it in Part VI), so our step index is $k$. When you read a paper, mentally map its $t$ to our $k$. A bonus falls out of this: because $k$ counts optimizer steps, "schedule by step, not by epoch" (page 21) becomes something the symbols themselves enforce.

Why $-\nabla\mathcal L$ is the steepest way down

Take one small step $\mathbf u$ from $\theta$. A first-order Taylor expansion says the loss changes by

$$\mathcal L(\theta + \mathbf u) \;\approx\; \mathcal L(\theta) \;+\; \mathbf g^\top\mathbf u.$$

Among all steps $\mathbf u$ of a fixed length, which one makes $\mathbf g^\top\mathbf u$ as negative as possible — i.e. drops the loss most? The dot product $\mathbf g^\top\mathbf u = \lVert\mathbf g\rVert\, \lVert\mathbf u\rVert\cos\vartheta$ is smallest when $\cos\vartheta = -1$, that is, when $\mathbf u$ points exactly opposite to $\mathbf g$. So the best direction is $-\mathbf g/\lVert\mathbf g\rVert$, and the update rule steps that way. [THREAD:CHAIN] — this is the same dot-product-as-alignment fact from page 3, now choosing a search direction.

🔬 Deep dive: the Cauchy–Schwarz proof that $-\mathbf g/\lVert\mathbf g\rVert$ is the fastest-descending unit step

We want the unit vector $\mathbf u$ ($\lVert\mathbf u\rVert = 1$) that minimises the first-order change $\mathbf g^\top\mathbf u$. The Cauchy–Schwarz inequality bounds any dot product:

$$\lvert\mathbf g^\top\mathbf u\rvert \;\le\; \lVert\mathbf g\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf u\rVert \;=\; \lVert\mathbf g\rVert,$$

so $\mathbf g^\top\mathbf u$ lives in $[-\lVert\mathbf g\rVert,\, +\lVert\mathbf g\rVert]$. The lower end, $-\lVert\mathbf g\rVert$, is reached exactly when $\mathbf u$ is antiparallel to $\mathbf g$, i.e. $\mathbf u^\star = -\mathbf g/\lVert\mathbf g\rVert$. No other unit step lowers the loss as fast — that is what "steepest descent" means, made precise.

What the learning rate does — four signatures

The direction is settled: always $-\mathbf g$. The only free choice left in the update rule is how far — the scalar $\eta$. Get it wrong and the marble either crawls, chatters, or launches. Each failure has a shape you can read straight off the loss curve, and page 24 will use these four shapes as a debugging table.

$\eta$ regimeLoss-curve signatureWhat is happening
Too smallsmooth, monotone, glacial — barely tilts downevery step is safe but tiny; you will run out of patience before it converges
About rightbrisk fall, then a plateaufast progress into a basin, then diminishing returns near the bottom
Too bigdrops, then bounces on a plateau and never settleseach step overshoots the bottom and lands on the far wall — oscillation
Unstablerises, then NaN within a few stepseach step overshoots by more than the last; the error compounds and blows up

The trap worth naming: too-small and a dead network look identical — both give a flat, unmoving loss. The cure for one (raise $\eta$) is poison for the other. That ambiguity is exactly why the next section pins down the boundary between "safe" and "unstable" with an actual number.

The critical learning rate, exactly

On a quadratic bowl the stability boundary is not folklore — it is a formula. Take the demo's ravine, a bowl whose steepest-curving direction has curvature $\lambda_{\max} = 20$. Along that direction the error after one step is multiplied by $\lvert 1 - \eta\lambda_{\max}\rvert$. Descent is stable only while that multiplier is below 1, which happens for $0 < \eta < 2/\lambda_{\max}$.

✍️ Worked: $\eta_{\text{crit}}$ and the error multiplier ($\lambda_{\max}=20$)

The critical step (constants.md §9.3, [DER]):

$$\eta_{\text{crit}} \;=\; \frac{2}{\lambda_{\max}} \;=\; \frac{2}{20} \;=\; \mathbf{0.1}\ \text{ exactly.}$$

Per-step error multiplier $m(\eta) = \lvert 1 - \eta\lambda_{\max}\rvert$ in the stiff direction:

$\eta$$m = \lvert 1 - 20\eta\rvert$Behaviour
$0.05$$\lvert 1 - 1.0\rvert = \mathbf{0.00}$collapses to the axis in one step, then crawls in the shallow direction
$0.09$$\lvert 1 - 1.8\rvert = \mathbf{0.80}$converges — error shrinks 20% per step
$0.10$$\lvert 1 - 2.0\rvert = \mathbf{1.00}$marginal — oscillates forever at constant amplitude
$0.15$$\lvert 1 - 3.0\rvert = \mathbf{2.00}$diverges — error doubles every step

Above $\eta_{\text{crit}} = 0.1$ the multiplier exceeds 1 and the iterate flies apart — you will see this land exactly at 0.1 in the demo. This threshold reappears on page 17, where momentum lets you push past the naive limit.

What the landscape actually looks like at scale

🔑 Saddles, not traps A generation of neural-net lore warned about "getting stuck in a bad local minimum." At scale that fear is largely obsolete. In a high-dimensional loss surface, a point where every one of millions of axes curves upward (a true local minimum) is astronomically rare; overwhelmingly, a flat spot is a saddle — up in some directions, down in others — and there is always a way out. Moreover, the many minima that gradient descent actually reaches on an overparameterised network are mostly about equally good. The real obstacles are saddle plateaus that slow you down, ill-conditioning (the ravine you are about to see), and bad learning rates — not a landscape full of traps. Say it plainly: you are not going to get stuck in a pit.

Three traps

⚠️ "The gradient points at the minimum." It does not. The gradient is perpendicular to the local contour line. On a stretched valley the contours are long ellipses, and the perpendicular to a long ellipse points mostly across the valley, not along it toward the bottom. That resolves the PREDICT: "downhill" is across the valley, so the marble zigzags wall-to-wall while inching toward the minimum. This wasted cross-motion is precisely the problem momentum (page 17) is built to fix.
⚠️ "A smaller learning rate is always safer." Safer against divergence, yes — but a too-small $\eta$ stalls indefinitely and produces a flat loss curve that is indistinguishable from a dead or broken network. "Nothing is happening" is not a safe state; it is a failure mode that merely looks calm.
⚠️ "Training gets stuck in bad local minima." Saddle points dominate high-dimensional surfaces; genuine local minima are rare, and the ones SGD finds on an overparameterised model are mostly equivalent in quality. The 1990s intuition — from tiny networks — does not transfer to the scale you will actually train at.
