Probability, the Gaussian & Sampling You Can Differentiate Through

A distribution is a shape saying which values are likely; an expectation is estimated by averaging samples; and the one trick $x=\mu+\sigma\varepsilon$ lets you backpropagate through a random draw. Hold that last one — the entire diffusion forward process is it, wearing new letters.

🔮 Predict first You draw one sample from a 4096-dimensional standard Gaussian $\mathcal N(0, I)$. The single highest-density point of that bell is the origin. How far from the origin does your sample actually land? [right at the origin / about distance 64 / about distance 1]. Commit to one, then run Panel C below.

A distribution, an expectation, a sample

Strip the jargon to three pictures. A distribution $p(x)$ is a shape over an axis: taller where values are more likely. An expectation $\mathbb E[X]$ is that shape's balance point — where it would sit on a pivot. A sample is reaching in and grabbing one value, more often from where the shape is tall.

$$\mathbb E[X]=\sum_x x\,p(x)\quad\text{or}\quad\int x\,p(x)\,dx, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=\mathbb E\big[(X-\mathbb E[X])^2\big]=\mathbb E[X^2]-\mathbb E[X]^2.$$

🔑 Key idea — an expectation is estimated by averaging samples $$\mathbb E[f(X)]\approx\frac1N\sum_{n=1}^{N}f(x_n),\qquad x_n\sim p.$$ This is what a mini-batch is. The true loss is an expectation over your whole dataset; a batch of $B$ examples is a $B$-sample Monte-Carlo estimate of it. That is the entire reason you can train on batches at all. How much the estimate wobbles — the $1/\sqrt B$ law, and why that noise turns out to be a feature — is page 16; here just hold that a batch is a sample average.

The Gaussian — because diffusion is built from it

One distribution earns its own section, because you will meet it on every page of the diffusion track. The Gaussian (normal) density is

$$p(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).$$

SymbolIsShape / unitsFrom
$\mu$mean — where the bell is centredunits of $x$a chosen parameter
$\sigma$standard deviation — the bell's widthunits of $x$a chosen parameter
$\sigma^2$varianceunits of $x^2$$\sigma$ squared
$x$the value whose likelihood you're reading offunits of $x$the input to $p$

The width sets the famous coverage rule — derivable by integrating the bell ([DER]): $\pm1\sigma$ holds 68.3 % of the mass, $\pm2\sigma$ holds 95.4 %, $\pm3\sigma$ holds 99.7 %. In several dimensions the only version this course ever needs is the isotropic multivariate Gaussian $\mathcal N(\mathbf x;\boldsymbol\mu,\sigma^2 I)$: a round fuzzy ball, the same spread in every direction, no correlations between axes. Its covariance $\Sigma=\sigma^2 I$ has no off-diagonal structure — which is exactly why this course never needs eigen-decomposition of a covariance: there is nothing to diagonalize.

🔑 Key idea — the reparameterization trick: sampling you can differentiate through

Drawing a Gaussian sample is the same as scaling and shifting a standard one:

$$x\sim\mathcal N(\mu,\sigma^2)\iff x=\mu+\sigma\varepsilon,\qquad \varepsilon\sim\mathcal N(0,1).$$

Sample the randomness once, from a fixed standard bell, then shift by $\mu$ and stretch by $\sigma$ — a plain deterministic map. Here is why that matters for training. You cannot backpropagate through "draw a sample from a distribution whose parameters depend on $\theta$" — a draw is not a function of $\mu$, so there is no derivative to take. The trick moves the randomness out of the path: $\varepsilon$ becomes an external input, rolled once and held fixed, and $x=\mu+\sigma\varepsilon$ is now an ordinary differentiable function with $\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial\mu}=1$ and $\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial\sigma}=\varepsilon$. The dice are still rolled — just outside the graph. Gradients flow straight through.

Announce the payoff without spending it: this exact move is (a) how you sample a Gaussian in code, (b) how a VAE gets gradients through its sampling step (page 53), and (c) literally the diffusion forward process $\mathbf x_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf x_0+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\boldsymbol\epsilon$. You will recognize that on page 54 as an old friend with new letters: $\mu=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbf x_0$, $\sigma=\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}$.

Concentration of measure: a high-D Gaussian is a shell, not a ball

Now the fact the PREDICT asked about. Take one draw $\mathbf x\sim\mathcal N(0,I_d)$ and measure its distance from the origin, $\lVert\mathbf x\rVert=\sqrt{\sum_i x_i^2}$. It is a sum of $d$ independent unit-variance terms, so it concentrates hard ([DER]):

$$\mathbb E\,\lVert\mathbf x\rVert\approx\sqrt{d},\qquad \operatorname{std}\lVert\mathbf x\rVert\approx\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\approx0.71.$$

ε (d,) one standard-normal draw per axis μ, σ scalars the knobs (may depend on θ) x = μ + σ·ε (d,) a plain differentiable function of μ, σ ‖x‖ scalar concentrates at √d (d = 4096 → 64.0)

At $d=4096$ that is $\sqrt{4096}=\mathbf{64}$ with a spread of only about $0.71$. Essentially no sample lands near the origin — even though the origin is the densest single point. All the volume is out in a thin spherical shell at radius $\sqrt d$. This is the same $\mathcal N(0,1/d)$ arithmetic you saw on page 03, where two random 4096-D vectors were almost always orthogonal: high dimensions are geometrically weird in one consistent way. It is why diffusion starts sampling from a shell of noise rather than from a point, and why "the model just outputs the mean" is not a thing that happens.

📐 Hold this for page 12 — MSE is secretly a Gaussian Plain squared-error loss (MSE) is exactly the negative log-likelihood of a Gaussian with fixed variance — drop $\mathcal N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ into $-\ln p(x)$ and the $(x-\mu)^2$ falls straight out. That is how the LLM track's cross-entropy and the diffusion track's MSE turn out to be one principle. The derivation is page 12; here just hold that "MSE" and "a Gaussian" are the same story.
⚠️ Watch out — four traps that bite when you read a diffusion paper
  • "The CLT means my data is Gaussian." No. The Gaussian is chosen in diffusion for two engineering reasons — it is closed under addition (a sum of Gaussians is Gaussian, which is what makes the closed-form $\mathbf x_t$ from $\mathbf x_0$ possible) and its score has a clean form. A design decision, not a discovered fact about images.
  • "100× the samples, 100× the accuracy." It is $1/\sqrt N$: 100× the samples buys 10× the precision. (Page 16 makes this the mini-batch story.)
  • "Variance and std are interchangeable." Units differ — $\sigma$ carries $x$'s units, $\sigma^2$ carries them squared. This bites directly: diffusion papers write $\beta_t$ (variances) and $\sigma_t$ (stds) and rarely say which.
  • "Sampling means the model is uncertain." Sampling is a decision procedure applied to the model's deterministic output distribution — the same point as page 01's temperature knob.
🔬 Deep dive: why a high-D Gaussian is a soap bubble

Write $\lVert\mathbf x\rVert^2=\sum_{i=1}^{d}x_i^2$. Each $x_i^2$ has mean $1$ (unit variance, zero mean) and is independent of the others. By the law of large numbers the average $\frac1d\sum x_i^2\to1$, so $\lVert\mathbf x\rVert^2\approx d$ and $\lVert\mathbf x\rVert\approx\sqrt d$. The relative spread shrinks like $1/\sqrt d$: at $d=4096$ the shell is razor-thin around radius $64$. The density is still highest at the origin, but there is almost no volume there — volume is what a sample lives in. This is the whole intuition; the distance number in the section above is the load-bearing fact.

Try it — code/reparam.py Draw $\mathcal N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ two ways and confirm the histograms match — then the payoff: make $\mu,\sigma$ require gradients, build $x=\mu+\sigma\varepsilon$ with a detached $\varepsilon$, and backprop through the sample. Runs on CPU in under a second.
Python
import torch

torch.manual_seed(0)
mu, sigma, n = 1.0, 2.0, 100_000

# two ways to draw N(mu, sigma^2) — they agree
a = mu + sigma * torch.randn(n)          # reparameterized
b = torch.normal(mu, sigma, (n,))          # direct draw
print(a.mean().item(), a.std().item())    # ~1.0, ~2.0
print(b.mean().item(), b.std().item())    # ~1.0, ~2.0

# now the payoff: gradients flow through the sample
mu = torch.tensor(1.0, requires_grad=True)
sig = torch.tensor(2.0, requires_grad=True)
eps = torch.randn(4)                       # external, no grad — rolled once
x = mu + sig * eps                        # a differentiable function of mu, sig
x.sum().backward()
print(mu.grad)     # tensor(4.)  =  sum of dx/dmu = 1 each
print(sig.grad)    # tensor(eps.sum())  =  dx/dsig = eps

You just backpropagated through randomness. That single trick is the whole VAE and the whole diffusion forward process.
