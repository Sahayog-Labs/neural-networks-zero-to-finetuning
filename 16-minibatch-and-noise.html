Batch, Mini-Batch, Stochastic — and Why Noise Is a Feature

Page 15's zero_grad() demo already trained on mini-batches without saying so out loud. This page says it out loud: every step you've taken since page 14 has secretly been a choice about how many examples to average before you move — and that choice is not just an efficiency knob. The noise it introduces is one of the reasons neural networks generalize at all.

Three regimes, one update rule

The update rule from page 13 never changed — $\theta_{k+1} = \theta_k - \eta\,\mathbf g_k$ — only what $\mathbf g_k$ is an average over changes:

  • Full batch. $\mathbf g_k$ is the exact gradient over all $N$ examples. One step costs a full pass; one step per epoch. Expensive, and — you'll see below — not actually the fastest way to train.
  • Stochastic (SGD, literally). $\mathbf g_k$ is the gradient of a single example. Maximal noise, maximal steps per epoch.
  • Mini-batch. The universal compromise: $B$ examples, typically $B\in[8,\ \text{a few}\times10^3]$. Almost everything you will ever train — including your LoRA runs later in this course — uses this regime.
📐 Epoch ≠ step An epoch is one full pass over the dataset. A step is one optimizer update. With batch size $B$ and $N$ examples, one epoch is $\lceil N/B\rceil$ steps — so the two only coincide at full batch. LLM pretraining runs are frequently less than one epoch over a trillion-token corpus (there is no "second pass" to speak of), which is exactly why every schedule in this course — warmup, cosine decay, page 21 — is indexed by step $k$, never by epoch. "Epoch" is a batch-size-1990s habit that stops meaning much once the dataset is bigger than any number of epochs you can afford.

Where the $1/B$ lives

Notation §2, stated once and held for the rest of the course: $\mathcal L$ is the loss of one example; $J$ is what you actually minimize each step, the average over the batch. The gradient you step with is the average of the per-example gradients — not their sum:

$$J = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}\mathcal L_b, \qquad \mathbf g_k=\nabla_\theta J=\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}\nabla_\theta\mathcal L_b$$

This $1/B$ is where a very common learning-rate bug is born. If a framework accidentally sums the per-example losses instead of averaging them, the gradient it hands the optimizer is $B$ times too large — and the step behaves as if you'd quietly multiplied your learning rate by $B$.

✍️ Worked example — the summed-loss trap With $B$ example losses, $\nabla_\theta J=\frac1B\sum_b\nabla_\theta\mathcal L_b$, but a summed loss $S=\sum_b\mathcal L_b$ gives $\nabla_\theta S=\sum_b\nabla_\theta\mathcal L_b = B\cdot\nabla_\theta J$ — exactly $B$ times larger, for any $B$. So a step $\theta\leftarrow\theta-\eta\nabla_\theta S$ moves exactly as far as a correct step with an inflated learning rate $\eta_{\text{eff}}=\eta B$. Concretely: intend $\eta=0.001$, batch $B=32$, and sum instead of mean — every step actually behaves like $\eta_{\text{eff}} = 0.001 \times 32 = \mathbf{0.032}$, thirty-two times too large. This is precisely why PyTorch's built-in losses default to reduction='mean'.

Why noise is a FEATURE

The instinct is to treat mini-batch noise as a tax you pay for not affording full batch. It is worse than that instinct suggests, and better: pure full-batch gradient descent on a neural net typically generalizes worse, not just trains slower. Three separate, real mechanisms are why.

  1. It escapes saddles and sharp minima. A stochastic gradient is the true gradient plus noise. On a saddle plateau the true gradient is nearly zero in some direction; the noise isn't, so a stochastic kick pushes the iterate off the plateau where exact, quiet full-batch GD can sit for a very long time.
  2. Implicit regularization. SGD's noise biases the search toward flatter minima — regions of parameter space that tolerate a nudge without the loss jumping — and flatter minima tend to generalize better to data the model hasn't seen. Full-batch GD has no such bias; it goes wherever the exact gradient points, sharp minima included.
  3. Throughput. More, cheaper, slightly-wrong steps beat fewer, expensive, exact ones. A full-batch step over $N=256$ examples costs 256 forward/backward passes for one update; the same compute buys 256 single-example updates. More updates per unit of compute usually wins, even though each one is noisier.

All three are real and independent — name them separately rather than folding them into one vague "noise helps" shrug.

Reading a noisy curve

A mini-batch loss curve is jagged by construction — every step is a different, small, biased sample of the true loss. That is not a sign of trouble. Judge the trend over a window of steps, not the step-to-step wiggle. (Page 24 sharpens this into a diagnostic: a spike that lands on the same step across different random seeds is data — a bad example, a numerical edge case; a spike that moves when you change the seed is just noise doing what noise does.)

⚠️ "Bigger batch is strictly better." Larger $B$ means less noise — but less noise means less of all three mechanisms above, often worse generalization, and diminishing returns on wall-clock time. It also silently changes the effective learning rate (see the worked example above) if you're not careful about mean-vs-sum. Bigger is not free.
⚠️ "Noise is a necessary evil we tolerate for speed." It is a feature with three separate, real mechanisms (saddle-escape, implicit regularization, throughput) — not a compromise. Pure full-batch GD on a neural net typically generalizes worse, even given unlimited compute.
⚠️ "Epoch and step are interchangeable — just different names." They coincide only at full batch. Schedules (warmup, decay — page 21) are indexed by step $k$; on a trillion-token pretraining run, "epoch" barely applies at all.
