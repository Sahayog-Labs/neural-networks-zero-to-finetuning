LoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation

You cannot afford to move all 8.19 billion of Qwen3-8B's weights — page 38 measured that bill in centuries. So you don't. You freeze the base and train two skinny matrices whose product is the change. This page derives that trick once, to the byte, for the whole course — neither track re-derives it.

🧵 Thread [THREAD:memory-ledger — the reality check] · [THREAD:dot-product — the atom] · [THREAD:chain-rule — the verb] · [THREAD:TN1 — the object]. Four threads converge here. The ledger you opened on page 18 (16 B/param) meets its escape hatch; the dot product returns as the product $BA$; the chain rule explains why a zero-initialised adapter is safe to bolt onto a production model; and TN-1 itself becomes a LoRA target. This is the page pages 50 (LLM) and the diffusion track both open by naming — "as the trunk derived on page 40…" — and then re-derive nothing.
🔮 Predict first LoRA freezes Qwen3-8B's 16.38 GB base and trains two skinny matrices per weight. Compared with a full fine-tune, where does LoRA's memory saving mostly come from? Commit to one before you scroll: (a) the model itself gets smaller,  (b) it needs to cache fewer activations,  (c) the gradients and the optimizer state — the bulk — simply vanish.

The bet: the change is low-rank, not the model

Fine-tuning turns the pretrained weight $W_0$ into $W_0 + \Delta W$. On page 39 you flipped a dropdown and saw the difference with your own eyes: a real weight matrix $W_0$ is nearly full rank — its scree plot decays slowly, and deleting directions destroys the model. But $\Delta W$, the change a fine-tune makes, has a scree plot that falls off a cliff: a few directions carry almost all of it. [THREAD:dot-product] — the same "how much of this is in that?" from the dot-product page, now measuring how many directions a matrix really moves in.

LoRA takes that observation to the bank. Don't store a $4096\times4096$ change — store two skinny matrices whose product is the change. A rank-$r$ update needs only $r(d_{\text{out}}+d_{\text{in}})$ numbers instead of $d_{\text{out}}\,d_{\text{in}}$, and — the part that actually matters — you only ever compute gradients and optimizer state for those.

The math, once

📐 The LoRA forward pass $$h \;=\; W_0\,\mathbf x \;+\; \Delta W\,\mathbf x \;=\; W_0\,\mathbf x \;+\; \frac{\alpha}{r}\,BA\,\mathbf x.$$
SymbolIsShapeFrom
$W_0$the frozen pretrained weight — never updated$(d_{\text{out}}, d_{\text{in}})$the base checkpoint; .detach()ed
$A$LoRA "down" projection$(r, d_{\text{in}})$trained; init $\mathcal N(0,\varsigma^2)$
$B$LoRA "up" projection$(d_{\text{out}}, r)$trained; init zeros — this is load-bearing
$r$the rank — a count, $r \ll \min(d_{\text{in}},d_{\text{out}})$scalaryour choice (the capacity knob)
$\alpha$the LoRA scaling constant (lora_alpha)scalaryour choice; only $\alpha/r$ matters
$\mathbf x$, $h$layer input / output$(d_{\text{in}},1)$ / $(d_{\text{out}},1)$the residual stream

Shape ribbon (the update never materialises as a full matrix at train time):

x            (d_in, 1)
A            (r, d_in)              # (out, in) — ALWAYS
A x          (r, 1)                 # squeeze d_in down to the rank
B            (d_out, r)
B (A x)      (d_out, 1)             # lift the rank back up to d_out
(α/r)·BAx    (d_out, 1)   ✓         # same shape as W₀x — it just adds

[THREAD:dot-product] $BA$ is $r$ stacked outer products, $BA=\sum_{k=1}^{r}\mathbf b_k\mathbf a_k^\top$ (page 39's rank-1 pieces). Reading it right-to-left, $BA\mathbf x$ asks $r$ alignment questions — "how much of $\mathbf x$ points along $\mathbf a_k$?" — and recombines the $r$ answers with the columns of $B$. The dot-product atom, assembling an update.

Why $B$ starts at zero (a chain-rule beat)

With $B=0$, the product $BA=0$, so $\Delta W = 0$ and the adapted model is bit-identical to the base at step 0. Training starts from the pretrained model exactly, not from a randomly perturbed copy — which is why you can bolt a LoRA adapter onto a production model without fear of degrading it before the first gradient. [THREAD:chain-rule].

🔑 The adapter unfreezes itself, $B$ first Zero-init looks like a trap — if $B=0$, how does anything learn? Read the chain rule. The gradient into $A$ passes through $B$, so $\partial\mathcal L/\partial A \propto B = 0$ at step 0 — $A$ cannot move. But $\partial\mathcal L/\partial B \propto A \neq 0$, so $B$ moves on the very first step. Once $B\neq0$, $A$'s gradient wakes up. The adapter bootstraps itself: $B$ first, then both. The live demo below shows this exactly — at step 1 the measured $\lVert\nabla A\rVert$ is identically zero while $\lVert\nabla B\rVert$ is not. (The escape hatch init_lora_weights=False exists only for debugging.)

Freezing $W_0$ is just .detach(). [THREAD:chain-rule] — it is the same stop-gradient you met in the autograd chapter (page 15): grey out one edge of the graph and no gradient flows to it, so the optimizer never allocates a slot for it. No new machinery; the entire memory win falls out of one method call.

A single $4096\times4096$ matrix: $16(4096+4096)=\mathbf{131{,}072}$ vs $4096^2=\mathbf{16{,}777{,}216}$ — a 128× reduction. Across the whole model: 43,646,976 trainable = 0.533% of $P$. You are training one parameter in 188.

🔑 It closes to the byte, not "to three significant figures" The 6,945,767,424 above omits the norms (correctly — they aren't LoRA targets), so it is not the model's parameter count. Add them back and the embeddings, and it reconciles exactly: $$\underbrace{6{,}945{,}767{,}424}_{\text{7 linears}\,\times\,36} + \underbrace{36\times8{,}448}_{304{,}128\ \text{norms}} + \underbrace{4{,}096}_{\text{final norm}} + \underbrace{2\times622{,}329{,}856}_{\text{embed + lm\_head}} = \mathbf{8{,}190{,}735{,}360}.$$ To the byte. This is the moment a model card stops being an authority and becomes arithmetic you can check. The adapter file itself, $r=16$ in bf16, is $43{,}646{,}976\times2 = \mathbf{87.3\ MB}$ — you can email it.

The memory ledger — and the PREDICT answer

[THREAD:memory-ledger] Page 18 charged 16 B/param for a full AdamW fine-tune: weights (2) + grads (2) + fp32 master (4) + Adam $m$ (4) + Adam $v$ (4). For Qwen3-8B that is $8{,}190{,}735{,}360\times16 = \mathbf{131.05\ GB}$ of state. LoRA keeps the 2-byte base weights resident but allocates grads + master + $m$ + $v$ only for the 43.6 million trainable params (14 B each):

✍️ Full → LoRA, state-to-state (activations named separately, both sides)
Frozen base weights (bf16, no grad)$8.19\text{e}9 \times 2\ \text{B}$16.38 GB
Adapter weights (bf16)$43{,}646{,}976 \times 2\ \text{B}$0.087 GB (87.3 MB)
Adapter trainable state (grad + master + $m$ + $v$)$43{,}646{,}976 \times 14\ \text{B}$0.61 GB
LoRA total (state)$16.38 + 0.087 + 0.61$17.08 GB / 15.91 GiB

Full → LoRA: 131.05 GB → 17.08 GB = 7.67× (state-to-state, both exclude activations). But the sharper number is the trainable state alone: 114.67 GB → 0.61 GB = 187× — and 187 is exactly the parameter ratio, $8{,}190{,}735{,}360 / 43{,}646{,}976 = 187.7$, because optimizer state is linear in trainable parameters.

🔑 What LoRA actually compresses (ratified, D-05) LoRA is not a model-compression technique. LoRA is an optimizer-state compression technique. The base model didn't shrink — all 16.38 GB is still resident. The trainable state shrank by 187×, which is exactly $8{,}190{,}735{,}360 \div 43{,}646{,}976$, because optimizer state is linear in trainable parameters. That's the whole trick. So the PREDICT answer is (c) — gradients and optimizer state, the bulk, are what vanish.

Four traps

⚠️ "LoRA saves memory because it has fewer weights." Half-true, and the boring half. The weights barely shrink — all 16.38 GB of base is still resident. What vanishes is the 114.67 GB of gradients and Adam state, replaced by 0.61 GB. That was the bulk. Saying "fewer weights" points at the one term that didn't move.
⚠️ "LoRA reduces activation memory." False (constants.md §2.4). LoRA freezes weights but does not shorten the graph — you still backprop through the entire network, so the activation cache (page 18's villain) is ~unchanged. Every LoRA-vs-full comparison on this page is state-to-state, with activations as a separate identical line on both sides. Measure LoRA's real footprint on your box and you'll find it above 17.08 GB by exactly the activation term — so the honest course tells you first. (Retired numbers you may see elsewhere, all wrong here: ~130 GB→14.3 GB, 224×, 417×.)
⚠️ "α is a strength dial — turn it up to learn harder." $\alpha$ and the learning rate $\eta$ are nearly redundant: what reaches the weights is the effective scale $\alpha/r$ times $\eta$, one combined step size. Pick a convention for $\alpha$ ($\alpha=2r$ folklore, or a fixed $\alpha=16$) and tune $\eta$ instead. The two conventions genuinely disagree (an unresolved fork — say so), but tuning both $\alpha$ and $\eta$ just wastes a hyperparameter on a knob you already have. The demo lets you crank $\alpha$ and watch it destabilise training exactly the way too-high $\eta$ does. (LoRA tolerates ~10× higher $\eta$ than full FT — 1e-4–3e-4 [EST] vs 1e-5–2e-5 — precisely because $B$ is zero-init, so the adapter starts as an exact no-op.)
⚠️ "Attention is where the knowledge is — target q_proj/v_proj." The most persistent stale advice in the field. Look at the count you just computed: the MLP matrices (gate/up/down) are 150,994,944 of 192,937,984 targetable params per layer = 78%. Attention-only means you froze 78% of the model and then wondered why it didn't learn. Use target_modules="all-linear". (One MoE caveat, D-06: HF implements experts as nn.Parameter, not nn.Linear, so all-linear silently skips them — reach them with target_parameters=[…].)
🔑 Rank follows the dataset, not the model $r$ is the capacity knob — the classical bias-variance U-curve (page 23), relocated into a config file. So it should scale with the information content of your dataset, not the size of your model. An LLM SFT run over a broad instruction set wants $r=64$–256; a diffusion concept LoRA on 15–20 photos wants $r=8$–32 — push $r$ high there and it memorises the photos. Same rule, opposite ends. And rank is nearly free in memory: $r=256$ all-linear is 698,351,616 params = 8.53% of $P$ → 27.55 GB, still trivially fits your box. Your dataset constrains your rank, not your memory — being stingy with $r$ out of superstition is the error. Both tracks reopen this rule at their own target-and-rank pages.

One page away: the 16.38 GB frozen base is read-only — so why store it at full precision? Compress it to ~4 bits and the whole thing gets cheaper still. That is QLoRA, page 41.

🔬 Deep dive: the 2026 PEFT menagerie — DoRA, rsLoRA, PiSSA, LoRA-GA (none of it changes the page above)

DoRA splits each weight into a magnitude and a direction and gives the magnitude its own trainable scalar; it helps most at low rank, so it buys little once you are at $r=256$. rsLoRA scales the adapter by $\alpha/\sqrt r$ instead of $\alpha/r$ — a wash at small $r$, but it keeps the effective scale from collapsing in the high-rank regime (the demo's toggle). PiSSA initialises $A,B$ from $W_0$'s principal singular vectors (page 39's SVD) rather than from noise/zeros, converging faster. LoRA-GA initialises from estimated full gradients (2–4× faster convergence) but is mutually exclusive with QLoRA — it needs full-precision weights, which page 41 throws away. All optional; nothing later in the course depends on any of them.
