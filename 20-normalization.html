Normalization: The Only Question Is Which Axis

Page 19 set the per-stage gain to ~1 before a single data point arrives — at initialization. But training moves the weights, and the gain drifts. Normalization is the operation that holds the signal well-conditioned during training. There look to be three of them — BatchNorm, LayerNorm, RMSNorm — and the whole apparent zoo collapses to one question: which axis do you reduce over?

What normalization is for

Page 19 gave you a picture: a deep network is a hundred-stage amplifier, and the per-stage gain $g$ has to sit at ~1 or the signal explodes ($1.1^{100}=13{,}781$) or vanishes ($0.9^{100}=2.7\times10^{-5}$). Initialization sets that gain once, at step $k=0$. Then the optimizer starts moving the weights, and nothing guarantees the gain stays put. A layer that was seeing a well-scaled input at initialization can, fifty steps later, be fed activations that are ten times too large or a tenth too small.

Normalization is the fix that acts every forward pass. It re-scales (and sometimes re-centres) each layer's input so the next layer always sees a well-conditioned signal — the per-stage gain is pinned at ~1 not just at init but throughout training. That is the entire job. Everything below is bookkeeping about which numbers you average to do it.

The three normalizers, precisely — and the one difference between them

Take a batch of activations $X$ with shape (B, …, d): $B$ examples, each a $d$-dimensional feature vector. There are two axes you could average over — down the batch (one statistic per feature) or across the features (one statistic per example) — and that single choice is the entire taxonomy.

X (B=4, d=6) # a batch of activations BatchNorm reduce over B ↓ # per feature, one mean/var per column LayerNorm reduce over d → # per example, one mean/var per row RMSNorm reduce over d → # per example, RMS only — no mean
📐 LayerNorm and RMSNorm

LayerNorm centres then scales each example's feature vector, then applies a learned gain $\gamma$ and shift $\beta$:

$$\mathrm{LN}(\mathbf{x})_i \;=\; \gamma_i\,\frac{x_i - \mu}{\sqrt{\varsigma^2 + \varepsilon}} \;+\; \beta_i, \qquad \mu = \frac1d\sum_{j} x_j, \quad \varsigma^2 = \frac1d\sum_{j}(x_j-\mu)^2.$$

RMSNorm keeps the scaling but drops the mean subtraction entirely — it divides by the root-mean-square, not the standard deviation:

$$\mathrm{RMS}(\mathbf{x})_i \;=\; \gamma_i\,\frac{x_i}{\sqrt{\frac1d\sum_{j} x_j^2 + \varepsilon}}.$$

The only difference between all three is which axis you reduce over — and RMSNorm additionally skips the $-\mu$ centring. BatchNorm reduces down $B$; LayerNorm and RMSNorm reduce across $d$. Same operation, three call sites.

By hand, on the course's canonical triple

Take the vector the course reuses everywhere, $\mathbf{x} = [2.0,\ 1.0,\ 0.1]$ ($d=3$, $\gamma=1$, $\beta=0$), and compute LayerNorm and RMSNorm on it so the "drops the mean" claim becomes a number.

✍️ Worked: LayerNorm vs RMSNorm on $[2.0,\,1.0,\,0.1]$

LayerNorm — first the mean, then centre, then scale by the standard deviation:

$$\mu = \frac{2.0+1.0+0.1}{3} = \mathbf{1.0333}, \qquad \varsigma = \sqrt{\tfrac13\!\left(0.9667^2 + 0.0333^2 + 0.9333^2\right)} = 0.7760,$$

$$\mathrm{LN}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{[\,0.9667,\ -0.0333,\ -0.9333\,]}{0.7760} = [\,\mathbf{1.246},\ \mathbf{-0.043},\ \mathbf{-1.203}\,].$$

RMSNorm — no mean; divide straight by the root-mean-square:

$$\mathrm{RMS} = \sqrt{\frac{2.0^2 + 1.0^2 + 0.1^2}{3}} = \sqrt{\frac{5.01}{3}} = \sqrt{1.6700} = \mathbf{1.2923},$$

$$\mathrm{RMS}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{[\,2.0,\ 1.0,\ 0.1\,]}{1.2923} = [\,\mathbf{1.548},\ \mathbf{0.774},\ \mathbf{0.077}\,].$$

That resolves the PREDICT. RMSNorm drops the mean subtraction — notice its output still averages to a nonzero number ($1.548+0.774+0.077$ over 3 $=0.800$, not 0), whereas LayerNorm's output is centred on 0 by construction. Empirically the re-centring term contributes little to a transformer's quality, so Llama and Qwen simply skip it and save the arithmetic.

Why BatchNorm lost in transformers

BatchNorm ruled computer vision for years. In sequence models it was quietly dropped, and the reasons are concrete, not fashion:

📐 Four reasons transformers reduce over $d$, not $B$
  1. Variable length & tiny effective batch per position. A sequence model has a different number of real tokens at each position; the "batch" seen by one position can be small and ragged.
  2. Train/eval mismatch. BatchNorm keeps running statistics for inference that differ from the per-batch statistics used in training — a whole class of silent bugs LayerNorm never has.
  3. It couples examples in a batch. One example's normalization depends on the others in its batch — poison for autoregressive generation, where you decode one sequence at a time.
  4. LayerNorm/RMSNorm are per-example and batch-independent. Reduce over $d$ and every one of these problems evaporates: the statistic for a token depends only on that token.

Pre-norm vs post-norm — the bracket that decides whether an 80-layer model trains

Where you place the norm relative to the residual add is a one-character edit with an outsized consequence. Write a residual block as "input, plus a function $F$ of the input." There are two places the norm can go:

🔑 The residual highway, kept or broken

$$\textbf{Post-norm:}\quad \mathbf{x} \leftarrow \mathrm{Norm}\big(\mathbf{x} + F(\mathbf{x})\big) \qquad\qquad \textbf{Pre-norm:}\quad \mathbf{x} \leftarrow \mathbf{x} + F\big(\mathrm{Norm}(\mathbf{x})\big)$$

In pre-norm, the $+\mathbf{x}$ skip is un-normalised: the identity gradient path of page 19 ($\partial\mathbf{y}/\partial\mathbf{x} = I + \partial F/\partial\mathbf{x}$, the $+I$ that guarantees a magnitude-~1 route to any depth) survives untouched to the bottom of the stack. In post-norm, the norm sits on that path — every backward gradient is multiplied by the norm's Jacobian on the way down, and deep post-norm stacks are hard to train without careful warmup. One bracket decides whether an 80-layer model trains. Modern LLMs — Qwen3 included — are pre-norm.

Three traps

⚠️ "The three normalizers are fundamentally different mechanisms." They are one operation — subtract a location, divide by a scale, optionally learn $\gamma,\beta$ — applied over a different reduction axis. BatchNorm reduces down the batch; LayerNorm and RMSNorm reduce across the features. That is the whole difference. If you can name the axis, you know the normalizer.
⚠️ "RMSNorm is an approximation that sacrifices accuracy." It is a deliberate, near-free simplification, not a lossy shortcut. Empirically the mean-centring term contributes little to model quality, so dropping it costs essentially nothing and saves a reduction and a subtraction per token. Llama, Qwen, and most modern LLMs ship RMSNorm on purpose.
⚠️ "Normalization removes the need for good init and residuals." It complements them; it does not replace them. A deep net still needs the residual highway (pre-norm) and a scaled init (page 19) — normalization pins the gain during training, but a pathological init or a norm sitting on the residual path (post-norm) will still fight you. All three tools solve one problem together.
