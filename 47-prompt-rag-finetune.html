The Decision: Prompt vs RAG vs Fine-Tune

The most expensive mistake in applied LLMs is fine-tuning a model to make it "know" something. Two words keep you out of that trap for life: fine-tuning teaches FORM, RAG supplies FACTS.

LLM Fine-Tuning Track

You now know how a model reads text in (p.44), what pretraining installs (p.45), and how it decodes text back out under a bandwidth ceiling (p.46). Before you spend a single GPU-hour training anything, this page answers the question everyone gets wrong first: which lever do I even pull?

🔮 Predict, before you commit You want your model to answer questions about your own 200 documents. Do you (a) fine-tune it on the documents, (b) put a search engine in front of it, or (c) improve the prompt? Commit to one now — most people pick (a), and it is usually the wrong and most expensive answer. By the end of this page you'll know exactly why, and what it costs.

The rule that ends most arguments

🔑 The one rule Fine-tuning teaches FORM. RAG supplies FACTS. If you can't answer the question by pasting the answer into the prompt, fine-tuning won't fix it.
Trunk callback · D-18

This is not a new idea — it's one axis of the type-signature the trunk already gave you. Back on page 42 ("'Fine-tuning' names three things") you saw that the word "fine-tuning" hides a WHERE / WHAT question: where does the new information live, and what do you change to install it? FACTS live in the evidence, so you change the input, not $\theta$. FORM lives in the behaviour, so you change $\theta$. This page is that taxonomy turned into a decision, and it is owned here.

The field's most expensive mistake

⚠️ The one that costs the most — "I'll fine-tune on my company docs so it knows them"

This is the misconception behind your PREDICT (a). Here is the mechanism, not a slogan. SFT on your documents optimizes $-\log p_\theta(\text{doc tokens})$ — it teaches the model to write text that sounds like your documents. It does not build a retrievable index.

The facts smear across all 8.19 billion parameters at a learning rate of $2\times10^{-4}$; no single weight holds "the Q3 revenue was 4.1 million." The result is fluent, confident, correctly-formatted wrong answers — which is strictly worse than an obvious failure, because the house style now signals an authority the content hasn't earned.

The fix, in one breath: Fine-tuning changes the model's prior, not its evidence. If you want a fact at inference time, the fact must be in the context window at inference time. There is no third option.

The demo below (the RAG-vs-fine-tune showdown) lets you watch a LoRA'd Qwen3-8B hallucinate a fact from your own document #147 in fluent prose, then watch RAG answer the same question correctly with a citation. That one experiment is the cheapest insurance you will ever buy.

Reason #6, priced out: the prompt you re-read a million times

Row 6 is real money, so let's make it a number. Suppose every request carries a 4,000-token system prompt (instructions, style guide, few-shot examples), and you serve 50,000 requests a month. Every one of those tokens is re-read from scratch on every call during prefill.

✍️ Worked number — hours spent re-reading your own instructions

Prefill tokens per month:

$$4{,}000 \frac{\text{tok}}{\text{call}} \times 50{,}000 \frac{\text{calls}}{\text{mo}} = 2\times10^{8}\ \frac{\text{tok}}{\text{mo}}$$

Your box prefills Qwen3-8B-class models at about 8,000 tok/s (constants §6.7 [VP] — the measured Llama-3.1-8B figure is 7,991 tok/s; prefill is compute-bound, so it is the same order for Qwen3-8B). So:

$$\frac{2\times10^{8}\ \text{tok}}{8{,}000\ \text{tok/s}} = 25{,}000\ \text{s} = \mathbf{6.9\ hours/month}$$

Distill those 4,000 tokens down to a 200-token prompt by baking the instructions into a LoRA once, and the same month costs $\tfrac{200\times50{,}000}{8{,}000} = 1{,}250\ \text{s} \approx \mathbf{20\ minutes}$ — a 20× cut, from about one hour of LoRA training. A strong reason to fine-tune that has nothing to do with knowledge.

So you chose RAG — what is it, actually?

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) is a search engine bolted to the front of the model. Nothing about the weights changes. The whole system is a pipeline, and the parameters that matter are boringly concrete (defaults from the field, brief §7):

  1. Chunk — split documents into 300–500 token passages with 10–15% overlap (so a fact straddling a boundary survives).
  2. Embed — map each chunk to a vector $e_c$ with a bidirectional encoder; similarity is cosine. Embedding dimension $d$ is typically 384 / 768 / 1024 / 1536.
  3. Retrieve the top-$N$ (≈20) by cosine, and in parallel run BM25 (classic keyword scoring) over the same chunks.
  4. Fuse the two ranked lists with Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF).
  5. Rerank the survivors down to ~5 with a cross-encoder that reads query and chunk jointly.
  6. Stuff 3–5 chunks into the prompt and let the model write the answer.

Cosine similarity — the retrieval score

$$\text{sim}(e_q, e_c) = \cos\vartheta = \frac{e_q \cdot e_c}{\lVert e_q\rVert\,\lVert e_c\rVert}$$

$e_q$query embedding$(d,)$encoder applied to the question
$e_c$chunk embedding$(d,)$encoder applied to a stored passage
$e_q \cdot e_c$the dot product — the atom, one more timescalarelementwise multiply-and-sum
$\vartheta$angle between the two vectors (not $\theta$ — that's parameters)radiansgeometry

Thread [THREAD:dot product] — retrieval is the dot product again. Same atom as the neuron (p.3), the attention score (p.29), and $BA$ in LoRA: a similarity is $e_q\cdot e_c$ with both sides normalized. What's new here is only that the two vectors are an encoded question and an encoded document.

BM25 — the keyword score dense retrieval keeps forgetting

Cosine over learned embeddings is semantic: it finds passages that mean something similar. It is terrible at exact strings — part numbers, error codes, rare names — because those get averaged into a smooth vector. BM25 is the opposite: a bag-of-words score that rewards rare query terms appearing in a chunk.

$$\text{BM25}(q, c) = \sum_{w\,\in\,q} \text{IDF}(w)\cdot \frac{f(w,c)\,(k_1 + 1)}{f(w,c) + k_1\bigl(1 - b_{\text{BM25}} + b_{\text{BM25}}\tfrac{|c|}{\overline{|c|}}\bigr)}, \qquad \text{IDF}(w) = \ln\!\Bigl(1 + \tfrac{N - n_w + 0.5}{n_w + 0.5}\Bigr)$$

$w$a query term (word)—the tokenized question
$f(w,c)$count of $w$ in chunk $c$integerterm frequency
$\text{IDF}(w)$inverse document frequency — rare query words score highscalar$n_w$ = chunks with $w$, $N$ = total chunks
$k_1$term-frequency saturation ($=1.2$)scalarBM25 constant
$b_{\text{BM25}}$length-normalization ($=0.75$)scalarBM25 constant
$|c|,\ \overline{|c|}$chunk length, and mean chunk lengthtokensthe corpus
⚠️ Notation — these are BM25's constants, not the course's Papers write BM25's parameters as bare $k_1$ and $b$. In this course $k$ is the optimizer step and $b$ is the batch index, so we write $k_1$ (fine — a numeric subscript) and $b_{\text{BM25}}$. When you read the BM25 paper, its $b$ is our $b_{\text{BM25}}$.

Reciprocal Rank Fusion — combining the two lists

Dense and BM25 return two different orderings. RRF fuses them without needing their scores to be on the same scale — it uses only ranks:

$$\text{RRF}(c) = \sum_{r\,\in\,\{\text{dense},\,\text{bm25}\}} \frac{1}{k_{\text{RRF}} + \text{rank}_r(c)}, \qquad k_{\text{RRF}} = 60$$

$c$a candidate chunk—appears in one or both lists
$r$a ranked list (dense, or BM25)—the two retrievers
$\text{rank}_r(c)$position of $c$ in list $r$ (1 = best)integersort order
$k_{\text{RRF}}$rank-smoothing constant ($=60$; not the optimizer step $k$)scalarRRF paper

The retrieval itself is one matmul and a sort. The shape ribbon for the dense side:

shapes
E            (N=200, d=384)          # the chunk embedding matrix, L2-normalized rows
e_q          (d=384,)                # the query embedding, L2-normalized
scores = E @ e_q                     (N=200,)   # one cosine per chunk — the whole search
top = argsort(scores)[::-1][:N_top]   (N_top,)   # the ranked list
✍️ Worked number — do you even need a vector database?

The whole "vector DB" industry exists for scale you probably don't have. Store 100,000 chunks of dimension 1024 in fp32:

$$100{,}000 \times 1024 \times 4\ \text{B} = 409{,}600{,}000\ \text{B} = \mathbf{410\ MB}$$

That is 410 MB in fp32, 205 MB in fp16, and 12.8 MB as 1-bit binary embeddings. All of it fits in a corner of your box's memory, and the search is E @ e_q — one matmul. Thread [THREAD:memory budget] — the same quantization ladder you used for weights (p.41) applies to the embedding store; 410 MB → 12.8 MB is the same trick.

🔑 You do not need a vector DB under ~100k chunks np.argsort(embeddings @ q) is exact, instant, and zero-ops on the Spark's GPU. Managed vector databases buy you approximate search and sharding for the millions-of-chunks regime; below ~100k chunks they buy you a bill and a dependency.
⚠️ "Cosine similarity finds relevant chunks" It finds similar ones, which is not the same thing. Ask for "the risks of Project Titan" and dense retrieval happily returns five chunks about Titan and zero about risks — because "Titan" dominates the vector. The cross-encoder reranker, which reads the query and each chunk together instead of comparing two independent vectors, is what repairs relevance. That is why reranking is not optional — and you can watch it happen in the sandbox below.

🔬 Deep dive: RAG is a search engine, not AI — the 80/20 rule

Everything upstream of "stuff" in the pipeline is classical information retrieval — chunking, embeddings, BM25, fusion, reranking. Only the last box is generation. And here is the uncomfortable ratio: retrieval quality dominates end-to-end quality. The language model can only answer from what you put in front of it; if the right chunk never makes it into the context window, no amount of model capability recovers it. Roughly 80% of your effort — and 80% of your wins — live in the retrieval half, which contains no neural generation at all.

This is why "the model hallucinated" is usually a retrieval bug in disguise: you asked it to answer from context that didn't contain the answer, and a fluent model did exactly what fluent models do.
