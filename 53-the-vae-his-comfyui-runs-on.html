Latent Space: The VAE Your ComfyUI Already Runs On

Before a single denoising step, every image you make is squeezed into a tiny latent grid by a VAE — and then decoded back out by one that was trained like a GAN. This page nails down the shapes on your own disk, and shows why the latent's own prior is not a Gaussian. That gap is the reason the next nine pages exist.

Diffusion Track begins here

You already turn these knobs — CFG, samplers, schedulers, LoRA strength, denoise, bucketing. This track never re-explains what they do; it derives why they behave as you have watched them behave. We start where every workflow starts: the VAEEncode / VAEDecode pair.

Do you get (a) a plausible image, (b) a structured-but-wrong image, or (c) colored static? Commit to a letter before you scroll.

The wall every later page climbs

The whole game is to sample from $p(\mathbf{x})$ — the distribution of real images. Maximum likelihood (the trunk's whole story, p.12) wants to write it down explicitly, $p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \tilde p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\,/\,Z_\theta$, and then push $\theta$ to raise the probability of the training set. The numerator $\tilde p_\theta$ is an easy unnormalized score you can evaluate. The denominator is the problem:

$$Z_\theta = \int \tilde p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\,\mathrm{d}\mathbf{x} \quad\text{— an integral over every possible image.}$$

For a $1024^2$ RGB image that integral has three million dimensions and no closed form. You cannot compute $Z_\theta$, so you cannot compute $p_\theta(\mathbf{x})$, so you cannot maximize it directly. This is the wall. Every technique in this track is a different way around it.

[THREAD: The chain rule · beat] Same verb, new escape route. Notice that $\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\log p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\log\tilde p_\theta(\mathbf{x})$ — the gradient of the log-density with respect to the image makes the constant $Z_\theta$ differentiate clean away. Work with that gradient (the score) and the intractable normalizer never appears. We build exactly this on p.56; hold the thread.

🔬 Deep dive: GANs in ninety seconds — and the one fact about them that lives inside your VAE

A GAN sidesteps $Z_\theta$ differently: it never writes down a likelihood at all. A generator maps noise to images and a discriminator learns to tell real from fake; the two play a minimax game. It samples beautifully and fast, but it gives you no likelihood, and it is prone to mode collapse — the generator finds a few outputs that fool the discriminator and stops covering the data. That is why the field largely moved to diffusion for coverage and stability. Do not learn WGAN-GP for this course.

The one fact to keep: the SD/FLUX VAE decoder is trained with a GAN-style adversarial loss (plus perceptual and reconstruction terms). A plain maximum-likelihood VAE decoder minimizes pixel MSE and comes out blurry — MSE rewards hedging toward the mean. The adversarial term is exactly what makes your decoded outputs crisp instead of soft. So your generator is diffusion, but your decoder still owes its sharpness to a GAN.

From autoencoder to VAE

A plain autoencoder is two networks: an encoder that squeezes an image down to a small code, and a decoder that reconstructs it. Train them together to minimize reconstruction error and the code becomes a compressed representation. The trouble: nothing organizes the code space, so the gaps between valid codes are dead — decode a point between two real codes and you get junk.

The variational autoencoder fixes the code space. The encoder no longer emits a point; it emits a little Gaussian, and the loss pulls all those Gaussians toward a shared reference:

$$q_{\theta_E}(\mathbf{z}\mid\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{N}\!\big(\mathbf{z};\ \boldsymbol\mu,\ \boldsymbol\sigma^2 I\big), \qquad \mathcal{L}_{\text{VAE}} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\big[\lVert\mathbf{x}-\hat{\mathbf{x}}\rVert^2\big]}_{\text{reconstruction}} + \beta_{\text{KL}}\,\underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\big(q_{\theta_E}(\mathbf{z}\mid\mathbf{x})\,\big\|\,\mathcal{N}(0,I)\big)}_{\text{keep codes near the reference}}.$$

To backprop through the sampling step $\mathbf{z}\sim q_{\theta_E}$ you use the reparameterization trick — trunk-owned, p.8, we do not re-derive it here. In one line: $\mathbf{z} = \boldsymbol\mu + \boldsymbol\sigma\odot\boldsymbol\epsilon$ with $\boldsymbol\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$, which moves the randomness off the parameters so the gradient can flow through $\boldsymbol\mu$ and $\boldsymbol\sigma$. Here is the round-trip as a shape ribbon, on your FLUX.2 VAE:

image (3, 1024, 1024) # RGB pixels, your input photo encoder → (64, 128, 128) # 64 = 32 mu + 32 logvar channels [VP, his vae/config.json] split → mu (32,128,128), logvar (32,128,128) z = mu + exp(0.5*logvar) * eps (32, 128, 128) # reparameterize; eps ~ N(0,I) decoder → (3, 1024, 1024) # back to pixels

What the KL term actually buys

🔑 Three things the KL does — and one it does not (1) It regularizes the codes toward a known reference $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ so they do not drift to arbitrary scales. (2) It makes the latent navigable: because every code is a little blob that overlaps its neighbors, there are no dead voids between valid codes — you can interpolate and the decoder stays sensible. (3) It is not what makes the model generative. A VAE is a compressor with a tidy code space; the ability to sample new images is not something the KL term confers.
⚠️ Misconception: "the KL term makes the VAE generative — just sample $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ and decode" This is the predict box, and the answer is no. In a latent-diffusion model the KL weight is deliberately tiny ($\beta_{\text{KL}}\approx 10^{-6}$) — it is turned almost off, precisely so the encoder keeps enough information to reconstruct sharply. The consequence: the aggregate distribution of real latents is not $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$. It is a thin, structured sheet sitting inside the latent space, and $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ is a bad model of it. Sampling the prior and decoding does not land you on that sheet — it lands you in the void, and the decoder turns the void into colored static. That mismatch is the entire reason you need a diffusion model on top of the VAE: to find the sheet.

So the predict answer is (c), colored static — and the reason is worth holding onto: the VAE gives you a good space (compact, navigable, sharply decodable), but it does not give you a good way to sample in that space. Diffusion is the sampler. The live demo below shows the geometry of why $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ misses.

The VAEs on your disk, by the numbers

All three VAEs below downsample by the same spatial factor $f=8$, so a $1024^2$ image always becomes a $128\times128$ latent grid. What differs is the channel count $C$ — and that single number sets the compression ratio through one clean identity:

$$\text{compression} = \frac{3\,H_{px}W_{px}}{C\,H_{\text{lat}}W_{\text{lat}}} = \frac{3\,H_{px}W_{px}}{C\,(H_{px}/f)(W_{px}/f)} = \frac{3 f^2}{C} \quad\text{— resolution-independent; only $C$ and $f$ matter.}$$

Your VAE round-trips your photo; your VAE's own prior gives noise. That gap is the whole rest of the track. (~30 s.)

Why the channel count climbed 4 → 16 → 32 over the years. Every extra latent channel is fidelity the field is buying back. SD 1.5's aggressive $48\times$ squeeze into 4 channels throws away fine texture the decoder then has to hallucinate; FLUX.2 spends compression (down to $6.0\times$) to carry more real information into the latent, so the decoder invents less. More channels cost you a bigger latent to diffuse over, and buy you sharper, more faithful reconstructions. That trade is draggable in the next demo.

Demo — round-trip vs. decoding the prior

Here is the predict box, run live on a real linear autoencoder: the 2-D DCT, the exact transform inside JPEG. Its encoder projects each $8\times8$ image block onto 64 frequency coefficients — that is the latent; keep the lowest $K$ and you have a compressor of ratio $64/K$. Its decoder is the inverse DCT. Everything below is that transform computed on the real pixels of the scene — no canned image, no lookup table.

Two decoded tiles, side by side. Round-trip: encode the photo, keep $K$ coefficients, decode — crisp, because a real image's latent lives on a thin sheet (nearly all of its energy sits in a handful of low frequencies). Decode the prior: throw the photo away and hand the decoder $\mathbf{z}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ at the same latent shape and the same total energy — but spread isotropically across all 64 frequencies instead of concentrated on the sheet. You get colored static. Same decoder, same energy budget; the only thing that changed is whether the code sits on the data sheet.

This DCT autoencoder is deliberately the simplest real one — a fixed linear basis, no learning. Your FLUX.2 VAE is nonlinear and GAN-trained, so its static is richer and its round-trip sharper, but the geometry is identical: run code/vae_ceiling.py and you get these same two tiles from the real 32-channel decoder on your own disk. And the gap only widens with scale, because of dimension: a real FLUX.2 latent is $32\times128\times128 = 524{,}288$-dimensional, and in that many dimensions $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ puts essentially all of its mass on a thin spherical shell of radius $\sqrt{D}$, nowhere near the low-dimensional sheet where images live. Decoding a prior sample never lands on the sheet — so it decodes to static. Diffusion is the machine that learns to walk from the shell onto the sheet. That is the answer to the predict box, and the reason for everything that follows.

[THREAD: The memory ledger · beat] Same reality check, new object. The FLUX.2 VAE is 84.0M params — at the fp32 it ships in, $\approx 0.34$ GB; in bf16, $\approx 0.17$ GB. Against your measured 121.6875 GiB MemTotal [MEA-DEV] that is a rounding error. The lesson the ledger keeps repeating: the weights are the mass, not the activations or the latents — a half-megabyte latent grid is free; it is the parameter tensors you budget for.
