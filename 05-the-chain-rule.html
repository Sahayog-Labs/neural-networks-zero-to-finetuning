The Chain Rule — Multiply Along Paths, Add Across Paths

One sentence on this page is backpropagation. We'll earn it: chain three sensitivities into a real gradient by hand, watch autograd print the same number, and see why a product of small slopes dies — and why one skip connection saves it.

🧵 Thread [THREAD:chain-rule beat 1/… — the verb]. Page 04 handed you a table of seven derivatives — the local slopes. This page gives you the rule that composes them into the slope of a whole network. That composition rule is the engine of every gradient you will ever compute: it returns as TN-1's mechanized backward pass (page 14), as autograd (page 15), and, much later, as the diffusion score. Same rule, reopened each time — never re-taught.
🔮 Predict first A gear train: a 3:1 gear feeds a 2:1 gear. What is the overall ratio? And if one gear buried in a deep chain has ratio 0.25 (the steepest a sigmoid ever manages), what happens to everything upstream of it after 10 such gears? Commit a number for each before you scroll.

Intuition: gears in a chain

Turn the first gear one notch and the last gear turns by the product of all the ratios in between. A 3× link feeding a 2× link makes a 6× overall link — you multiply, you don't add. That is the whole idea of the chain rule, stated in brass: sensitivities compose by multiplication. Backprop is this exact move, done a few million times, in the one order that never recomputes a gear.

✍️ Resolving the prediction 3:1 feeding 2:1 → 6:1. Ratios multiply: $3 \times 2 = 6$. And a single 0.25 gear ten deep? $0.25^{10} = 9.54\times10^{-7}$ — the upstream gear turns about a millionth as much as the downstream one. Ten mild reductions in a row is a near-total stall. Hold that number; the deep-chain demo below detonates it, and page 11 names it the reason sigmoids were abandoned as hidden units.

The 0.25 is not arbitrary: it is the largest value $\sigma'$ ever takes, at $\sigma'(0)=0.25$ (page 04). Stack a chain of sigmoids and every link multiplies in a factor $\le 0.25$. Ten of them cost six orders of magnitude; thirty-two of them cost $0.25^{32}=5.4\times10^{-20}$ (constants.md §9.3) — below the smallest normal float32, i.e. exactly zero on your box. This is the vanishing-gradient problem, and you can already see its cause: it is not "depth," it is repeated multiplication by numbers less than one.

The rule, single variable

If $y$ depends on $u$, and $u$ depends on $x$, then the sensitivity of $y$ to $x$ is the product of the two local sensitivities:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\,f\bigl(g(x)\bigr) \;=\; f'\bigl(g(x)\bigr)\,\cdot\, g'(x).$$

Read it left to right as "slope of the outer function, evaluated where the inner function landed, times the slope of the inner function." The phrase evaluated where the inner landed is the part that will cost you memory later: to multiply by $f'(g(x))$ you must have kept $g(x)$ around from the forward pass.

The rule, multipath — the version backprop actually uses

Weights fan out: one knob can influence the loss through several different downstream units at once. When a quantity $x$ feeds several intermediates $u_1,\dots,u_k$, its total sensitivity is the sum over paths of the per-path products:

$$\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial x} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{k}\; \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial u_i}\,\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x}.$$

🔑 The sentence that is backpropagation For every path from a knob to the loss, multiply the sensitivities along the path; then add across all paths. Multiply along paths, add across paths. Say it out loud — that sentence, executed in reverse topological order so no slope is computed twice, is backpropagation. There is no additional idea in Part II; there is only bookkeeping that makes this cheap.

The "add across paths" half is the one everyone forgets, and it is exactly what makes a residual connection work. A residual gives a signal two ways to reach the loss: one through the block, and one straight through, whose local derivative is exactly 1. Add across those two paths and the $+1$ term guarantees the gradient can never fully vanish — a superhighway that survives any number of small-slope detours. You'll build that intuition in the demo and cash it in for real on page 34.

Worked by hand — one neuron, carried to a decimal

Take the smallest network that has a chain in it: one input, one weight, one bias, a sigmoid, and a squared-error loss. Concrete numbers: $x=2$, $w=0.5$, $b=0.1$, target $y=1$.

✍️ Forward, then backward Forward (build the chain):
$z$$wx+b = 0.5(2)+0.1$$1.1$
$a$$\sigma(z)=\sigma(1.1)$$0.75026$
$\mathcal{L}$$(a-y)^2 = (0.75026-1)^2$$0.06237$
Backward (multiply the local slopes along the one path $w \to z \to a \to \mathcal{L}$):
$\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial a$$2(a-y) = 2(-0.24974)$$-0.49948$
$\partial a/\partial z$$\sigma(z)\bigl(1-\sigma(z)\bigr)=0.75026\times0.24974$$0.18737$
$\partial z/\partial w$$x$$2$

Chain them: $\dfrac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial w} = (-0.49948)(0.18737)(2) = \mathbf{-0.1872}$, and since $\partial z/\partial b = 1$, $\;\dfrac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial b} = (-0.49948)(0.18737)(1) = \mathbf{-0.0936}$.

Take one step ($\eta = 0.5$): $w \leftarrow 0.5 - 0.5(-0.1872) = 0.5936$, $\;b \leftarrow 0.1 - 0.5(-0.0936) = 0.1468$. Re-run the forward pass with the new knobs: $\mathcal{L}' = 0.0435$ — down from 0.0624. You just trained a neuron with a pencil.

The same numbers, from autograd

This is the one .backward() call Part I permits — not to teach the reverse pass (that is page 14), but to prove the pencil was right. Run it with your ComfyUI venv's Python; it costs nothing (<0.1 s, CPU).

Python
import torch

w = torch.tensor(0.5, requires_grad=True)
b = torch.tensor(0.1, requires_grad=True)
x = torch.tensor(2.0)
y = torch.tensor(1.0)

z = w * x + b
a = torch.sigmoid(z)
loss = (a - y)**2
loss.backward()               # autograd walks the same chain, in reverse
print(w.grad, b.grad)        # tensor(-0.1872) tensor(-0.0936)
🔑 Three representations, one number Pencil: $-0.1872$. Autograd: $-0.1872$. The demo below: $-0.1872$. Backpropagation is not a different thing from the chain rule — it is the chain rule plus memoization plus a reverse-topological order, and nothing else. The mechanics that make it cheap are page 15's; the rule is entirely on this page.

Traps

⚠️ "Backprop is a different, harder thing than the chain rule." It is the chain rule, plus keeping intermediate results so no slope is recomputed, plus visiting nodes in reverse order. That is the entire content. The mechanization onto a graph is page 15; the idea is the sentence you just repeated.
⚠️ "The gradient flows back through the same weights $W$." It flows through $W^{\top}$, not $W$ — and you don't have to take that on faith: the shapes force it. Forward, an activation of shape $(B,d_{\text{in}})$ meets $(d_{\text{in}},d_{\text{out}})$; backward, an error of shape $(B,d_{\text{out}})$ must come back out as $(B,d_{\text{in}})$, which only $(d_{\text{out}}, d_{\text{in}})$ — i.e. $W^{\top}$ — makes dimensionally legal (pages 02 and 07; cashed in on page 14).
⚠️ "Autograd differentiates my Python symbolically." No. It records the operations that actually ran into a graph and walks that graph backward. That is why an if or a Python loop just works (only the taken branch is on the tape), and why .detach() and no_grad() are real tools — they keep ops off the tape. Full story: page 15.
⚠️ "Intermediate activations are thrown away after the forward pass." They are kept — because $\partial a/\partial z$ needs the stored $z$, and $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial W$ needs the stored input. [THREAD:memory beat 3] This is precisely why training memory scales with batch × sequence × depth, and why gradient checkpointing — trading recomputation for memory — is a real knob on your Spark. Page 18 turns this into the activation line of the memory ledger.
⚠️ "Vanishing gradients are about depth." Depth is only the multiplier count. The cause is repeated multiplication by factors below one: $0.25^{32}=5.4\times10^{-20}$, dead in float32. ReLU's factor of exactly 1 (for active units) is the fix you'll meet on page 11 — and the residual's $+1$ path is the fix you meet in the demo above.
🔬 Deep dive: why backprop runs in reverse, not forward

There are two honest ways to apply the chain rule to a whole network. Forward mode pushes a derivative with respect to one input forward through the graph; one sweep gives you how every quantity responds to that single input. Reverse mode pulls a derivative of one output backward; one sweep gives you how that output responds to every input.

A neural network has one output that matters — the scalar loss — and $P$ inputs that matter — the $P$ parameters (page 01's asymmetry). Reverse mode computes all $P$ gradients in a single backward pass; forward mode would need $P$ separate passes, one per parameter. At $P = 8.19\times10^{9}$ that difference is the difference between "trainable" and "never." That asymmetry is why backprop exists. The reverse pass itself — over TN-1's nine parameters — is page 14.
