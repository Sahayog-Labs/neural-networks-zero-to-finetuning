Learning-Rate Schedules and Why Warmup Exists

On page 17 you computed that Adam's second-moment estimate $\hat v$ is off by a factor of $1000$ at the very first step. That single fact is the entire mechanistic reason a training run doesn't just turn $\eta$ on at full strength — and it tells you exactly how long to wait.

🧵 Thread — reopened [THREAD:MEM] Page 18 gave Adam's $m,v$ a real memory cost: two extra copies of every trainable parameter. The schedule you build here costs nothing — $\eta_k$ is a pure function of the step counter $k$, not a learned or stored quantity. It's free to compute and free to keep; the only thing it "remembers" is which step you're on.

Big steps to the neighbourhood, small steps to find the house

A learning-rate schedule is annealing, in the metallurgical sense the word borrows from: start hot, so the optimizer can move fast and cover ground, then cool it, so the last stretch is a controlled search rather than an overshoot. Two separate jobs, two separate phases. Warmup is a third phase bolted onto the front for a reason that has nothing to do with "finding the neighbourhood" — it exists to protect Adam's own bookkeeping while it fills up.

The standard 2026 schedule: linear warmup, cosine decay

📐 Warmup-cosine, indexed by step $k$ $$\eta_k = \begin{cases} \eta_{\max}\cdot\dfrac{k}{T_{\text{warm}}} & k \le T_{\text{warm}} \\[6pt] \eta_{\min} + \dfrac{1}{2}(\eta_{\max}-\eta_{\min})\Big(1+\cos\Big(\pi\,\dfrac{k-T_{\text{warm}}}{T_{\text{total}}-T_{\text{warm}}}\Big)\Big) & k > T_{\text{warm}} \end{cases}$$ Two phases, one formula. Linear ramp from $0$ to $\eta_{\max}$ over the first $T_{\text{warm}}$ steps; then a half-cosine glide from $\eta_{\max}$ down to $\eta_{\min}$ over the rest of the run. Typical values (constants.md §9.4, [VP]): $T_{\text{warm}} = $ 1–10% of total steps (or a flat $2000$), $\eta_{\min} = \eta_{\max}/10$.

Why warmup exists — three reasons, in order of how much they matter

Reason 1 — the one that matters most. Adam's second moment $\hat v_k$ is a running average of squared gradients. At $k=1$ it has seen exactly one gradient, so its variance is enormous even after bias correction fixes its mean (RAdam's whole thesis). Dividing by a wildly noisy $\sqrt{\hat v_k}$ turns an ordinary gradient into an update of unpredictable size — sometimes far too big. This resolves the predict above: the learning rate should ramp up from ~0, so that while $\hat v_k$ is still unreliable, no single step is large enough to do damage. By the time $k \approx T_{\text{warm}}$, $\hat v_k$ has had enough gradients to become a trustworthy estimate, and $\eta$ has reached its full value to match.

Reason 2 — the most memorable, because you computed it yourself. A single bad early gradient (a rare-token spike, a bad init draw) enters $v_k$ and, at the classic $\beta_2=0.999$, takes $1/(1-0.999)=\mathbf{1000}$ steps (constants.md §9.3, [DER] — the exact number from page 17) to decay back out. One unlucky step can poison the optimizer's memory for the next thousand. Ramping $\eta$ up slowly gives that one early gradient less leverage before it ever enters $v$.

Reason 3. LayerNorm combined with a large batch produces its own early instability — the loss landscape right at initialization is unusually steep before the norm layers' statistics settle. Pre-norm placement (page 20) reduces this but does not remove it; warmup is still cheap insurance.

Decay to $\eta_{\max}/10$, not to zero

The cosine half-cycle above never reaches $0$ by design — it bottoms out at $\eta_{\min}=\eta_{\max}/10$ (sometimes $/20$). A true decay-to-zero schedule means zero learning for the entire tail of the run: whatever fraction of the data is scheduled last gets no update at all. Leaving a floor keeps the optimizer doing real, if gentle, work all the way to the final step.

🔑 The honest state of 2026 Warmup is a patch, not a law of nature. RAdam rescales the update analytically to remove the need for it; T-Fixup and GradInit choose initializations specifically so early training is already stable; some recipes skip warmup entirely with those techniques. None of that is why you'll still see warmup_steps in every training config you open. Plain linear warmup costs two extra hyperparameters and zero extra compute, it composes with anything, and it has worked for years across every architecture people have thrown at it. The field kept the cheap patch instead of adopting the more elegant fix — a genuinely useful thing to know about how ML practice actually works, as opposed to how a textbook might wish it worked.

Five ways to misread this

⚠️ "Warmup is because the model is fragile at the start." Too vague to be useful, and it points at the wrong culprit. The precise mechanism is Adam's second-moment estimate, not the model's weights — a plain SGD run needs warmup far less, because SGD has no $\hat v$ to be unreliable about.
⚠️ "Warmup and warm restarts are the same thing." Unrelated, despite the shared word. Warmup is a one-time ramp at the very start of training. Warm restarts (SGDR) periodically re-raise $\eta$ back up during training, on a repeating cycle. Different problems, different mechanisms.
⚠️ "Schedule by epoch." By step $k$, always. Many LLM fine-tunes never even complete one epoch (page 16), so an epoch-indexed schedule can end up doing nothing at all, or firing at the wrong point in training — a real, common bug.
⚠️ "Decay all the way to zero." Decay to $\eta_{\max}/10$–$/20$. Zero means zero learning for the whole tail of the schedule, which is a worse outcome than a small but nonzero step.
⚠️ "Adam is adaptive, so it doesn't need a schedule." Adam's adaptivity rescales the direction per parameter ($\hat m/\sqrt{\hat v}$, page 17); it never touches the scalar $\eta$ out front. That $\eta$ still needs exactly the shape this page derives.

Schedule shaper Live
Try it code/21_schedule.py implements this exact warmup-cosine schedule as a torch.optim.lr_scheduler.LambdaLR, plots $\eta_k$, and plots it against the step index specifically to expose the classic "scheduled by epoch, not step" off-by-one — the bug page 24's instrumentation panel checks for on your own fine-tuning run.
Python
import torch

def warmup_cosine(k, T_warm, T_total, eta_min_ratio=0.1):
    # k is the OPTIMIZER STEP, not the epoch — the bug this function exists to prevent.
    if k <= T_warm:
        return k / max(T_warm, 1)
    progress = (k - T_warm) / max(T_total - T_warm, 1)
    cosine = 0.5 * (1 + torch.cos(torch.tensor(torch.pi * progress)))
    return eta_min_ratio + (1 - eta_min_ratio) * cosine.item()

model = torch.nn.Sequential(torch.nn.Linear(2, 8), torch.nn.Tanh(), torch.nn.Linear(8, 1))
opt = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=2e-4, betas=(0.9, 0.95))
scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.LambdaLR(
    opt, lr_lambda=lambda k: warmup_cosine(k, T_warm=2000, T_total=100000))

x = torch.randn(32, 2); y = torch.randn(32, 1)
for k in range(100000):    # k, not epoch — call scheduler.step() every OPTIMIZER step
    loss = torch.nn.functional.mse_loss(model(x), y)
    loss.backward()
    opt.step(); scheduler.step(); opt.zero_grad()
🔬 Deep dive: why $T_{\text{warm}}$ is usually 1–10% of $T_{\text{total}}$, not a fixed count

The 1000-step figure from page 17 is about $\beta_2$'s memory, not about how long training is. A run of $5{,}000$ total steps and a run of $500{,}000$ total steps both need $\hat v$ to fill before trusting $\eta_{\max}$ — but spending $1000$ steps on warmup is $20\%$ of the short run and $0.2\%$ of the long one. Practice compromises: use a percentage (so warmup scales with the run) with a floor around the $1000$-ish mark the Adam memory sets, and a common flat default of $2000$ steps when in doubt (constants.md §9.4).
